## Slide 5: Influenza Virus Characteristics
**Family Orthomyxoviridae - distinctive features**

**Type A (most virulent):**
- Hemagglutinin (HA): 18 subtypes (H1-H18)
- Neuraminidase (NA): 11 subtypes (N1-N11)
- Pandemic potential through antigenic shift
- Examples: H1N1 (2009 pandemic), seasonal H3N2

**Type B (seasonal):**
- No subtypes, two lineages: Victoria & Yamagata
- Seasonal epidemics, no pandemics
- Less severe than type A
- Antigenic drift requires annual vaccine updates

*Virus structure and surface antigen illustrations*

## Slide 6: Antigenic Evolution - Annual Vaccine Challenge
**Why influenza vaccines need yearly reformulation**

**Antigenic Drift:**
- Point mutations in HA/NA genes
- Gradual immune escape from prior vaccines
- Predictable seasonal circulation patterns
- Vaccine mismatch risk (20-50% annually)

**Antigenic Shift:**
- Reassortment between human/animal viruses
- Sudden emergence of novel subtypes
- Pandemic potential (H5N1, H7N9 surveillance)
- Rapid vaccine development required

*Evolution timeline showing drift vs shift*

## Slide 11: Pediatric Vaccination Special Considerations
**Children 6 months to 8 years - unique requirements**

**First Vaccination Ever:**
- **Two doses required:** 4 weeks apart for optimal response
- **Immune naive individuals:** Primary series needed
- **Subsequent years:** Annual single dose
- **LAIV option:** Ages 2-8 years (better immunogenicity)

**Catch-up Situations:**
- **6-35 months:** 0.25 mL dose (standard pediatric formulation)
- **36+ months:** Adult dose (0.5 mL)
- **Historical barriers:** Needle fear, parental concerns
- **School-based programs:** High coverage achievement potential

## Slide 12: Pregnancy & Maternal Immunization Benefits

**Universal Recommendation: All trimesters safe and effective**

**Maternal Benefits:**
- Severe influenza prevention during pregnancy
- Reduced hospitalization and complications
- Postpartum immunity maintenance

**Fetal/Infant Benefits:**
- Placental antibody transfer until 6 months
- Neonatal protection against severe illness
- Reduced infant hospitalization (31-63%)
- Extended protection through breastfeeding

**Safety Profile:**
- Extensive safety data from millions of doses
- No increased adverse pregnancy outcomes
- Category B classification (FDA pregnancy categories)

---

## Slide 13: Immunocompromised Patient Considerations

**HIV-Infected Individuals:**
- Safe regardless of CD4 count (>200 recommended)
- Better response with CD4 >500 cells/μL
- Combination with pneumococcal vaccination advised
- Annual revaccination recommended

**Cancer Patients:**
- Timing with chemotherapy cycles crucial
- Safe during remission or stable disease
- May need higher antibody titers post-vaccination
- Often requires annual vaccination

**Organ Transplant Recipients:**
- Post-transplant protection (3-6 months recommended)
- May require higher antigen doses
- Reduced response in immunosuppressive states
- Live vaccines (LAIV) generally contraindicated

## Slide 14: Adverse Events and Safety Profile

**Common Local Reactions (10-30%):**
- **Injection site pain, redness, swelling**
- **Generally mild, resolve within 24-48 hours**
- **No specific treatment usually required**
- **Higher incidence with high-dose formulations**

**Systemic Reactions (5-15%):**
- **Low-grade fever, fatigue, myalgia**
- **Headache, arthralgia, chills**
- **Generally self-limiting within 3-7 days**
- **More common in younger adults than elderly**

**Rare Serious Events:**
- **Guillain-Barré syndrome:** Not causally linked (<1:1 million doses)**
- **Anaphylaxis:** Extremely rare (1:1 million)**
- **New onset thrombocytopenia:** Idiopathic, coincidental**
- **Oculorespiratory syndrome:** Rare, self-limiting**

## Slide 15: LAIV (Live Attenuated) Special Considerations

**Advantages over inactivated vaccines:**
- **Better mucosal immunity** (intranasal administration)
- **Longer duration of protection**
- **Cross-protection** against drifted viruses
- **Easier administration** (needle-free)

**Disadvantages & Contraindications:**
- **Not recommended in pregnancy**
- **Contraindicated in immunocompromised**
- **Avoid in children with asthma** (risk of wheezing)
- **Garlic/onion allergy** (stabilizer component)
- **Recent influenza antiviral use** (reduces efficacy)

**Effectiveness Data:**
- 50-58% against antigenically matched viruses
- Better for children 2-17 years
- Lower effectiveness in adults >60 years

## Slide 17: Pandemic Vaccine Development Strategies

**Traditional Egg-Based Production:**
- **6-9 months development timeline**
- **Stockpiling of egg-adapted strains**
- **Global distribution challenges**
- **Limitations in low/middle-income countries**

**Next-Generation Platforms:**

**Cell-Based Vaccines:**
- **Faster production cycles** (weeks instead of months)
- **No egg allergies risk**
- **Higher fidelity to circulating viruses**
- **Scalable manufacturing capacity**

**RNA/DNA Vaccines:**
- **24-48 hour development** possible
- **Highly adaptable** to any genetic sequence
- **Stored in freeze-dried form** (long shelf-life)
- **Pandemic-ready stockpiles** available

## Slide 19: Vaccine Hesitancy in Influenza Context

**Influenza-Specific Concerns:**

**"It's just the flu - not serious"**
- *Response:* Seasonal influenza causes 290,000-650,000 deaths annually

**"Vaccine causes the flu"**
- *Response:* Inactivated vaccines cannot cause infection

**"I never get the flu"**
- *Response:* 5-20% of adults get symptomatic influenza annually

**"Too many side effects"**
- *Response:* Local reactions mild and transient (<2% serious)

**"Not worth it for pregnant women"**
- *Response:* Prevents maternal-fetal complications, safe in all trimesters
