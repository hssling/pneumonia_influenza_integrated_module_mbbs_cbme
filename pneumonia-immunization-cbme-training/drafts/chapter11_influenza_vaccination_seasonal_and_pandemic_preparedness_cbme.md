## 11.1 INFLUENZA EPIDEMIOLOGY AND IMPACT

### Seasonal Influenza Burden
- **Annual global deaths:** 290,000-650,000
- **Annual cases:** Up to 1 billion worldwide
- **Hospitalizations:** 3-5 million severe cases yearly
- **Economic impact:** $87 billion US healthcare costs annually

### Secondary Pneumonia Complications
- **Primary cause of pneumonia:** Influenza viruses (types A and B)
- **Bacterial superinfection:** Streptococcus pneumoniae, Staphylococcus aureus
- **Death rate in pneumonia:** Up to 50% in immunocompromised
- **Age-specific mortality:** Children <2 and adults >65 years most affected

### Indian Context
- **Seasonal pattern:** Year-round circulation, peaks June-August
- **Southern region:** Year-round transmission
- **Northern states:** Distinct winter epidemics
- **Strain circulation:** H1N1, H3N2, influenza B

---

## 11.2 INFLUENZA VIRUS CHARACTERISTICS

### Virologic Types
- **Influenza A:** Most virulent, pandemics potential (1918, 2009)
- **Influenza B:** Seasonal epidemics, no pandemics
- **Surface antigens:** 
  - Hemagglutinin (HA): 18 subtypes (H1-H18)
  - Neuraminidase (NA): 11 subtypes (N1-N11)

### Antigenic Drift vs Shift
- **Antigenic drift:** Point mutations, yearly epidemics
- **Antigenic shift:** Reassortment, pandemics (H1N1 2009)
- **Vaccine adaptation:** Annual strain updates required

### Transmission Dynamics
- **Person-to-person:** Respiratory droplets, fomites
- **R0 value:** 1.3-1.8 (sustained transmission)
- **Incubation period:** 1-4 days (median 2 days)
- **Infectious period:** 1 day before symptoms to 5-7 days after

## 11.3 INFLUENZA VACCINE TYPES

### Trivalent Influenza Vaccines (TIV)
**Standard dose trivalent inactivated:**
- **Composition:** 2 influenza A strains + 1 influenza B strain
- **Route:** Intramuscular (deltoid preferred in adults)
- **Dosing:** 0.5 mL adults, 0.25 mL children 6-35 months
- **Storage:** 2-8°C, requires cold chain

### Quadrivalent Influenza Vaccines (QIV)
**Four-strain protection:**
- **Additional B strain:** Covers both B lineages (Victoria, Yamagata)
- **Superior coverage:** 99% of circulating strains
- **Pediatric formulations:** Special versions for children
- **Adult formulations:** Standard and high-dose versions

### High-Dose Trivalent Vaccine (HD-TIV)
**Fluzone High-Dose:**
- **Antigen content:** 4x higher than standard dose
- **Target age:** ≥65 years (impaired immune response)
- **Effectiveness:** 25% better protection against hospitalization
- **Side effects:** Higher injection site reactions

### Live Attenuated Influenza Vaccine (LAIV)
**FluMist nasal spray:**
- **Delivered as:** Intra-nasal spray, replication-competent virus
- **Age range:** 2-49 years preferred
- **Advantages:** Better mucosal immunity, easier administration
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy, immunocompromise, egg allergy

### Novel Platforms
**RNA-based vaccines:** Moderna, Pfizer (pandemic preparedness)
**Recombinant HA vaccines:** Flublok (egg-free manufacturing)
**Universal vaccines:** Targeting conserved regions

## 11.4 SEASONAL VACCINATION RECOMMENDATIONS

### Priority Risk Groups

**Priority Group 1: All Persons ≥6 Months**
- **Universal recommendation:** Annual vaccination advised
- **Public health priority:** 70-90% effectiveness when matched
- **Community protection:** Herd immunity benefits

**Priority Group 2: High-Risk Medical Conditions**
- **Chronic pulmonary diseases:** Asthma, COPD, cystic fibrosis
- **Cardiovascular diseases:** Heart failure, coronary disease
- **Metabolic disorders:** Diabetes mellitus
- **Renal/neurologic conditions:** ESRD, neuromuscular diseases
- **Immunosuppression:** Cancer, HIV, transplant recipients

**Priority Group 3: Special Populations**
- **Pregnant individuals:** All trimesters, post-partum
- **Children:** Ages 6-59 months high priority
- **Elderly:** Age ≥65 years (high-dose preferred)
- **Healthcare workers:** Patient protection
- **Classroom contacts:** Teachers, childcare workers

### Age-Specific Schedules

#### Infants and Children (6-35 months)
- **Dose:** 0.25 mL (full dose when net weight >36 lbs)
- **First vaccination:** Two doses 4 weeks apart
- **Subsequent years:** Annual single dose
- **Vaccine type:** Age-appropriate inactivated vaccine

#### Children and Adolescents (3-18 years)
- **Annual dose:** 0.5 mL intramuscular
- **Route preference:** Deltoid or anterolateral thigh
- **LAIV option:** Ages 2-8 years acceptable
- **Special needles:** Smaller gauge for comfort

#### Adults (19-64 years)
- **Standard dose:** 0.5 mL intramuscular
- **High-dose option:** For adults 50-64 years
- **Pregnancy:** Safe in all trimesters
- **Timing:** Anytime during influenza season

#### Elderly (≥65 years)
- **High-dose TIV:** Preferred for better immunogenicity
- **QIV standard:** Alternative high-dose option unavailable
- **Annual revaccination:** Required each season
- **Timing:** September-October pre-season

## 11.5 PEDIATRIC AND ADOLESCENT CONSIDERATIONS

### Vaccine Safety in Children
- **Extensive trials:** Millions of doses safely administered
- **No autism link:** Multiple meta-analyses confirm safety
- **Febrile seizures:** Rare, self-limited episodes
- **Guillain-Barré syndrome:** Not causally associated

### Special Pediatric Populations
- **Asthmatics:** LAIV contraindicated, use inactivated vaccine
- **Egg allergy:** Inactivated vaccines safe (trace amounts)
- **Immunodeficiency:** Avoid LAIV, use inactivated vaccines
- **Neurologic conditions:** Careful monitoring, inactivated preferred

### School-Based Programs
- **High vaccination rates:** 60-80% coverage achievable
- **Cost-effective:** Low incremental cost per vaccination
- **Sustained protection:** Before winter epidemic peaks
- **Community impact:** Reduces circulation in school settings

## 11.6 PANDEMIC PREPAREDNESS

### Pandemic Influenza Characteristics
- **Rapid spread:** R0=2.0-3.0 (highly contagious)
- **Novel strains:** Limited population immunity
- **High virulence:** 1918 H1N1 killed 50 million
- **Healthcare strain:** Overwhelms hospital systems

### Rapid Vaccine Production
**Global response strategies:**
- **Seed strains:** Shared within 2-3 weeks of identification
- **Vaccine manufacturing:** 3-6 months for standard production
- **Advanced platforms:** RNA vaccines (mRNA-1010) ready in weeks
- **Stockpiling:** Strategic reserves of monovalent vaccines

### Contingency Planning
**Hospital response:**
- **Prioritization:** Healthcare workers, critical patients first
- **Staff vaccination:** Mandatory for sustained operations
- **Personal protective equipment (PPE):** Conserve supplies
- **Triage systems:** Ethics committees for resource allocation

### Public Health Measures
**Multi-layered approach:**
- **Non-pharmacological:** Social distancing, mask wearing
- **Contact tracing:** Traditional and digital methods
- **Isolation/quarantine:** Symptomatic individuals
- **Surveillance systems:** Early warning networks

## 11.7 VACCINE ADMINISTRATION AND STORAGE

### Injection Techniques
- **Site selection:** Adult deltoid muscle
- **Needle size:** 23-25 gauge, 1-1.5 inch length
- **Aspiration:** Not recommended for IM injection
- **Multiple vaccines:** 1 inch separation if co-administered

### Contraindications and Precautions
- **Severe allergy:** Previous anaphylaxis to vaccine components
- **Guillain-Barré syndrome:** Within 6 weeks of prior vaccination
- **Moderate illness:** Defer until recovery
- **Pregnancy caution:** LAIV not recommended

### Adverse Event Management
- **Common reactions:** Soreness, redness at injection site
- **Systemic symptoms:** Low-grade fever, fatigue, myalgia
- **Rare events:** Anaphylaxis (<1:1 million doses)
- **Reporting mechanism:** VAERS or similar systems

### Vaccine Storage Considerations
- **Temperature maintenance:** 2-8°C continuous monitoring
- **Cold chain equipment:** Refrigerators with alarm systems
- **Handling:** Thawing not applicable (killed vaccines)
- **Expiry monitoring:** Use oldest stock first

## 11.8 EFFECTIVENESS AND IMPACT MEASUREMENT

### Vaccine Effectiveness Studies
**Test-negative design:** Cases vs controls seeking care for influenza-like illness
- **Healthy adults:** 40-60% effectiveness against symptomatic illness
- **Elderly persons:** 50-70% protection against severe outcomes
- **Children/adolescents:** 60-75% effectiveness demonstrated

### Real-World Impact
**Hospitalization reduction:** 45-70% based on age and comorbidities
**Mortality prevention:** 70% reduction in vaccinated elderly populations
**Workplace productivity:** Fewer sick days, reduced economic impact
**Community protection:** 20-30% reduction in unvaccinated populations

### Surveillance Systems
**Weekly reporting:** US FluView, WHO FluNet
**Strain identification:** PCR-based characterization
**Antiviral resistance:** Amantadine, oseltamivir resistance monitoring
**Adverse events:** VAERS and VSD (Vaccine Safety Datalink)

## 11.9 ANNUAL VACCINE FORMULATION PROCESS

### Strain Selection Committee
- **World Health Organization (WHO):** Global recommendations
- **Food and Drug Administration (FDA):** US-specific decisions
- **Timing:** February-March each year
- **Data sources:** Southern hemisphere influenza patterns (predictive of Northern)

### Manufacturing Challenges
**Lead time requirements:**
- **Strain isolation:** 2-3 weeks
- **Seed development:** 4-6 weeks
- **Production:** 16-26 weeks
- **Regulatory approval:** Final weeks
- **Distribution:** September-November

### Pandemic Response Framework
**Accelerated development:**
- **Candidate vaccine viruses:** Stockpile of 150 strains
- **Mock-up seeds:** Ready for antigen switching
- **Clinical trials:** Phase 1-3 protocols in place
- **Emergency use authorization:** FDA pathways developed
