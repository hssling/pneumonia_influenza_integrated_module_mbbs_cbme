## 8.1 OXYGEN THERAPY AND RESPIRATORY SUPPORT

### Oxygen Therapy Principles
**Goal:** Maintain adequate tissue oxygenation without toxicity

**Delivery Methods:**
- **Nasal cannula:** 1-6 L/min (FiO2 24-44%)
- **Simple face mask:** 5-10 L/min (FiO2 35-55%)
- **Venturi mask:** Precise FiO2 delivery (24-60%)
- **Non-rebreather mask:** High concentration (up to 95%)

### Oxygen Targets
- **General pneumonia:** SpO2 ≥92-96%
- **COPD with CO2 retention:** SpO2 ≥88-92%
- **Pregnancy:** SpO2 ≥95%
- **Monitor:** Pulse oximetry, ABG if critically ill

### Oxygen Weaning Protocol
- **Reduce FiO2 by 10-20% every 2-4 hours**
- **Replace with nasal cannula** when possible
- **Discontinue** when SpO2 ≥92% on room air
- **Monitor for 24 hours** after discontinuation

## 8.2 FLUID MANAGEMENT

### Hydration Status Assessment
**Signs of Dehydration:**
- **Tachycardia, hypotension, tachycardia**
- **Dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor**
- **Concentrated urine, decreased urine output**
- **Elevated hematocrit, creatinine**

**Signs of Fluid Overload:**
- **Peripheral edema, pulmonary rales**
- **Increased JVP, pleural effusion**
- **Weight gain (>2-3 kg/day)**
- **Hyponatremia, dilutional anemia**

### Fluid Therapy Guidelines
**CAP without shock:** Oral fluids preferred, IV only if unable to drink
**Severe CAP/Sepsis:** 30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus within 3 hours
**Maintenance fluids:** 25-30 mL/kg/day for adults
**Diuretic phase:** If signs of overload, consider loop diuretics

### Special Considerations
**Heart failure:** Careful fluid management, monitor BNP
**Renal failure:** Renal-dose dopamine, fluid restriction
**Cirrhosis:** Albumin infusion for hyponatremia
**Diabetes insipidus:** Free water replacement

## 8.3 CHEST PHYSIOTHERAPY AND BRONCHIAL HYGIENE

### Postural Drainage Positions
**Right Upper Lobe:** Left side up, head down (Trendelenburg)
**Right Middle Lobe:** Right side up, prone position
**Right Lower Lobe:** Right side up, head down
**Left Lung:** Opposite positions for symmetry

### Manual Chest Physiotherapy Techniques
**Percussion/Clapping:** Loose secretions mobilization
**Vibrations:** While patient exhales, reduces airflow resistance
**Chest Shaking:** Loosens adherent secretions
**Deep Breathing Exercises:** Prevents atelectasis
**Coughing:** Forced expiratory technique (FET)

### Airway Clearance Devices
**Incentive Spirometry:** Prevents atelectasis, improves lung volume
**Flutter Valve:** Oscillatory positive expiratory pressure (PEP)
**High-Frequency Chest Wall Oscillation:** For difficult secretions
**Mechanical Percussion:** Devices like The Vest

### Contraindications
**Recent surgery:** Chest physiotherapy avoided
**Unstable patient:** Risk of hypoxia or arrhythmia
**Bleeding disorders:** Risk of hemoptysis
**Rib fractures:** Pain prevents effective technique

## 8.4 CORTICOSTEROID THERAPY

### Evidence for Adjunct Corticosteroids
**CAP with severe sepsis/septic shock:** Reduces mortality by 20%
**Influenza-associated pneumonia:** May reduce complications
**Caution:** Increased mortality in less severe cases

### Recommended Dosing
**Dexamethasone:** 0.15 mg/kg IV every 6 hours for 5 days
**Prednisolone:** 40 mg/day PO for 7 days (then taper)
**Hydrocortisone:** 50 mg IV every 6 hours for 7 days + fludrocortisone
**Duration:** 5-10 days maximum, abrupt stop after longer courses

### Indications
**Severe CAP:** CURB-65 ≥3, mechanical ventilation
**Septic shock:** Vasopressor-dependent hypotension
**Refractory hypoxemia:** Despite maximal support
**Acute exacerbations:** With ABG evidence of respiratory acidosis

### Contraindications
**Uncontrolled infection:** Gastropathy, Glaucoma, Diabetes mellitus
**Avoid routine use:** Normal-appearing chest X-ray, outpatient management
**Monitor carefully:** For hyperglycemia, secondary infections, adrenal suppression

## 8.5 ANTIPYRETIC AND ANALGESIC THERAPY

### Fever Management
**Acetaminophen/Paracetamol:** First-line (500-1000mg PO/IV every 6 hours)
**Avoid:** Asterisk (may mask fever of worsening infection)
**Aspirin:** Only in Kawasaki disease/specific indications
**Physical methods:** Sponge bath, fan for comfort

### Pain Control
**Pleural Chest Pain:** NSAIDs preferred if no contraindications
**Ibuprofen:** 400mg PO every 6-8 hours (anti-inflammatory effects)
**Tramadol:** For severe pain (controlled substance)
**Codeine:** Combination with acetaminophen (avoid in elderly)

### Hydration Support
**Fever increases insensible losses:** 500 mL/day additional fluids
**Oral rehydration:** Preferred over IV in stable patients
**IV hydration:** For nausea, severe dehydration

## 8.6 NUTRITIONAL SUPPORT

### Importance in Pneumonia Recovery
**Protein-calorie malnutrition worsens outcomes**
**Immune system requires adequate nutrients**
**Muscle wasting during prolonged illness**
**Delayed recovery with malnutrition**

### Nutritional Goals
**Energy: 25-35 kcal/kg/day**
**Protein: 1.2-2.2 g/kg/day**
**Early enteral feeding preferred**
**Avoid refeeding syndrome in malnourished**

### Feeding Strategies
**Oral diet:** As tolerated, high-calorie supplements
**Nasogastric feeding:** If unable to eat adequately
**Parental nutrition:** Last resort, portal vein thrombosis risk
**Probiotics:** May reduce VAP risk in ICU patients

### Micronutrient Supplementation
**Vitamin D:** 2000 IU daily (deficiency impairs immunity)
**Zinc:** 15-30 mg daily (immune function)
**Vitamin C:** 500-1000 mg daily (antioxidant effects)
**Selenium:** 100-200 mcg daily (antioxidant)

## 8.7 THROMBOPROPHYLAXIS

### Indications for All Pneumonia Patients
**Hospitalized patients:** Any length of stay
**Immobilized patients:** Even outpatient management
**High-risk comorbidities:** Cancer, prior DVT/PE, thrombophilia

### Low Molecular Weight Heparin (Preferred)
**Enoxaparin:** 40 mg SC daily (for prophylaxis)
**Adjust for renal function:** CKD III-IV use 50% dose
**Dalteparin:** 5000 units SC daily
**Tinzaparin:** 4500 units SC daily

### Alternative Agents
**Unfractionated Heparin:** 5000 units SC three times daily
**Fondaparinux:** 2.5 mg SC daily (renal adjustment)
**Direct oral anticoagulants:** Not for prophylaxis in pneumonia

### Bleeding Risk Assessment
**High risk:** Recent surgery, active blee díng, severe thrombocytopenia
**Intermediate risk:** PUD, severe HTN, anticoagulant use
**Low risk:** Most pneumonia patients acceptable for prophylaxis

**Monitoring:** Platelet count if suspect HIT, signs of bleeding
**Duration:** Until mobility restored or discharge, extended prophylaxis if indicated

## 8.8 GLYCEMIC CONTROL

### Diabetes Management in Pneumonia
**Hyperglycemia worsens outcomes:** Infection, steroids, catecholamines
**Target glucose:** 140-180 mg/dL (tight control not beneficial)
**Avoid hypoglycemia:** <70 mg/dL increases mortality
**Capillary blood glucose:** Less painful than venous sampling

### Insulin Therapy
**Correction with rapid-acting:** Novorapid or Humalog
**Basal-bolus regimen:** For long-term glucose control
**Subcutaneous sliding scale:** Adjusted for carbohydrate intake
**Continuous infusion:** In ICU patients with hyperglycemia

### Enteral Feeding Impact
**Dietary carbohydrates:** Predictable insulin requirements
**Bolus feeding:** Lower hypoglycemia risk than continuous
**Tube feeds:** High osmolarity may cause diarrhea
**Monitor:** Blood glucose before meals and at bedtime

## 8.9 PSYCHOSOCIAL SUPPORT

### Psychological Impact of Pneumonia
**Anxiety and depression:** Common with severe illness
**Fear of recurrence:** Especially in elderly patients
**Sleep disruption:** Due to hospitalization and noise
**Cognitive impairment:** May persist after discharge

### Support Interventions
**Family involvement:** Daily updates, reassurance
**Patient education:** Understanding illness and recovery
**Breathing exercises:** Reduces anxiety, improves oxygenation
**Relaxation techniques:** Music therapy, guided imagery

### Social Services
**Discharge planning:** Home care assessment
**Community resources:** Meals-on-wheels, transportation
**Rehabilitation referral:** Physical therapy, occupational therapy
**Follow-up care:** Primary care provider coordination

### Spiritual Care
**Respect beliefs:** Cultural and religious considerations
**Pastor/clergy visits:** If requested by patient/family
**Existential support:** Addressing meaning and mortality concerns
