## 5.1 Cardiac Structure and Chambers

### Heart as a Four-Chambered Pump

**Right Heart (Pulmonary Circuit)**:
- **Right Atrium**: Receives deoxygenated blood from venae cavae
- **Right Ventricle**: Pumps blood to pulmonary circulation through pulmonary trunk
- **Trabeculae carneae**: Muscular ridges preventing vortex formation during systole
- **Moderator band**: Conduction bundle preventing asynchronous contraction

**Left Heart (Systemic Circuit)**:
- **Left Atrium**: Receives oxygenated blood from pulmonary veins
- **Left Ventricle**: Most muscular chamber, pumps to entire systemic circulation
- **Papillary muscles**: Prevent atrioventricular valve prolapse during contraction
- **Chordae tendineae**: Fibrous cords connecting valve leaflets to papillary muscles

### Cardiac Skeleton
- **Fibrous rings**: Support valve attachments and prevent annular dilatation
- **Interventricular septum**: Muscular wall separating right and left ventricles
- **Atrioventricular bundle (Bundle of His)**: Enters septum through membranous portion

## 5.2 Cardiac Valves and Circulation

### Atrioventricular Valves

**Mitral (Bicuspid) Valve**:
- Two leaflets: anterior (larger) and posterior
- Located between left atrium and ventricle
- Most commonly involved in rheumatic heart disease
- **Clinical Correlation**: Mitral stenosis causes left atrial enlargement → atrial fibrillation

**Tricuspid Valve**:
- Three leaflets: anterior, posterior, septal
- Located between right atrium and ventricle
- Less susceptible to rheumatic involvement
- **Pathology**: Functional regurgitation in pulmonary hypertension

### Semilunar Valves

**Aortic Valve**:
- Three cusps: left, right, right-left (anterior)
- Located at aortic root
- Separates left ventricle from ascending aorta
- **Clinical Significance**: Bicuspid aortic valve increases endocarditis and stenosis risk

**Pulmonary Valve**:
- Three cusps: anterior, left, right
- Located at pulmonary trunk origin
- Separates right ventricle from pulmonary artery
- **Clinical Correlation**: Pulmonary stenosis often congenital (tetralogy of Fallot)

## 5.3 Coronary Circulation

### Coronary Artery Distribution

**Left Coronary Artery**:
- **Left Main Trunk**: Branches into LAD and LCx
- **Left Anterior Descending (LAD)**: Anterior interventricular artery, "widow maker"
  - Supplies anterior 2/3 of interventricular septum
  - Anterior wall of left ventricle
  - Left ventricular apex
- **Left Circumflex (LCx)**: Supplies lateral wall of left ventricle
  - Obtuse marginal branches
  - Posterior left ventricular branch (PLV)
  - Left atrial branch

**Right Coronary Artery**:
- Supplies right ventricle (entire free wall)
- Right atrium and parts of left atrium
- Posterior 1/3 of interventricular septum
- Sinoatrial node (55% of population)
- Atrioventricular node (90% of population)

### Cardiac Venous Drainage

**Coronary Sinus** (major collector):
- Great cardiac vein: Follows LAD in anterior interventricular groove
- Middle cardiac vein: Follows posterior interventricular artery
- Small cardiac vein: Follows right coronary artery
- Receives 75% of cardiac venous blood

**Anterior Cardiac Veins**: Drain directly into right atrium from right ventricle

## 5.4 Vascular System Architecture

### Arterial System Classification

**Elastic Arteries** (conducting vessels):
- Aorta, pulmonary trunk, common carotid, subclavian, common iliac arteries
- Large lumen-to-wall ratio, resilient compliance
- Accommodate pulsatile flow from heart

**Muscular Arteries** (distributing vessels):
- Most named arteries
- Thick tunica media with smooth muscle
- Vasoconstriction/dilation regulation

**Arterioles** (resistance vessels):
- Smallest arteries, minimal elastic tissue
- Primary site of peripheral resistance control
- Precapillary sphincter regulation

### Venous System Features

**Venous Valves**:
- Prevent retrograde blood flow
- Most numerous in lower extremities
- Competent valves essential for venous return

**Venous Capacitance**:
- Veins contain ~60% of total blood volume
- "Blood reservoir" function
- Varicose veins result from valve incompetence

### Capillary Bed Structure

**Continuous Capillaries**: Most common, endothelial continuity (CNS, muscle)
**Fenestrated Capillaries**: Pores for rapid exchange (kidneys, endocrine glands)
**Sinusoidal Capillaries**: Large fenestrae (liver, bone marrow)

## 5.5 Embryological Development of Cardiovascular System

### Primitive Heart Tube Formation

**Day 18-22**: Primitive heart tube forms from:
- **Angiogenic cell clusters** fuse in midline
- **Cranial**: Truncus arteriosus
- **Middle*** Primordial ventricles
- **Caudal**: Primitive atria

### Septation and Chamber Formation

**Atrial Septation**:
- Primary septum forms crescent-shaped flap
- Foramen primum: Initially large opening
- Foramen secundum: Backup opening
- Septum secundum: Muscular ridge
- Final atrial septum = septum primum + secundum

**Ventricular Septation**:
- Muscular septum grows from apex toward AV valves
- AV cushions fuse to form membranous septum
- Failure creates ventricular septal defect

**Outflow Tract Division**:
- Neural crest cells migrate to divide truncus arteriosus
- Aorticopulmonary septum forms spiral division
- **Tetralogy of Fallot**: Abnormal septation + RV outflow obstruction

## 5.6 Congenital Cardiac Malformations

### Cyanotic Heart Disease

**Tetralogy of Fallot** (most common cyanotic defect):
- **VSD**: Large defect in ventricular septum
- **Pulmonary Stenosis**: Narrow pulmonary valve/outflow tract
- **RVH**: Right ventricular hypertrophy due to pressure overload
- **Overriding Aorta**: Aortic root straddles VSD
- **Clinical**: Boot-shaped heart on X-ray, cyanotic spells

**Transposition of Great Arteries**:
- Aorta exits right ventricle, pulmonary trunk exits left ventricle
- Parallel rather than series circulation
- Requires PGE1 infusion and balloon septostomy for survival

### Acyanotic Heart Disease

**Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)**
- Secundum type: 75% of cases in fossa ovalis region
- Primum type: AV canal defect involving septum and valves
- Sinus venosus defect: Near superior vena cava
- **Clinical**: Wide split S2, pulmonary hypertension later

**Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)**
- Perimembranous: Adjacent to aortic valve (80%)
- Muscular: Within muscular septum
- Inlet/supracristal defects also occur
- **Natural History**: Most small VSDs close spontaneously

**Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)**
- Fetal shunt remains patent
- Left-to-right shunt causes pulmonary hypertension
- Characteristic "machinery murmur"
- **Treatment**: Indomethacin (premature) or surgical ligation

## CBME Skill Station: Cardiovascular Physical Examination

**Objective**: Perform systematic cardiovascular assessment integrating anatomy with clinical findings

**Equipment**:
- Stethoscope with bell and diaphragm
- Sphygmomanometer
- Goniometer for pulse assessment
- Penlight for capillary refill

**Procedure**:

### Inspection
1. **General appearance**: Cyanosis, clubbing, edema
2. **Precordial activity**: Visible apical impulse, abnormal pulsations
3. **Jugular veins**: Jugular venous pressure, venous pulsations

### Palpation
1. **Radial pulse**: Rate, rhythm, volume, character
2. **Carotid pulse**: Compare with radial for delays/pathology
3. **Apical impulse**: Location, size, intensity
4. **Heart sounds**: Precordial palpation of thrills

### Auscultation
1. **Aortic area** (2nd right intercostal space): Aortic valve sounds
2. **Pulmonic area** (2nd left intercostal space): Pulmonary valve sounds
3. **Tricuspid area** (left 4th intercostal space): Tricuspid valve
4. **Mitral area** (apex, 5th intercostal space): Mitral valve

**Key Cardiac Auscultation Points**:
- Sit patient at 30-45° for optimal hearing
- Use diaphragmatic surface for higher frequency sounds (S1, S2, murmurs)
- Use bell for lower frequency sounds (S3, S4)

### Blood Pressure Assessment
- **Korotkoff sounds**: Phase I (appearance) to Phase V (disappearance)
- Simultaneous palpation of radial pulse during cuff deflation
- **Pseudo-hypertension**: Non-compressible arteries in elderly

## Clinical Correlations and Applications

### Acute Coronary Syndromes

**ST-Elevation MI (STEMI)**:
- **Anterior**: LAD occlusion - anteroseptal wall
- **Inferior**: RCA occlusion - inferior wall ± RV
- **Lateral**: LCx occlusion - lateral wall
- **Posterior**: RCA occlusion - posterior wall

**Cardiac Biomarkers Correlation**:
- **Troponin I/T**: Myocardial specific, peaks at 12-24 hours
- **CK-MB**: Myocardial specific, peaks at 6-12 hours
- **Standardization**: hs-cTn preferred for early detection

### Valvular Heart Disease

**Aortic Stenosis**:
- Calcific.deg?enerative (senile) vs bicuspid valve disease
- LVH → diastolic dysfunction → heart failure
- Classic triad: Angina, dyspnea, syncope

**Mitral Regurgitation**:
- Chordal rupture, annular dilatation, or leaflet abnormalities
- Acute vs chronic forms
- Heart failure symptoms + pulmonary hypertension

### Arrhythmias and Conduction

**Atrial Fibrillation**:
- Rheumatic mitral disease → left atrial dilatation → AF
- Stroke risk from left atrial thrombus
- Rate vs rhythm control strategies

**AV Block**:
- First-degree: Prolonged PR interval
- Second-degree: Dropped beats
- Third-degree: Complete dissociation (ventricular escape rhythm)

## Advanced Clinical Integration

### Echocardiography Correlation

**2D Echocardiography Views**:
- **Parasternal long-axis**: LV size/function, valves
- **Parasternal short-axis**: LV cross-sections, RV size
- **Apical 4-chamber**: All chambers, AV valves
- **Subcostal**: For IVC assessment in tamponade

**Doppler Applications**:
- Color flow: Detects valvular regurgitation
- Pulse wave: Estimates pressures
- Continuous wave: Calculates gradients

### Cardiac Catheterization

**Coronary Angiography**:
- Judkins vs Amplatz catheter shapes
- Selective coronary engagement
- TIMI flow grading (0-3)

**Intervention**:
- Balloon angioplasty
- Stent deployment
- Thrombectomy devices

## Summary and CBME Integration

The cardiovascular system represents the most critical anatomical-clinical intersection in medicine. CBME-aligned learning transforms cardiac anatomy from abstract structures into:

**Clinical Decision-Making Tools**:
- Progressive evaluation from basic structure to complex pathophysiology
- Integration of anatomical knowledge with physical findings
- Patient-centered explanations of cardiac abnormalities

**Competency Development**:
- From identifying heart sounds to interpreting their clinical significance
- Progressive skill acquisition in examination and interpretation
- Professional development through patient interaction

**Lifelong Learning Foundation**:
- Understanding cardiac anatomy provides framework for all cardiovascular medicine
- Pattern recognition skills applicable to echocardiography and catheterization
- Research foundation for evidence-based cardiology

**CBME Key Insight**: "The heart isn't just a pump—it's a sophisticated organ where anatomical structure directly determines physiological function and clinical pathology."

## MCQs for Chapter 5

**Question 1**: Which coronary artery supplies the AV node in 90% of individuals?
A) Left anterior descending
B) Left circumflex
C) Right coronary artery
D) Posterior descending

**Answer: C** - RCA supplies AV node in most people, LAD/AV node artery in remaining 10%

**Question 2**: Embryologically, tetralogy of Fallot results from abnormal development of which cardiac structure?
A) Atrial septum
B) Ventricular septum
C) Aorticopulmonary septum
D) AV cushions

**Answer: C** - Aorticopulmonary septum maldevelopment creates the tetralogy components
