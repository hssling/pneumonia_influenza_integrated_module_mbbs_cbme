## Introduction to CBME Approach in Anatomy

This chapter introduces the fundamental concepts of human anatomy while establishing the competency-based medical education (CBME) framework. Unlike traditional anatomy textbooks that focus primarily on memorization, this CBME-aligned approach emphasizes:

- **Clinical Relevance**: Understanding how basic anatomical concepts apply to patient care
- **Progressive Competency Development**: Building skills from observation to independent practice
- **Integration**: Connecting anatomy with physiology, biochemistry, and clinical medicine
- **Assessment for Learning**: Using competency checklists rather than marks-based testing

## 1.1 Anatomical Terminology and Nomenclature

### Anatomical Position
The **anatomical position** is the standard reference position used worldwide in medicine:
- Standing erect
- Face forward
- Arms at sides with palms facing forward
- Feet parallel with toes pointing forward

**CBME Integration**: This standardized position ensures consistent communication in clinical settings.

### Planes of the Body
The body can be divided by different planes:

#### Sagittal Plane
- Divides body into right and left portions
- Mid-sagittal (median) plane: Equal division
- Para-sagittal planes: Unequal division

**Clinical Application**: Sagittal plane CT scans identify pathology affecting one side versus midline structures.

#### Coronal (Frontal) Plane
- Divides body into anterior and posterior portions
- Also called frontal plane

**Clinical Application**: Coronal plane MRI useful for evaluating spinal pathology and neurological conditions.

#### Horizontal (Transverse) Plane
- Divides body into superior and inferior portions
- Cross-sections at right angles to long axis

**Clinical Application**: Transverse CT slices essential for evaluating abdominal organs and vascular structures.

### Anatomical Directions

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Superior** | Towards head | Heart is superior to umbilicus |
| **Inferior** | Away from head | Nails are inferior to knuckles |
| **Anterior** (Ventral) | Towards front | Sternum is anterior to heart |
| **Posterior** (Dorsal) | Towards back | Vertebrae are posterior to heart |
| **Medial** | Towards midline | Ulnar artery is medial to radial artery |
| **Lateral** | Away from midline | Lungs are lateral to heart |
| **Proximal** | Closer to trunk | Elbow is proximal to wrist |
| **Distal** | Farther from trunk | Fingers are distal to wrist |
| **Superficial** | Near surface | Skin is superficial to muscle |
| **Deep** | Far from surface | Femur bone is deep to muscles |

## 1.2 Body Systems and Their Integration

### Major Body Systems

1. **Integumentary System**: Skin and appendages
2. **Skeletal System**: Bones, joints, and cartilage
3. **Muscular System**: Muscles and tendons
4. **Cardiovascular System**: Heart and blood vessels
5. **Lymphatic System**: Lymph vessels and immune organs
6. **Respiratory System**: Lungs and airways
7. **Nervous System**: Brain, spinal cord, and nerves
8. **Endocrine System**: Hormone-producing glands
9. **Digestive System**: Gastrointestinal tract and accessory organs
10. **Urinary System**: Kidneys and urinary tract
11. **Reproductive System**: Gonads and reproductive organs
12. **Special Senses**: Eyes, ears, and sensory organs

**CBME Perspective**: Understanding system interactions is crucial for holistic patient care.

## 1.3 Embryological Development: Foundation for Clinical Understanding

### Fertilization and Early Development
- **Zygote**: Single cell formed by sperm-egg fusion
- **Blastocyst**: Hollow ball of cells implanting in endometrium
- **Embryoblast**: Inner cell mass giving rise to embryo
- **Trophoblast**: Outer cells forming placenta

### Germ Layer Formation
Three primary germ layers form by day 16:
1. **Ectoderm**: Nervous system, skin epidermis
2. **Mesoderm**: Musculoskeletal system, cardiovascular system
3. **Endoderm**: Respiratory and digestive systems, glands

**Clinical Correlation**: Congenital anomalies often result from disruptions in germ layer development:
- Neural tube defects from ectoderm abnormalities
- Cardiac malformations from mesoderm defects
- Hernias from incomplete body wall closure

### CBME Clinical Case Study: Embryological Basis of Disease

**Case 1: Esophageal Atresia with Tracheo-esophageal Fistula**
- **Anatomical Basis**: Failure of proper separation between foregut derivatives
- **Embryological Defect**: Abnormal partitioning of primordial pharynx (Week 4-6)
- **Clinical Presentation**: Regurgitation of saliva, cyanosis with feeding
- **Diagnostic Imaging**: Barium swallow showing blind-ending esophagus
- **Surgical Management**: Primary esophageal anastomosis
- **CBME Learning Point**: Understanding embryology helps predict associated anomalies

**Competency Demonstration**: Student can explain to parents the embryological basis of their infant's condition.

## 1.4 Anatomical Variation and Clinical Significance

### Common Variations

1. **Branching Pattern of Arteries**: Renal artery variations affect angiography interpretation
2. **Bowel Rotation**: Incomplete rotation causes internal hernias
3. **Venous Drainage**: Variations in hepatic vein drainage affect liver transplant surgery

### Clinical Implications

**Case 2: Accessory Renal Artery**
- **Variation**: Additional renal artery originating from aorta
- **Surgical Risk**: Potential injury during aortic surgery
- **Clinical Detection**: Preoperative angiography essential
- **Management**: Arterial preservation or reimplantation

## 1.6 Surface Anatomy and Landmark Identification

### Key Surface Landmarks

| Landmark | Anatomical Relations | Clinical Use |
|----------|---------------------|--------------|
| **Suprasternal Notch** | Junction of sternum and manubrium | Insertion site for central venous catheters |
| **Xiphoid Process** | Lower end of sternum | Landmark for cardiopulmonary resuscitation |
| **Costal Margin** | Lower borders of ribcage | Palpation for splenomegaly |
| **Midclavicular Line** | From clavicle midpoint downward | Location of gallbladder point tenderness |

### CBME Skill Station: Surface Marking

**Objective**: Mark surface landmarks accurately on a partner
**Materials**: Body marking pen, anatomical model
**Procedure**:
1. Identify anatomical position
2. Locate midline and lateral reference points
3. Mark key bony landmarks
4. Draw lines representing planes
5. Verify accuracy with instructor

**Assessment Rubric**:
- **Level 3**: Performa all markings accurately
- **Level 2**: Performs with minor errors requiring correction
- **Level 1**: Requires significant guidance

## 1.7 CBME Milestones and Competency Assessment

### Entrustment Scales in Anatomy

**Level 1 - Observes**: Watches instructor perform anatomical demonstrations
**Level 2 - Assists**: Helps with specimen handling and basic identification
**Level 3 - Performs with Direct Supervision**: Conducts basic dissection and surface marking
**Level 4 - Performs Independently**: Executes anatomical examinations without supervision
**Level 5 - Supervises**: Teaches peers and junior students

### Competency Checklist

**Week 1-2: Foundation**

**Week 3-4: Application**

- [ ] Describes embryological basis of common anomalies
- [ ] Uses anatomical terminology in peer teaching

A) Divides the body into right and left portions
B) Divides the body into anterior and posterior portions

**Answer: C** - Competency: PC.1 (Demonstrates understanding of anatomical planes in clinical context)

A) Embryological rotation during gut development
B) Germ layer formation

D) Limb bud rotation

**Answer: A** - Competency: KS/KP.2 (Explains principles of embryological development)
