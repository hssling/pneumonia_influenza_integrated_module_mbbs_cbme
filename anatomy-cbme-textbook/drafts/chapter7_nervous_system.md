## CBME Clinical Case Study: Anterior Spinal Artery Syndrome

**Case 1: 62-year-old male with sudden leg weakness and anterior chest pain**
- **Clinical Presentation**: Bilateral lower extremity paralysis, loss of pain/temperature below lesion, preserved posterior column sensation
- **Anatomical Basis**: Anterior spinal artery occlusion at T10 level supplying anterior 2/3 of cord
- **CBME Integration Point**: Understanding spinal cord vascular anatomy enables rapid diagnosis and prognosis discussion
- **Competency Demonstration**: Student explains to family why motor function may recover while pain sensation may not

## 7.1 Central Nervous System Anatomy

### Brain Divisions

**Forebrain (Telencephalon + Diencephalon)**:
- **Cerebral Hemispheres**: Cortex (6 layers), white matter tracts, basal ganglia
- **Limbic System**: Hippocampus (memory), amygdala (emotion), fornix pathways
- **Thalamus**: Sensory "relay station" for all modalities except smell
- **Hypothalamus**: Masters gland of endocrinology, maintains homeostasis

**Midbrain (Mesencephalon)**:
- **Tectum**: Superior/inferior colliculi (visual/auditory reflexes)
- **Tegmentum**: Reticular formation, cranial nerve nuclei (CN III, IV)
- **Cerebral Aqueduct**: CSF pathway from third to fourth ventricle

**Hindbrain (Metencephalon + Myelencephalon)**:
- **Pons**: Respiratory center, CN V-VIII nuclei, corticospinal tract
- **Cerebellum**: Coordination, posture, motor learning (vestibulocerebellum, spinocerebellum, cerebrocerebellum)
- **Medulla Oblongata**: Vital centers (cardiorespiratory), CN IX-XII nuclei

### Ventricular System

**Four Brain Ventricles**:
- **Lateral Ventricles**: Within cerebral hemispheres (bodies, atria, horns)
- **Third Ventricle**: Between thalami, communicates via foramen of Monro
- **Fourth Ventricle**: Between pons/medulla/ cerebellum, communicates via aqueduct of Sylvius
- **Circulation**: CSF produced by choroid plexus (500 mL/day), flows through subarachnoid space

## 7.2 Spinal Cord Organization

### Gross Structure

**External Landmarks**:
- **Cervical Enlargement**: C5-T1, supplies upper limbs and branches extensively
- **Lumbar Enlargement**: L2-S1, supplies lower limbs and maintains extensive trunk innervation
- **Conus Medullaris**: Tapered tip at L1-2, adult equivalent of embryonic end
- **Filum Terminale**: Fibrous extension to coccyx (cauda equina means "horse's tail")

**Cord Segments Correspondence**:
- **C1-C7**: Spinal roots exit above corresponding vertebral pedicles
- **C8**: Between C7-T1 vertebrae
- **T1-S5**: Roots exit below corresponding pedicles (cauda equina)
- **Clinical Significance**: L4 vertebral level contains L5-S1 cord segments

### Internal Organization

**White Matter Tracts**:
- **Ascending (Sensory) Pathways**:
  - Dorsal columns (touch, vibration, proprioception) → medial lemniscus → contralateral thalamus
  - Spinothalamic tract (pain, temperature) → contralateral thalamus (crossing in spinal cord)
  - Spinocerebellar tracts (proprioception) → ipsilateral cerebellum

- **Descending (Motor) Pathways**:
  - Corticospinal tract (95% cross in medulla) → ventral corticospinal tract
  - Rubrospinal tract (fine motor coordination)
  - Reticulospinal tract (posture, locomotion)

**Gray Matter Organization**:
- **Dorsal Horn**: Sensory input integration
- **Ventral Horn**: Motor neuron cell bodies (alpha motor neurons)
- **Intermediate Zone**: Autonomic preganglionic neurons, interneurons

### Arterial Supply

**One Anterior and Two Posterior Spinal Arteries**:
- **Anterior Spinal Artery**: Supplies anterior 2/3 of cord (corticospinal/pyramidal tracts)
- **Posterior Spinal Arteries**: Supplies posterior 1/3 (dorsal columns)
- **Radicular Arteries**: Feed individual roots, largest at C5-C8 (artery of Adamkiewicz)

## 7.3 Peripheral Nervous System

### Spinal Nerves (31 Pairs)

**Five Types by Region**:
- **Cervical (8 pairs)**: Most mixed functions, dermatomes follow anatomical landmarks
- **Thoracic (12 pairs)**: Intercostal nerves between ribs, T10 marks umbilical dermatome
- **Lumbar (5 pairs)**: Lumbar plexus (L2-L4), femoral(L2-4)/sciatic(L4-S3) divisions
- **Sacral (5 pairs)**: Sacral plexus forms sciatic nerve (L4-S3), supplies majority of leg
- **Coccygeal (1 pair)**: Minimal motor, sensory from coccygeal region

### Cranial Nerves (12 Pairs)

**Sensory Nerves**:
- **I - Olfactory**: Smell, olfactory bulb to cerebral cortex
- **II - Optic**: Vision, retina to lateral geniculate body
- **VIII - Auditory/Vestibular**: Hearing/equilibrium, cochlear/vestibular nuclei
- **X - Vagus**: Extensive autonomic, sensory from viscera

**Motor Nerves**:
- **III/IV/VI - Oculomotor/Trochlear/Abducens**: Extraocular muscles, parasympathetics
- **V - Trigeminal**: Facial sensation/mastication motor
- **VII - Facial**: Facial expression, taste, parasympathetic salivary glands
- **IX - Glossopharyngeal**: Pharyngeal muscles, taste, parasympathetic
- **XI - Accessory**: Trapezii/sternocleidomastoids, pharyngeal muscles
- **XII - Hypoglossal**: Tongue muscles

**Mixed Nerves**:
- **X - Vagus**: True mixed cranial nerve, most extensive distribution

## 7.4 Autonomic Nervous System

### Sympathetic Division

**Thoracolumbar Outflow (T1-L2)**:
- **Preganglionic**: Short myelinated fibers from intermediolateral cell column
- **Ganglia**: Paravertebral chain (sympathetic trunks) and prevertebral ganglia
- **Postganglionic**: Long unmyelinated fibers to target organs

**Key Sympathetic Effects**:
- **Cardiovascular**: Increased heart rate/contractility, vasoconstriction (fight/flight)
- **Respiratory**: Bronchodilation, decreased mucous secretion
- **Gastrointestinal**: Decreased motility and secretions (rest/digest inhibition)
- **Thermoregulation**: Vasoconstriction, piloerection, sweating

### Parasympathetic Division

**Craniosacral Outflow**:
- **Cranial**: CN III, VII, IX, X (parasympathetic nuclei)
- **Sacral**: S2-S4 (pelvic splanchnic nerves)
- **Postganglionic**: Short fibers near target organs

**Key Parasympathetic Effects**:
- **Cardiovascular**: Decreased heart rate, mild vasodilation
- **Ophthalmic**: Miosis, accommodation for near vision
- **Salivary/Skeletal Glands**: Increased secretions
- **Gastrointestinal**: Increased motility and secretions
- **Genitourinary**: Bladder emptying, sexual arousal

**Dual Innervation Principle**: Most organs receive both sympathetic and parasympathetic input for coordinated control.

## CBME Skill Station: Neurological Examination

**Objective**: Perform systematic neurological assessment integrating anatomical knowledge with clinical findings

**Equipment Required**:
- Neurological hammer, tuning fork, cotton wool, pin
- Opthalmoscope, pupil gauge cards
- Snellen chart, Ishihara color plates
- Reflex hammer, monofilament

**Standard Examination Sequence**:

### Higher Mental Functions (Cerebrum Assessment)
**Mini-Mental Status**: Orientation, memory, attention, language
**Executive Function**: Abstraction, problem-solving, judgment
**Cortical Functions**: Praxis, gnosis, neglect syndromes

### Cranial Nerves Examination

**Olfactory (I)**: Identify common odors (coffee, vanilla, soap)
**Optic (II)**: Visual acuity, visual fields, fundoscopic examination
**Oculomotor (III)/Trochlear (IV)/Abducens (VI)**: Pupil reactivity, eye movements, ptosis
**Trigeminal (V)**: Facial sensation (all three divisions), corneal reflex, jaw strength
**Facial (VII)**: Facial symmetry, taste (anterior tongue), stapedius reflex
**Auditory (VIII)**: Weber/Rinne tests, vestibular function (Romberg test)
**Glossopharyngeal (IX)**: Gag reflex, taste (posterior tongue), pharyngeal sensation
**Vagus (X)**: Voice quality, palatal elevation, gag reflex
**Accessory (XI)**: Shoulder shrug, head turning against resistance
**Hypoglossal (XII)**: Tongue strength, tremor, atrophy

### Motor Examination
**Inspection**: Muscle atrophy, fasciculations, abnormal postures
**Tone**: Spasticity (upper motor) vs flaccidity (lower motor)
**Power**: MRC scale 0-5 for all muscle groups
**Involuntary Movements**: Tremor, chorea, dystonia

### Sensory Examination
**Primary Modalities**:
- **Light Touch**: Soft cotton wool, dermatome assessment
- **Pain**: Sharp pin, spinothalamic tract evaluation
- **Temperature**: Cold/warm perceptions
- **Vibration**: 128 Hz tuning fork, dorsal column assessment
- **Proprioception**: Joint position sense, finger-nose test

**Complex Sensations**:
- **Two-point Discrimination**: Fingertips vs palm
- **Stereognosis**: Object identification by touch
- **Graphesthesia**: Number recognition
- **Cortical Sensations**: Double simultaneous stimulation

### Reflexes
**Deep Tendon Reflexes (DTR)**:
- Biceps (C5-6), triceps (C6-7), knee (L2-4), ankle (S1-2)
- Grading: 0 (absent), 1+ (diminished), 2+ (normal), 3+ (brisk), 4+ (clonus)

**Superficial Reflexes**:
- Abdominal reflexes (T8-12), cremasteric reflex (L1-2), plantar response
- Pathologic signs: Babinski (upgoing toe), Hoffman's sign (finger flick)

### Coordination and Gait
**Finger-Nose-Finger Test**: Cerebellar hemisphere function
**Rapid Alternating Movements**: Dysdiadochokinesia
**Heel-Shin Test**: Posterior column integrity
**Romberg Test**: Vestibular vs proprioceptive dysfunction
**Gait Assessment**: Normal, tandem, heel, toe walking

## Clinical Localization in Neurology

### Upper Motor Neuron vs Lower Motor Neuron Lesions

**Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) Lesion**:
- **Location**: Cerebral cortex, brainstem, spinal cord (corticospinal tract)
- **Signs**: Spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign, loss of dexterity
- **Pathophysiology**: Loss of cortical inhibition, release phenomenon
- **Example**: CVA affecting motor cortex

**Lower Motor Neuron (LMN) Lesion**:
- **Location**: Anterior horn cell, peripheral nerve, neuromuscular junction
- **Signs**: Flaccidity, hyporeflexia, fasciculations, atrophy
- **Pathophysiology**: Direct motor neuron loss or innervation failure
- **Example**: Guillain-Barré syndrome

### Spinal Cord Syndromes

**Complete Transection**:
- Loss of all modalities below lesion level
- Initial spinal shock (flaccid paralysis)
- Later spasticity, autonomic dysreflexia
- Brown-Sequard syndrome (hemisphere lesion)

**Central Cord Syndrome**:
- Upper extremity > lower extremity weakness
- Cap-like sensory loss over shoulders
- Bladder dysfunction spared

**Anterior Cord Syndrome**:
- Motor and spinothalamic loss below lesion
- Preserved dorsal column sensation
- Result of anterior spinal artery occlusion

### Cerebrovascular Territories

**Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA)**:
- **Supplies**: Medial frontal/parietal lobes, corpus callosum
- **Deficit**: Contralateral leg weakness/sensory loss, abulia, incontinence
- **Unique Sign**: Grasp reflex

**Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA)**:
- **Supplies**: Lateral cortical surfaces, internal capsule
- **Deficit**: Contralateral hemiplegia, hemisensory loss, visual field loss
- **Dominant Hemisphere**: Aphasia (Broca's/Wernicke's)

**Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA)**:
- **Supplies**: Occipital lobe, parts of temporal/parietal lobes
- **Deficit**: Contralateral homonymous hemianopia, visual agnosia
- **Additional**: Memory impairment (hippocampus involvement)

## Advanced Neuroimaging Correlation

### Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

**T1-Weighted Images**: CSF dark, fat bright, good for anatomy
**T2-Weighted Images**: CSF bright, edema bright, good for pathology
**FLAIR**: Suppresses CSF, highlights periventricular lesions
**DWI/ADC**: Acute stroke detection (cytotoxic edema)
**MRA**: Vascular anatomy without contrast

### Computed Tomography (CT)

**Non-contrast CT**: Hemorrhage bright white, good for acute stroke
**Contrast-enhanced CT**: Meningeal enhancement, tumor vascularity
**CTA**: Arterial anatomy for aneurysms/AVMs/stenosis
**CTP**: Cerebral blood flow and perfusion parameters

### Specific Disease Patterns

**Multiple Sclerosis**: Periventricular "Dawson's fingers" on FLAIR
**Acute Stroke**: DWI hyperintense lesions in vascular territories
**Tumor**: Ring enhancement with surrounding edema
**Infection**: Meningeal enhancement, ventriculitis patterns

## Summary and CBME Integration

The nervous system transforms neuroanatomical structure into the foundation of human consciousness and interaction. CBME learning develops clinicians who understand neurological disorders as anatomical failures rather than abstract conditions:

**From Anatomy to Clinical Diagnosis**: Stroke localization guides therapeutic decisions
**From Pathology to Patient Impact**: Neurological deficits become understandable functional losses
**From Diagnosis to Rehabilitation**: Anatomical knowledge guides recovery expectations
**From Organ to Person**: Neurological examination becomes holistic patient assessment

**CBME Key Insight**: "The nervous system isn't just wires and switches—it's the anatomical foundation that makes us think, feel, move, and interact with our world."

## MCQs and Applied Questions

### Competency-Based MCQs

**Question 1**: A patient has contralateral weakness and sensory loss but preserved proprioception. The most likely lesion location is:
A) Cerebral cortex
B) Posterior limb of internal capsule
C) Spinal cord dorsal column
D) Peripheral nerve

**Answer: B** - Posterior limb of internal capsule lesion affects spinothalamic tract but spares dorsal column medial lemniscus

**Question 2**: Which cranial nerve examination tests both sensory and motor function of the glossopharyngeal nerve?
A) Gag reflex only
B) Taste from posterior tongue only
C) Both gag reflex and posterior tongue taste
D) Neither adequately tests both functions

**Answer: C** - Gag reflex tests sensory and motor; posterior taste tests sensory component

**Question 3**: In Brown-Sequard syndrome, which sensation is lost ipsilaterally below the lesion level?
A) Pain and temperature
B) Proprioception and vibration
C) Both modalities
D) Neither modality

**Answer: B** - Ipsilateral dorsal column disruption affects vibration/proprioception
