## 4.1 Skeletal Muscle Structure

### Macroscopic Organization
- **Fascicles**: Bundles of muscle fibers
- **Epimysium**: External connective tissue sheath
- **Perimysium**: Surrounds fascicles
- **Endomysium**: Surrounds individual fibers
- **Tendons**: Continuation of connective tissue for bone attachment

### Microscopic Structure
- **Muscle fiber**: Multinucleated cell, syncytium from embryological fusion
- **Sarcomere**: Functional unit between Z-lines
- **Myofilaments**: Thick (myosin) and thin (actin) filaments
- **Sarcoplasmic reticulum**: Calcium storage and release

---

## 4.2 Muscle Classification by Function

**Prime Movers** (Agonists): Major force producer for movement
**Antagonists**: Opposing movement to agonists
**Synergists**: Assist agonists, reduce unwanted movements
**Fixators**: Stabilize joints during movement

### Upper Limb Muscles

**Shoulder Muscles**:
- **Deltoid**: Shoulder abduction, all deltoid parts
- **Rotator Cuff**: Supraspinatus (abduction), Infraspinatus/Teres minor (external rotation), Subscapularis (internal rotation)

**Arm Muscles**:
- **Biceps Brachii**: Supination and elbow flexion
- **Triceps Brachii**: Elbow extension
- **Brachialis**: Elbow flexion (pure flexor)

## 4.3 Clinical Muscle Testing

### Manual Muscle Testing Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Normal power against full resistance |
| 4 | Movement against resistance/good power |
| 4- | Movement against gravity/minimal resistance |
| 3 | Movement against gravity only |
| 2 | Movement possible with gravity eliminated |
| 1 | Trace contraction/no movement |
| 0 | No contraction |

**CBME Correlation**: Grading helps distinguish upper vs lower motor neuron lesions.

---

## 4.4 Neuromuscular Junction

### Structure and Function
- **Presynaptic neuron**: Acetylcholine vesicles
- **Synaptic cleft**: 20-30nm gap
- **Postsynaptic membrane**: Acetylcholine receptors
- **Acetylcholinesterase**: Breaks down acetylcholine

### Pathophysiology
**Myasthenia Gravis**:
- Autoantibodies against acetylcholine receptors
- Fatigable weakness beginning with ocular muscles
- Ice pack test: Muscle cooling improves function

**Lambert-Eaton Syndrome**:
- Antibodies against voltage-gated calcium channels
- Reduced acetylcholine release
- Proximal muscle weakness plus autonomic symptoms

---

## 4.5 Lower Motor Neuron Disorders

### Peripheral Nerve Injuries

**Radial Nerve**: "Wrist drop"
- Motor loss: Triceps, wrist extensors, finger extensors
- Sensory loss: Posterior lateral forearm, dorsum of hand

**Peroneal Nerve**: "Foot drop"
- Motor loss: Tibialis anterior, peronei
- Sensory loss: Dorsum of foot

### Brachial Plexus Injuries

**Erb-Duchenne Palsy**: C5-C6 roots
- "Waiter's tip position"
- Arm adducted and internally rotated

**Klumpke Paralysis**: C7-T1 roots
- Claw hand deformity
- Horners syndrome (T1 root involvement)

---

## 4.6 Upper Motor Neuron Disorders

### Cortical Lesions
- Contralateral hemiplegia/spastic paralysis
- Babinski sign present
- No muscle atrophy

### Brainstem Lesions
- Alternating syndromes (e.g., Wallenberg syndrome)
- Cranial nerve deficits plus contralateral body findings

### Spinal Cord Lesions
**Complete Transection**:
- Paralysis below lesion
- Loss of sensation below lesion
- Spinal shock initially (flaccid paralysis)

**Brown-Sequard Syndrome** (Partial hemisection):
- Ipsilateral loss: Proprioception, contralateral pain/temperature
- Contralateral loss: Pain and temperature below lesion

## CBME Skill Station: Muscle Power Assessment

**Objective**: Systematically assess muscle power and identify motor neuron lesions

**Equipment**: Hand strength dynamometer, neurological hammer

**Procedure**:
1. **Inspection**: Note muscle wasting, fasciculations
2. **Muscle testing**: Grade each muscle group 0-5
3. **Tone assessment**: Passive movement resistance
4. **Reflexes**: Biceps, triceps, knee, ankle
5. **Sensation**: Light touch, pinprick, proprioception

**Competency Rubric**:
- **Level 3**: Complete assessment, identifies pathology, explains findings
- **Level 2**: Adequate technique, recognizes abnormalities
- **Level 1**: Incomplete assessment, inaccurate conclusions

## Clinical Applications

### Sports Injuries
**Hamstring tear**: Sudden painful pop, ecchymosis
**Quadriceps rupture**: Inability to extend knee against gravity
**Rotator cuff tear**: Weak painful abduction

### Neurological Emergencies
**Acute flaccid paralysis**: Could indicate Guillain-Barré syndrome
**Progressive weakness**: Differential diagnosis between myopathy vs neuropathy
**Facial asymmetry**: Consider Bell's palsy vs stroke

## Summary

The muscular system provides force for movement while neuromuscular control enables precision. CBME learning transforms muscle knowledge into clinical competence by:

- **Motor Assessment Skills**: Systematic evaluation of power and tone
- **Lesion Localization**: Differentiating central vs peripheral nervous system pathology
- **Patient Functional Impact**: Explaining how weakness affects daily activities
- **Therapeutic Planning**: Guiding rehabilitation based on anatomical recovery potential

**CBME Insight**: "Muscles don't work in isolation—understanding neuromuscular integration reveals why patients can't walk, write, or even smile."

## MCQs

**Question**: Patient with wrist drop but preserved wrist flexion. Most likely nerve injury?
A) Radial
B) Median
C) Ulnar
D) Brachial plexus

**Answer: A** - Radial nerve innervates wrist extensors
