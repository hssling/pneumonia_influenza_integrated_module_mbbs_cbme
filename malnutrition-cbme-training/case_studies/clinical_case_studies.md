## Case Study 1: Classic Marasmus

### **Patient Profile**
- **Name:** Ravi Kumar
- **Age:** 10 months (male)
- **Presenting Complaint:** Gradual weight loss and lethargy for 2 months
- **Family Background:** Second child, birth interval 18 months, low socioeconomic status

### **Clinical Presentation**
- **General:** Severely malnourished appearance, cachectic ("old man" look)
- **Vital Signs:** Temperature 35.2°C, heart rate 110/min, respiratory rate 40/min
- **Anthropometry:**
  - Weight: 5.2 kg (expected: 9.0 kg, deficit 42%)
  - Height: 68 cm (height-for-age z-score: -2.8)
  - MUAC: 9.8 cm (severe wasting)
- **Physical Examination:**
  - Baggy pants sign positive
  - Depressed temples, visible ribs
  - Dry, wrinkled skin with some pigmentation
  - No edema
  - Sparse, dry hair easily pluckable

### **Laboratory Investigations**
- Hb: 7.2 g/dl (anemia)
- Albumin: 2.8 g/dl (low)
- Glucose: 45 mg/dl (hypoglycemia)
- Serum potassium: 3.2 mEq/L
- Total lymphocyte count: 1200/µl

### **Diagnosis**
Moderate-severe marasmus (protein-energy malnutrition)

### **Management**
- **Immediate (Day 1):**
  - Hypoglycemia correction: 10% dextrose IV
  - Monitor temperature to prevent hypothermia
  - Started F-75 formula feeds

- **Stabilization Phase (Days 1-7):**
  - F-75 formula: 100 kcal/kg/day initially
  - Small frequent feeds (2-hourly)
  - Oral rehydration for loose stools
  - Amoxicillin for prevention of infections

- **Rehabilitation Phase (Weeks 2-4):**
  - F-100 formula transition
  - Weight gain target: 10-15 g/kg/day
  - Vitamin/mineral supplementation
  - Family counseling on feeding practices

### **Outcome**
Discharge after 4 weeks with 2.5 kg weight gain. Mother taught complementary feeding and hygiene practices.

### **Discussion Points**
1. Why was hypoglycemia the presenting complaint?
2. What are the pathophysiological mechanisms leading to marasmus?
3. How does the WHO 10-step protocol apply here?
4. What preventive measures should be emphasized?

## Case Study 2: Kwashiorkor with Infection

### **Patient Profile**
- **Name:** Priya Sharma
- **Age:** 16 months (female)
- **Presenting Complaint:** Swelling of body and fever for 1 week
- **Family History:** Recent change from breastfeeding to diluted milk and rice, family migrated seasonally

### **Clinical Presentation**
- **General:** Irritable, apathetic child with moon-like facies
- **Vital Signs:** Temperature 38.5°C, heart rate 125/min, respiratory rate 50/min
- **Anthropometry:**
  - Weight: 8.5 kg (expected: 10.2 kg, deficit 17%)
  - Height: 78 cm (normal height-for-age)
  - MUAC: 11.2 cm (moderate wasting)
  - Generalized pitting edema (Grade II)

- **Physical Examination:**
  - Flaky paint dermatosis (peeling skin)
  - Sparse hair with flag sign
  - Hepatomegaly (3 cm below costal margin)
  - Conjunctival pallor and angular cheilitis
  - Bilateral chest crepitations

### **Laboratory Investigations**
- Hb: 6.8 g/dl
- Albumin: 1.5 g/dl
- Chest X-ray: Right lower lobe pneumonia
- Stool microscopy: Giardia cysts (+)
- Serum electrolytes: Hyponatremia (128 mEq/L)

### **Complications**
- Severe pneumonia requiring oxygen
- Hyponatremic seizures
- Sepsis with shock (required ICU care)

### **Management Challenges**
- **Edema Management:** Careful fluid restriction initially, then gradual correction
- **Infection Control:** IV antibiotics for pneumonia + albendazole for Giardia
- **Nutritional Rehabilitation:** Delayed start due to sepsis
- **Refeeding Syndrome Prevention:** Slow electrolyte correction

### **Outcome**
Hospital stay of 6 weeks. Discharged with significant catch-up growth. Required nutritional rehabilitation center support.

### **Discussion Points**
1. Why did kwashiorkor develop despite relatively preserved weight?
2. How does infection complicate kwashiorkor management?
3. What role did sudden feeding changes play?
4. Long-term developmental prognosis?

## Case Study 3: Marasmic-Kwashiorkor (Mixed Form)

### **Patient Profile**
- **Name:** Amit Singh
- **Age:** 13 months (male)
- **Presenting Complaint:** Severe wasting with edema, recurrent diarrhea
- **Background:** Multiple episodes of infection, inadequate complementary feeding

### **Clinical Presentation**
- **General:** Most severe malnutrition picture
- **Vital Signs:** Bradycardia, hypothermia (34.8°C), acidosis
- **Anthropometry:**
  - Weight: 5.8 kg (expected: 10.5 kg, deficit 45%)
  - Height: 72 cm (stunted)
  - MUAC: 9.2 cm (<11.5 cm = severe wasting)
  - Generalized edema (marked)

- **Severe Features:**
  - Extreme bradycardia and hypotension
  - Hypothermia resistant to warming
  - Severe wasting with edema (marasmic-kwashiorkor)
  - Multiple organ dysfunction

### **Complications**
- Cardiac failure during rehydration
- Severe hypoglycemia (glucose <20 mg/dl)
- Sepsis with disseminated candidiasis
- Refractory hyponatremia and metabolic acidosis

### **Management**
- **Initial Stabilization:** ICU admission, mechanical ventilation support
- **Fluid Management:** Cautious rehydration to avoid cardiac overload
- **Nutritional Support:** Prolonged stabilization with F-75, gradual advancement
- **Infection Control:** Broad-spectrum antibiotics, antifungal coverage
- **Electrolyte Management:** Aggressive correction of imbalances

### **Poor Prognosis Factors**
- Age <12 months with marasmic-kwashiorkor
- Severe wasting + edema (highest mortality)
- Sepsis and organ dysfunction
- Delayed presentation to hospital

### **Outcome**
Despite intensive management, succumbed to multiple complications. Case highlighted importance of early intervention and prevention.

### **Discussion Points**
1. Why is marasmic-kwashiorkor the most lethal form?
2. What alternative feeding methods could be used in ICU?
3. Role of community interventions in prevention?
4. Ethical considerations in resource-limited settings?

## Case Study 4: Micronutrient Deficiency in Adolescent

### **Patient Profile**
- **Name:** Seema Devi
- **Age:** 14 years (female)
- **Presenting Complaint:** Delayed puberty, angular cheilitis, night blindness
- **Context:** Rural tribal girl from Jharkhand, recently married

### **Clinical Presentation**
- **General:** Short statured, thin adolescent girl
- **Vital Signs:** Normal
- **Physical Signs:**
  - Angular cheilitis and glossitis
  - Koilonychia and ragged cuticles
  - Night blindness (Vitamin A deficiency)
  - Goiter (Grade II)
  - Pallor and easy fatigability

### **Laboratory Investigations**
- Hb: 7.5 g/dl (iron deficiency anemia)
- Vitamin A levels: 8 µg/dl (<20 µg/dl deficient)
- Thyroid profile: T3/T4 low, TSH high (iodine deficient goiter)
- Zinc levels: 45 µg/dl (<65 µg/dl deficient)
- Bone age: Delayed by 2 years

### **Multiple Deficiencies**
- **Iron Deficiency Anemia:** Due to poor dietary iron intake
- **Vitamin A Deficiency:** Seasonal vegetable scarcity in tribal areas
- **Iodine Deficiency:** Lack of iodized salt in tribal communities
- **Zinc Deficiency:** Cereal-based diet with poor bioavailability

### **Management**
- **Iron Therapy:** Iron syrup 6 mg/kg/day + vitamin C for absorption
- **Vitamin A Supplementation:** 2 lakh IU immediately, then quarterly
- **Iodine Replacement:** Iodized oil injection followed by regular use
- **Zinc Supplementation:** 20 mg/day for 2 weeks
- **Balanced Diet Counseling:** Using locally available resources

### **Social Challenges**
- Early marriage and pregnancy risk
- Limited access to healthcare
- Cultural food taboos
- Low maternal education

### **Outcome**
Marked improvement in all deficiency signs. Night vision recovered, goiter size reduced, energy levels improved.

### **Discussion Points**
1. Why are multiple deficiencies common in this group?
2. Public health interventions needed?
3. Role of tribal development programs?
4. Cultural interventions required?

## Case Study 5: Adult Malnutrition (Hospitalized Patient)

### **Patient Profile**
- **Name:** Ram Prasad
- **Age:** 65 years (male)
- **Presenting Complaint:** Chronic illness with weight loss and edema
- **Diagnosis:** Advanced carcinoma stomach with gastric outlet obstruction

### **Clinical Presentation**
- **General:** Elderly, wasted appearance
- **Anthropometry:**
  - BMI: 16.2 kg/m² (severe underweight)
  - MUAC: 18 cm (muscle wasting)
  - Weight loss: 12 kg in 6 months (>10%)

- **Physical Signs:**
  - Muscle wasting, temporal wasting
  - Subcutaneous fat loss
  - Sacral edema
  - Poor wound healing
  - Recurrent infections

### **Mini-Nutritional Assessment (MNA)**
- BMI <19: 0 points
- Weight loss >3 kg: 0 points
- Mobility reduced: 1 point
- Acute disease: 2 points
- Psychological stress: 0 points
- Neuropsychological problems: 2 points
- Total Score: 5/30 → Severely malnourished

### **Management**
- **Dietary Counseling:** Small, frequent nutrient-dense meals
- **Protein Supplementation:** High-protein diet plan
- **Vitamin/Mineral Supplements:** Multivitamin preparation
- **Monitoring:** Regular weight and MUAC measurement
- **Palliative Care:** Symptom management while preserving nutritional status

### **Discussion Points**
1. How does malignancy affect nutritional status?
2. Assessment tools for hospitalized elderly?
3. Ethical issues in artificial nutrition?
4. Role of palliative nutritional support?

## Case Study 6: Community Screening Camp

### **Scenario**
Medical students conducting growth monitoring camp at Anganwadi center in rural Karnataka. Children aged 6-59 months screened for malnutrition.

### **Findings**
- **Total Children:** 125 screened
- **Severe Acute Malnutrition:** 8 children (6.4%) with MUAC <11.5 cm
- **Moderate Acute Malnutrition:** 15 children (12%) with MUAC 11.5-12.5 cm
- **Stunting:** 42 children (33.6%) with height-for-age <-2SD
- **Underweight:** 35 children (28%) with weight-for-age <-2SD

### **Clinical Encounters**

**Child 1: Severe Wasting**
- 9-month-old boy, MUAC 9.8 cm, no edema
- Mother reports frequent loose stools and inadequate complementary feeding
- Referred to NRC for treatment, mother counseled on feeding

**Child 2: Stunting with Recent Wasting**
- 18-month-old girl, height z-score -3.2 (severely stunted), weight normal recently
- Indicates chronic malnutrition with acute episode possibly due to infection
- Investigation revealed worm infestation
- Albendazole given, nutrition education provided

**Child 3: Overweight but Stunted**
- 30-month-old boy, MUAC normal but height z-score -2.5
- Case of "double burden" - overfeeding with nutrient-deficient foods
- Education on balanced feeding and activity promotion

### **Public Health Actions**
1. **Active Case Detection:** All SAM cases referred to treatment centers
2. **Family Counseling:** Nutrition education sessions conducted
3. **Community Mobilization:** Mothers' groups formed for peer support
4. **Linkages Created:** With ICDS delivery and health services
5. **Referral System:** Established for severely malnourished children

### **Discussion Points**
1. Why are screening camps important?
2. What are the limitations of camp-based assessments?
3. How can community-based management be strengthened?
4. Surveillance role of such camps?
