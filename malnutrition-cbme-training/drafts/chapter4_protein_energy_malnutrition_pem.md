## Protein-Energy Malnutrition (PEM)

### **Definition**
Protein-energy malnutrition (PEM) is a range of pathological conditions arising from **coexistent lack of protein and energy (calories)** in varying proportions, occurring most frequently in infants and young children and commonly associated with **infectious diseases**.

*(WHO, 2021)*

### **Pathophysiology**

#### **Energy Deficiency Effects**
- **↓ Basal metabolic rate**: Adaptive reduction to conserve energy
- **↓ Protein synthesis**: Impaired tissue repair and growth
- **↓ Immune function**: Increased susceptibility to infections
- **↓ Cardiac output**: Due to reduced muscle mass

#### **Protein Deficiency Effects**
- **↓ Plasma proteins**: Reduced oncotic pressure → edema
- **↓ Muscle mass**: Wasting of skeletal muscles
- **↓ Enzyme function**: Impaired metabolic processes
- **↓ Hormone production**: Altered endocrine function

---

## Classification of PEM

### **Wellcome Classification System**

#### **Based on Body Weight and Edema**

| Classification | Weight as % of Expected* | Edema | Clinical Features |
|----------------|--------------------------|-------|-------------------|
| **Normal** | >80% | Absent | Well-nourished child |
| **Underweight** | 60-80% | Absent | Early signs of malnutrition |
| **Marasmus** | <60% | Absent | Severe wasting without edema |
| **Kwashiorkor** | Any weight | Present | Edematous malnutrition |
| **Marasmic-Kwashiorkor** | <60% | Present | Mixed form |

*Expected weight calculated using WHO growth standards

---

### **IAP/Indian Classification**

#### **PEM Grading**

| Grade | Weight Deficit | Clinical Signs | Management |
|-------|----------------|----------------|------------|
| **Grade I** | 71-80% | Mild wasting | Community management |
| **Grade II** | 61-70% | Moderate wasting | Supplementary feeding |
| **Grade III** | 51-60% | Severe wasting | Institutional care |
| **Grade IV** | <50% | Life-threatening | Intensive care unit |

## Marasmus

### **Definition**
Marasmus is a severe form of **acute malnutrition** characterized by **severe wasting** of both **fat and muscle**, affecting children usually between 6-24 months of age.

### **Clinical Features**

#### **General Appearance**
- **Cachectic appearance**: "Old man" look
- **Severe wasting**: Skeleton-thin body, prominent bones
- **Loss of subcutaneous fat**: Temple, ribs, arms clearly visible
- **Baggy pants sign**: Loose skin over buttocks

#### **Facial Features**
- **Moon-like facies**: Shrunken cheeks with depressed temples
- **Sparse, dry hair**: Easily pluckable, dyspigmented
- **Wide-eyed appearance**: Large eyes in small face
- **Expressionless face**: Reduced facial fat

#### **Body Systems**

**Skin:** Dry, scaly, wrinkled, patchy pigmentation  
**Muscles:** Severe wasting, flaccid, pallor  
**Abdomen:** Scaphoid abdomen, visible gastric peristalsis  
**Extremities:** Thin limbs, matchstick arms/legs  

### **Laboratory Findings**

- **Hemoglobin**: 6-9 g/dl (normocytic anemia)
- **Serum albumin**: 2.0-2.5 g/dl (decreased)
- **Electrolytes**: Usually normal, but may have hyponatremia
- **Glucose**: Often hypoglycemia (stress response)
- **Blood urea nitrogen**: Low
- **Total lymphocyte count**: <1500/µl

### **Pathophysiology**
```
Protein-Calorie Deficiency → ↓Metabolic Rate → Muscle Breakdown → Keiosis → Ammonia Toxicity
                                                                  ↓
                                                         ↓Glutamine Synthesis → ↓Liver Function
                                                                  ↓
                                                         Acid-Base Imbalance → Metabolic Acidosis
```

## Kwashiorkor

### **Definition**
Kwashiorkor is a severe form of PEM characterized by **edema** in the presence of **adequate calorie intake** but **severe protein deficiency**, occurring commonly after weaning or during periods of dietary change.

### **Clinical Features**

#### **Edema as Cardinal Sign**
- **Grade I**: Pitting edema on feet only
- **Grade II**: Edema on feet and legs
- **Grade III**: Generalized edema including face, arms
- **Grade IV**: Anasarca with pleural effusion

#### **Skin Changes ("Flaky Paint Dermatosis")**
- **Peeling skin**: Starting from extremities, progressing inward
- **Red/purple discoloration**: Underlying dermis visible
- **Hyperkeratosis**: Thickened, dry skin lesions
- **Infection prone**: Due to skin breakdown

#### **Hair Changes**
- **Flag sign**: Alternating bands of normal/dyschromic hair
- **Silky, sparse hair**: Easy to pluck
- **Hypopigmentation**: Lightening of normal hair color
- **"Squirrel tail" appearance**: Twisted, thin hair

#### **Hepatic Manifestations**
- **Hepatomegaly**: Due to fatty infiltration
- **Jaundice**: Due to hepatic dysfunction
- **Ascites**: In severe cases
- **Hypoalbuminemia**: <2.0 g/dl consistently

#### **Other Features**
- **Moon facies**: Edematous face
- **Muscle wasting**: Moderate (less than marasmus)
- **Apathy and irritability**: Due to brain protein deficiency
- **Diarrhea**: Often present

### **Laboratory Findings**

- **Hemoglobin**: 7-10 g/dl (microcytic anemia)
- **Serum albumin**: <2.0 g/dl (severe hypoalbuminemia)
- **Electrolytes**: Hyponatremia, hypokalemia
- **Blood glucose**: Variable, may have hypoglycemia
- **SGOT/SGPT**: Mild elevation
- **Serum cholesterol**: Decreased due to liver disease

### **Pathophysiology**
```
Protein Deficiency → ↓Albumin Synthesis → ↓Oncotic Pressure → Leaky Capillaries → Edema
                                      ↓
                           ↓Immune Function → Recurrent Infections → ↓Albumin Further
                                      ↓
                           Fatty Liver Infiltration → Liver Cell Damage → Metabolic Disturbances
```

## Marasmic-Kwashiorkor

### **Mixed Form Characteristics**
- **Severe wasting**: <60% expected weight (marasmic features)
- **Edema present**: Kwashiorkor features
- **Most severe form**: Highest mortality rate
- **Clinical features**: Combination of both conditions

---

## Complications of PEM

### **Cardiovascular Complications**
- **Cardiomyopathy**: Decreased cardiac mass, reduced output
- **Bradycardia**: Due to electrolyte imbalance
- **Hypotension**: Orthostatic changes
- **Sudden death**: During refeeding

### **Metabolic Complications**
- **Refeeding syndrome**: Phosphate, potassium, magnesium depletion
- **Hypothermia**: Impaired temperature regulation
- **Hypoglycemia**: Depleted glycogen stores
- **Metabolic acidosis**: Lactic acidosis, ketoacidosis

### **Infection Complications**
- **Increased susceptibility**: Due to immune deficiency
- **Common infections**: Pneumonia, diarrhea, sepsis
- **Septicaemia**: Leading cause of death
- **TB activation**: Decreased host defenses

### **Nutritional Complications**
- **Vitamin deficiencies**: Multiple micronutrient deficits
- **Trace element deficiencies**: Zinc, copper, selenium
- **Growth failure**: Long-term developmental delay
- **Multiple organ failure**: In severe untreated cases

---

## Differential Diagnosis

### **Conditions Mimicking PEM**

#### **With Edema (Kwashiorkor-like)**
- **Nephrotic syndrome**: Proteinuria, hyperlipidemia
- **Congestive heart failure**: Cardiac causes
- **Liver cirrhosis**: Ascites present
- **Hypothyroidism**: Slow movements, edema

#### **With Wasting (Marasmus-like)**
- **Juvenile diabetes**: Polyuria, polydipsia
- **TB abdomen**: Fever, distension
- **Celiac disease**: Steatorrhea, abdominal pain
- **Cystic fibrosis**: Recurrent infections

## Management of Severe Acute Malnutrition

### **WHO 10-Step Protocol for SAM Management**

#### **Step 1: Initial Assessment**
- **ABC approach**: Airway, Breathing, Circulation
- **Hypothermia**: <35.5°C wrap in blankets
- **Hypoglycemia**: Blood sugar <54 mg/dl, treat immediately
- **Dehydration**: Assess for shock or severe dehydration

#### **Step 2: Nutritional Rehabilitation**
**Phase 1: Stabilization (Days 1-2)**
- **F-75 formula**: 100-130 kcal/kg/day, 1.0-1.5 g protein/kg/day
- **Small frequent feeds**: Every 2-3 hours
- **Monitor intake**: Target 130 ml/kg/day in children <1 year
- **Breast milk continuation**: For breastfed infants

**Phase 2: Transition (Days 3-7)**
- **F-100 formula**: 150 kcal/kg/day, 2.9 g protein/kg/day
- **Gradual increase**: Weight gain target 5-10 g/kg/day
- **Appetite stimulation**: One-to-one feeding
- **Nutrient supplementation**: Vitamins and minerals

**Phase 3: Outpatient Care**
- **RUTF (Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food)**: Plumpy'nut, BP-100
- **Home-based management**: With RUTF and weekly follow-up
- **Growth monitoring**: Continued assessment
- **Family education**: Prevention strategies

#### **Management of Specific Complications**

**Hypoglycemia Management:**
- 10% dextrose: 5 ml/kg IV or 50 ml orally
- Prevention: Night feeds containing sugar

**Hypothermia Management:**
- Warm environment, blankets, mother's body heat
- NG tube feeding if unable to suck

**Dehydration Management:**
- Plan C (severe dehydration): IV fluids with caution
- Avoid rapid correction: May cause heart failure

**Electrolyte Imbalance:**
- Potassium correction: Only after urine output established
- Micronutrient supplementation: Zinc, vitamin A

**Infection Management:**
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics: Ampicillin + Gentamicin
- Antimalarials if endemic area
- Deworming after stabilization

## Criteria for Discharge and Follow-up

### **Discharge Criteria**
- **Weight gain**: Steady weight gain for 3 consecutive days
- **Appetite return**: Eating therapeutic quantities
- **Medical stability**: No active infections, edema resolved
- **Family preparedness**: Ability to provide adequate home care

### **Follow-up Protocol**
- **Weekly monitoring**: Weight, MUAC, edema assessment
- **Immunization status**: Complete vaccinations
- **Micronutrient supplements**: Vitamin A, iron, zinc
- **Growth chart plotting**: Continuing monitoring
- **Dietary counseling**: Locally available, affordable foods

---

## Prevention Strategies

### **Individual Level**
- **Exclusive breastfeeding**: 0-6 months
- **Appropriate complementary feeding**: 6-24 months
- **Vitamin A supplementation**: Quarterly doses
- **Deworming**: 6-monthly regimen
- **Immunization**: Complete EPI schedule

### **Family Level**
- **Nutrition education**: Balanced diet preparation
- **Hygiene practices**: Safe water, handwashing
- **Birth spacing**: Minimum 36 months
- **Women empowerment**: Education, economic independence

### **Community Level**
- **ICDS strengthening**: Anganwadi services expansion
- **Nutrition surveillance**: Community-based monitoring
- **Supplementary nutrition**: THR distribution
- **Behavior change communication**: Local media utilization

---

## Prognosis and Long-term Outcomes

### **Mortality Risk Factors**
- **Age <6 months**: Highest risk (OR 3.2)
- **Weight <50% median**: Severe deficit
- **Edema present**: Marasmic-kwashiorkor worst
- **Infections present**: Multiplies mortality risk
- **Hypothermia/low blood sugar**: Immediate danger signs

### **Recovery Expectations**
- **Weight gain**: 15-20 g/kg/day during rehabilitation
- **Stunting reversal**: 50-70% height deficit recovery possible
- **Developmental delay**: 30-50% can be reversed
- **Cognitive development**: School performance may be affected

### **Long-term Complications**
- **Growth retardation**: Permanent stunting
- **Intellectual impairment**: IQ reduction by 10-15 points
- **Immune deficiency**: Prolonged susceptibility
- **Metabolic disorders**: Increased metabolic diseases risk

## Case Studies

### **Case 1: Classic Marasmus**

```
Patient: Ravi, 12 months, male
Presentation: Severe wasting, weight 5kg (expected 9kg), no edema
Clinical findings: Baggy pants sign, sparse hair, sunken cheeks
Management: F-75 diet initiation, treated hypoglycemia
Outcome: Recovered with supplementary feeding, discharge after 6 weeks
Learning: Early intervention prevents organ damage
```

### **Case 2: Kwashiorkor**

```
Patient: Priya, 18 months, female
Presentation: Severe edema, moon facies, peeling skin
Clinical findings: Albumin 1.8 g/dl, hepatomegaly, flag sign hair
Management: Careful rehydration, protein supplementation
Complications: Sepsis managed with antibiotics
Outcome: Gradual recovery, nutritional counseling emphasized
Learning: Protein deficiency causes edema, not just calorie deficit
```

### **Case 3: Marasmic-Kwashiorkor**

```
Patient: Kumar, 14 months, male
Presentation: Severe wasting + edema, weight 5.2kg, generalized edema
Clinical findings: Most severe case, hypothermia, bradycardia
Management: Intensive care unit admission, slow refeeding
Outcome: Prolonged rehabilitation, 8-week hospital stay
Learning: Highest mortality risk, careful biochemical monitoring essential
```

## Summary

Protein-energy malnutrition represents the most severe manifestation of undernutrition with two distinct clinical forms: marasmus (wasting without edema) and kwashiorkor (edema with adequate calories). Management requires systematic approach using WHO protocols, careful monitoring for complications, and comprehensive rehabilitation programs. Prevention focuses on improving complementary feeding practices, maternal education, and strengthening ICDS services.
