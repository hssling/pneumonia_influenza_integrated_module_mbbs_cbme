## Classification of Malnutrition

### **WHO International Classification (2016)**

#### **1. Weight-Related Classifications**

**For Children Under 5 Years:**

| Category | Indicator | Cut-off Points | Interpretation |
|----------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Underweight** | Weight-for-age | <-2 SD (moderate)<br><-3 SD (severe) | Overall malnutrition |
| **Wasting** | Weight-for-height | <-2 SD (moderate)<br><-3 SD (severe) | Acute malnutrition |
| **Stunting** | Height-for-age | <-2 SD (moderate)<br><-3 SD (severe) | Chronic malnutrition |
| **Overweight** | Weight-for-height | >+2 SD | Excessive weight gain |

**SD = Standard Deviation from WHO Child Growth Standards median**

#### **2. BMI Classification for Adults**

| Category | BMI (kg/m²) | Risk of Co-morbidities |
|----------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Underweight** | <18.5 | Low (but risk of other clinical problems) |
| **Normal weight** | 18.5-24.9 | Average |
| **Overweight** | 25.0-29.9 | Increased |
| **Obese Class I** | 30.0-34.9 | Moderate |
| **Obese Class II** | 35.0-39.9 | Severe |
| **Obese Class III** | ≥40.0 | Very severe |

---

### **Protein-Energy Malnutrition (PEM) Classification**

#### **Waterlow's Classification (1972)**

| % of Expected Weight | % of Expected Height | Clinical Condition |
|----------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Normal (>90%) | Normal (>95%) | **Normal** |
| Reduced (<90%) | Normal (>95%) | **Acute PEM (Wasting)** |
| Normal (>90%) | Reduced (<95%) | **Chronic PEM (Stunting)** |
| Reduced (<90%) | Reduced (<95%) | **Acute on Chronic PEM** |

#### **Wellcome Classification**

Based on presence of edema and percentage of expected weight:

| Classification | Weight % of Expected | Edema | Comment |
|----------------|----------------------|-------|---------|
| **Normal** | >80% | - | Well nourished |
| **Undernutrition** | 60-80% | - | Mild to moderate malnutrition |
| **Marasmus** | <60% | - | Severe wasting |
| **Kwashiorkor** | Any weight | + | Edematous malnutrition |
| **Marasmic-kwashiorkor** | <60% | +/- | Mixed picture |

## Anthropometric Assessment Tools

### **Essential Measurements**

#### **1. Weight Measurement**
- **Equipment**: Digital weighing scale (0.1 kg accuracy)
- **Technique**: Child in minimal clothing, no shoes
- **Calibration**: Zero daily, use flat surface
- **Recording**: Record to nearest 0.1 kg

#### **2. Height/Length Measurement**
- **Equipment**: Stadiometer/infantometer
- **Child <2 years**: Supine length measurement
- **Child >2 years**: Standing height
- **Technique**: Child against wall, head in Frankfort plane
- **Recording**: To nearest 0.1 cm

#### **3. Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC)**
- **Equipment**: MUAC tape (color coded)
- **Technique**: Measure midpoint of upper arm
- **Interpretation**:
  - **Green (<11.5 cm)**: Severe acute malnutrition
  - **Yellow (11.5-12.5 cm)**: Moderate acute malnutrition
  - **Red (>12.5 cm)**: Normal

#### **4. Head Circumference (0-36 months)**
- Measured for detecting growth faltering
- Normal range: 32-37 cm at birth, 45-50 cm at 12 months

---

### **Z-Score Calculations**

#### **Formula:**
```
Z-score = (Observed value - Median value for age/sex) / Standard deviation for age/sex
```

#### **Interpretation of Z-scores:**

| Z-score Range | Classification | Public Health Significance |
|---------------|----------------|---------------------------|
| **>-2** | Normal | Acceptable |
| **-3 to -2** | Moderate deficit | Poor |
| **<-3** | Severe deficit | Very poor |

#### **Examples:**
- Child with weight-for-age z-score = -2.5 → Moderate underweight
- Child with height-for-age z-score = -3.2 → Severe stunting
- Child with weight-for-height z-score = -2.8 → Severe wasting

## Clinical Assessment

### **Physical Signs of Malnutrition**

#### **General Signs:**
- **Muscle wasting**: Temporal, interosseous, quadriceps
- **Subcutaneous fat loss**: Cheeks, chest, abdomen, thighs
- **Hair changes**: Sparse, easily pluckable, dyspigmented
- **Skin changes**: Dry, flaky, hyperpigmented, loose
- **Edema**: Nutritional edema (kwashiorkor)

#### **Specific Signs:**

**Vitamin A Deficiency:**
- Night blindness, Bitot's spots, corneal xerosis
- Follicular hyperkeratosis

**Iron Deficiency:**
- Koilonychia, atrophic glossitis, angular cheilitis
- Pallor of conjunctiva, palms, nail beds

**Vitamin C Deficiency:**
- Scurvy: petechiae, gingival bleeding, subperiosteal hemorrhage

**Protein Deficiency:**
- Moon facies, baggy pants, flag sign of hair

## Biochemical Assessment

### **Laboratory Investigations**

#### **1. Hematological Tests:**
- **Hemoglobin**: <11 g/dl indicates anemia
- **Serum ferritin**: <15 ng/ml indicates iron deficiency
- **Total iron binding capacity (TIBC)**: >400 µg/dl in iron deficiency

#### **2. Protein Status:**
- **Serum albumin**: <3.5 g/dl indicates protein malnutrition
- **Total lymphocyte count**: <1500/µl indicates malnutrition
- **Serum transferrin**: Decreased in PEM

#### **3. Micronutrient Levels:**
- **Vitamin A**: <20 µg/dl indicates deficiency
- **Vitamin D**: <20 ng/ml indicates deficiency
- **Zinc**: <65 µg/dl indicates deficiency
- **Iron, folate, B12**: According to deficiency patterns

## Dietary Assessment Methods

### **Quantitative Methods**

#### **1. 24-Hour Dietary Recall:**
- Detailed account of all foods consumed in past 24 hours
- Multiple passes for accuracy
- Use food models/measuring tools
- Limitations: Day-to-day variation, recall bias

#### **2. Food Frequency Questionnaire:**
- How often specific foods consumed over defined period
- Useful for ranking nutrient intake
- Semiquantitative approach

#### **3. Food Weighing Method:**
- Food weighed before and after cooking/consumption
- Most accurate method
- Resource intensive

---

### **Indirect Methods**

#### **1. Food Consumption Score:**
- Based on frequency of consumption of 7 food groups
- Range: 0-56 points
- <21 points: Poor food consumption

#### **2. Household Hunger Scale:**
- Worried about food shortage
- Eating less preferred foods
- Eating fewer meals

#### **3. Coping Strategies Index:**
- Changes in food consumption patterns
- Selling assets, borrowing money
- Skipping meals

## Growth Monitoring Tools

### **WHO Growth Charts**

#### **Components:**
1. **Weight-for-age chart**: Tracks growth progress
2. **Height-for-age chart**: Identifies stunting
3. **Weight-for-height chart**: Detects wasting

#### **Uses:**
- **Individual level**: Monitor child growth
- **Program level**: Evaluate intervention effectiveness
- **Epidemiological**: Estimate population malnutrition burden

---

### **Growth Velocity**

#### **Normal Growth Rates:**
- **Infants (0-3 months)**: 25-30 g/day (weight)
- **Infants (3-6 months)**: 15-20 g/day
- **Children (6-12 months)**: 7-12 g/day
- **Children (1-2 years)**: 5-8 g/day

#### **Causes of Poor Growth Velocity:**
- Inadequate intake
- Frequent infections
- Genetic factors
- Endocrine disorders

## Screening Tools for Community Use

### **Simplified Assessment Protocol**

#### **Step 1: MUAC Measurement**
- Single most practical screening tool
- Can be done by community health workers
- No age adjustment needed

#### **Step 2: Bilateral Pitting Edema**
- Check for edema at feet, legs, sacrum
- Indicates kwashiorkor
- Medical emergency when present

#### **Step 3: General Appearance**
- Visible wasting
- Loss of subcutaneous tissue
- Changes in behavior/activity

## Integrated Assessment Framework

### **WHO Conceptual Framework**

```
Immediate Causes               Underlying Causes              Basic Causes
• Inadequate dietary intake    • Food insecurity              • Political/economic
• Diseases                     • Poor care practices           • Cultural factors
• Improper feeding practices   • Inadequate health services    • Limited resources
                                • Unhygienic environment       • Environmental degradation
```

### **Assessment at Different Levels**

#### **Individual Level:**
- Anthropometrics + clinical examination
- Dietary intake assessment
- Biochemical tests when indicated

#### **Household Level:**
- Food security assessment
- Caregiver knowledge and practices
- Socioeconomic status

#### **Community Level:**
- Population surveys
- Health system assessment
- Environmental factors

## Assessment in Special Situations

### **Assessment During Pregnancy**

#### **Key Indicators:**
- Maternal BMI and weight gain
- Iron-folic acid supplementation
- Iodine nutrition status
- Gestational diabetes screening

#### **Risk Assessment:**
- Pre-pregnancy BMI <18.5 → High risk
- Adolescent pregnancy → Extra monitoring
- Previous preterm/low birth weight delivery

### **Assessment in Elderly**

#### **Mini Nutritional Assessment (MNA):**
- Anthropometric measurements
- Dietary questionnaire
- Self-assessment
- Score <17: Malnourished
- Score 17-23.5: At risk

### **Assessment in Hospitalized Patients**

#### **Subjective Global Assessment:**
- Weight change, dietary intake
- Gastrointestinal symptoms
- Functional capacity
- Physical examination

## Quality Assurance in Assessment

### **Standardization Requirements:**

#### **Equipment:**
- Calibrated weighing scales
- Standardized MUAC tapes
- Accurate measuring boards/stadiometers

#### **Training:**
- Inter-observer variability <5%
- Annual refresher training
- Competency assessment

#### **Supervision:**
- Random spot checks
- Data quality audits
- Corrective actions

---

## Summary

Nutritional assessment is a systematic process requiring accurate measurements, appropriate classification, and comprehensive evaluation. The choice of assessment tools depends on the setting, purpose, and resources available. Proper documentation and quality assurance are essential for effective malnutrition management programs.
