## The ECG Complex: A Systematic Overview

The ECG represents cardiac electrical activity as a waveform that can be divided into waves, segments, and intervals. Each component reflects specific electrophysiological events within the heart.

### Understanding ECG Nomenclature
- **Wave**: A positive or negative deflection from baseline
- **Segment**: A portion between waves (usually isoelectric)
- **Interval**: Includes a wave plus surrounding segment
- **Complex**: Complete waveform series (e.g., QRS complex)

## P Wave: Atrial Depolarization

The P wave represents right and left atrial depolarization and conduction through the interatrial pathways.

### P Wave Characteristics
- **Morphology**: Usually upright in leads I, II, aVF; biphasic or inverted in V1
- **Duration**: 0.06-0.12 seconds (≤2.5 mm in height)
- **Amplitude**: 2.5 mm or less in any lead
- **Axis**: 0° to +75° (direction of atrial depolarization)

### P Wave Variations and Significance
- **Tall P wave (>2.5 mm)**: Right atrial enlargement (P pulmonale)
- **Wide P wave (>0.12 sec)**: Left atrial enlargement (P mitrale)
- **Inverted P wave**: Retrograde conduction (junction rhythm); technical error
- **Absent P wave**: Atrial fibrillation; other atrial arrhythmias

### Clinical Correlates
- **P wave morphology** reflects atrial pathology
- **P wave axis** indicates conduction abnormalities
- **P-P interval** helps assess atrial rate and regularity

## PR Interval: Atrioventricular Conduction

The PR interval measures time from atrial depolarization onset to ventricular depolarization onset.

### PR Interval Measurement
- **Normal Range**: 0.12-0.20 seconds (3-5 mm at 25 mm/s)
- **Heart Rate Dependence**: Shortens slightly with tachycardia
- **Apparent vs True**: Measured from same P-QRS complex

### PR Interval Abnormalities
- **Short PR interval (<0.12 sec)**:
  - Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome
  - Enhanced AV conduction
  - Accessory pathways

- **Long PR interval (>0.20 sec)**:
  - First-degree AV block
  - Medications (digitalis, beta-blockers)
  - Degenerative conduction disease
  - Myocarditis, ischemia

### Clinical Applications
- **First-degree block**: Often benign; monitor for progression
- **Short PR interval**: Evaluate for pre-excitation syndromes
- **PR segment depression**: Usually a benign normal variant

## QRS Complex: Ventricular Depolarization

The QRS complex represents right and left ventricular depolarization, atrial repolarization (usually hidden), and early ventricular repolarization.

### QRS Complex Components
- **Q wave**: First negative deflection (septal depolarization)
- **R wave**: First positive deflection (main ventricular activation)
- **S wave**: Negative deflection following R wave
- **R' or S'**: Additional deflections (bundle branch blocks)

### QRS Complex Measurements
- **Duration**: 0.06-0.10 seconds (<0.12 sec normal)
- **Voltage**: Varies by lead system and patient factors
- **Axis**: Mean direction of ventricular depolarization (usually +30° to +90°)

### Specific Wave Components and Significance
- **Q Wave**: Normal <40 ms, <1/4 R wave height; pathological if QS or >40 ms
- **R Wave**: Largest ventricular wave; poor R wave progression suggests pathology
- **S Wave**: Deeper in right precordial leads; profound S suggests RV strain

### Pathological QRS Patterns
- **Wide QRS (>0.12 sec)**: Bundle branch blocks, ventricular rhythms, drug toxicity
- **Low voltage**: Pericardial effusion, infiltrative disease, poor conduction
- **Fragmented QRS**: Myocardial scarring, risk of arrhythmias

## ST Segment: Early Ventricular Repolarization

The ST segment connects the QRS complex to the T wave, representing the isoelectric period when ventricles are depolarized but not contracting.

### ST Segment Characteristics
- **Normal**: Isoelectric (at TP segment level)
- **Duration**: Variable with heart rate and autonomic tone
- **Slope**: Normally upward in limb leads, variable in precordial

### ST Segment Abnormalities
- **ST Elevation (>1 mm in limb leads, >2 mm in precordial)**:
  - Myocardial infarction (STEMI)
  - Prinzmetal's angina
  - Pericarditis (diffuse elevation)
  - Left ventricular aneurysm

- **ST Depression (>1 mm)**:
  - Myocardial ischemia (NSTEMI)
  - Digitalis effect
  - Strain patterns (LVH)
  - Tachycardia effects

### Measurement Techniques
- **Reference point**: TP segment (between T wave end and P wave start)
- **Lead-specific criteria**: Different thresholds for different lead groups
- **Serial comparison**: Changes over time more significant than absolute values

## T Wave: Ventricular Repolarization

The T wave represents ventricular repolarization, a complex process influenced by multiple factors.

### T Wave Morphology
- **Normal**: Asymmetric, rounded peak; same direction as preceding QRS
- **Amplitude**: 1/6 to 2/3 of R wave height
- **Duration**: Approximately 160-200 ms
- **Direction**: Usually concordant with QRS (except aVR, V1)

### T Wave Abnormalities
- **Tall peaked T waves**: Hyperkalemia, early acute ischemia, strain
- **Flattened T waves**: Hypokalemia, ischemia, metabolic disorders
- **Inverted T waves**: Ischemia, strain, medications, normal variants
- **Biphasic T waves**: Intermediate ischemia, electrolyte changes

### Regional Patterns
- **Precordial T inversion**: Anterior ischemia, coronary artery disease
- **Limb lead T inversion**: Lateral/femoral ischemia, systemic conditions
- **Wellens' T waves**: Deep symmetric inversion in anterior leads (proximal LAD)

## QT Interval: Complete Ventricular Depolarization-Repolarization

The QT interval represents total time for ventricular depolarization and repolarization.

### QT Interval Measurement
- **Definition**: Start of QRS to end of T wave (downstroke returns to baseline)
- **Normal Range**: 0.36-0.44 seconds (varies with age, gender, heart rate)
- **Heart Rate Correction**: QTc = QT / √(RR interval in seconds) [Bazett formula]

### Gender and Age Differences
- **Women**: QTc slightly longer than men (by ~20 ms)
- **Age**: QTc longer in children and elderly
- **Diurnal Variation**: Shortens during daytime, lengthens at night

### QT Interval Prolongation (>0.44-0.46 sec)
**Congenital Long QT Syndromes**:
- LQTS1 (KCNQ1 gene): Risk of torsades with exercise
- LQTS2 (KCNH2 gene): Auditory triggers for arrhythmias
- LQTS3 (SCN5A gene): Life-threatening during sleep

**Acquired QT Prolongation**:
- Antiarrhythmics (quinidine, sotalol)
- Antibiotics (erythromycin, clarithromycin)
- Antipsychotics (haloperidol, risperidone)
- Electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia, hypocalcemia)

### QT Interval Shortening (<0.34 sec)
- **Hypercalcemia**: Accelerated repolarization
- **Digitalis effects**: PROLONG QT (paradoxical effect!)
- **Early repolarization**: Normal variant in young individuals

## U Wave: Late Ventricular Repolarization

The U wave is a small deflection following the T wave, representing late ventricular repolarization of mid-myocardial cells (M-cells).

### U Wave Characteristics
- **Amplitude**: Usually <25% of T wave height
- **Direction**: Same as T wave (concordant)
- **Timing**: 0.02-0.04 seconds after T wave end

### U Wave Abnormalities
- **Tall U waves**: Severe hypokalemia, bradycardia, hypothermia
- **Inverted U waves**: Coronary artery disease, LVH
- **Absent U waves**: Normal variant or pathological conditions
- **Prominent U waves**: Often seen with bradycardia

### Clinical Significance
- **U wave ratio (>1:1 with T wave)**: Suggests increased arrhythmia risk
- **Negative U waves**: May indicate ischemia
- **Hyperacute U waves**: Early sign of ischemia

## Measurement Techniques and Accuracy

### ECG Paper Basics
- **Speed**: 25 mm/second (5 mm = 0.2 seconds)
- **Voltage**: 10 mm = 1 mV (standard calibration)
- **Box size**: Small box = 1 mm, Large box = 5 mm (0.04 sec)

### Systematic Measurement Approach
1. **Confirm paper speed**: Always verify recording parameters
2. **Select appropriate lead**: Use lead with clearest morphology
3. **Measure consistently**: Always from same points
4. **Calculate intervals**: Use appropriate formulas
5. **Repeat measurements**: Check for consistency
6. **Compare serially**: Changes over time are significant

### Common Measurement Pitfalls
- **Wrong lead selection**: Measurements vary between leads
- **Incorrect calipers**: Digital vs manual measurements
- **Artifacts**: Motion, baseline drift affect accuracy
- **Fusion complexes**: Abnormal rhythms complicate measurement
- **Changing heart rate**: QT interval varies with rate

## Clinical Applications of ECG Measurements

### Rate Calculations
- **Heart Rate = 300 / number of large squares between R waves**
- **Ventricular rate**: Measure R-R intervals
- **Atrial rate**: Measure P-P intervals (if visible)

### Diagnostic Utility
- **QRS duration**: Detects conduction delay, bundle branch blocks
- **QT interval**: Risk assessment for arrhythmias
- **PR interval**: Assesses AV conduction
- **R wave progression**: Evaluates ventricular function

### Serial Monitoring
- **QTc monitoring**: Essential for QT-prolonging drugs
- **QRS trend**: Assess progression of conduction disease
- **ST segment changes**: Evaluate response to therapy
- **Right ventricular strain**: Pulmonary hypertension monitoring

## Key Takeaways

1. ECG components reflect specific electrophysiological events
2. Systematic measurement is essential for accurate interpretation
3. Normal ranges vary with age, gender, and heart rate
4. Wave morphology provides clues to underlying pathology
5. Changes over time are often more significant than absolute values
6. Understanding physiology is crucial for interpreting abnormalities

## Clinical Cases

### Case 1: Drug Monitoring
A 45-year-old patient on amiodarone therapy has ECG showing QT interval of 0.52 seconds at heart rate 70 bpm.
- **Question**: What action is most appropriate?
- **Analysis**: QTc = 0.52 / √(1.03) ≈ 0.51 sec (prolonged >0.46 sec)
- **Answer**: Reduce dose or discontinue drug; monitor QTc serially

### Case 2: Morphology Assessment
A patient presents with chest pain. ECG shows ST elevation in leads V1-V4 but ST depression in aVL.
- **Mechanism**: Reciprocal changes confirm anterior ischemia
- **Clinical significance**: Extensive anterior MI requiring urgent intervention
