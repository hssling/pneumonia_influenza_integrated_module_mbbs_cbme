# Chapter 7: Supraventricular Arrhythmias

## Learning Objectives
After completing this chapter, the reader will be able to:
1. Classify supraventricular arrhythmias and understand their mechanisms
2. Recognize ECG patterns of atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, and SVT
3. Differentiate between narrow and wide complex tachycardias
4. Identify pre-excitation syndromes like Wolff-Parkinson-White
5. Apply appropriate diagnostic and treatment approaches

## Introduction

Supraventricular arrhythmias originate above the ventricles, typically from atrial tissue or the atrioventricular junction. They account for the majority of clinical arrhythmias encountered in medical practice. Understanding their mechanisms, ECG characteristics, and clinical implications is essential for medical students, as these conditions often require urgent intervention and have significant impact on patient care.

## Mechanisms of Supraventricular Arrhythmias

### Arrhythmia Pathways
- **Automaticity**: Enhanced pacemaker activity in ectopic foci
- **Re-entry**: Impulse traveling in circles through anatomically or functionally distinct pathways
- **Triggered Activity**: Spontaneous depolarizations following action potentials

### Anatomic Considerations
- **Atrial Tissue**: Multiple potential foci for automatic rhythm disturbances
- **AV Node**: Acts as both impulse filter and re-entry pathway
- **His-Purkinje System**: Rapid conduction to ventricles
- **Accessory Pathways**: Congenital bypass tracts (WPW syndrome)

## Atrial Fibrillation

### Pathophysiology
- **Most Common Sustained Arrhythmia**: Affects 2.7-6.1 million Americans
- **Mechanism**: Multiple wavelets re-enter through atrial tissue
- **Trigger**: Pulmonary vein foci in 90% of paroxysmal AF
- **Maintenance**: Refractory atrial tissue with complex activation patterns

### ECG Characteristics
#### Classic Pattern
- **Absence of P Waves**: Replaced by chaotic "f" waves
- **Irregular Baseline**: Fibrillatory waves at 300-600 bpm
- **Ventricular Response**: Irregularly irregular (atrial activation <1000/min)
- **Isoelectric Baseline**: When "f" waves not visible

#### Variants
- **Coarse Fibrillatory Waves**: Larger amplitude (>1 mm) in peripheral leads
- **Fine Fibrillatory Waves**: Smaller amplitude (<1 mm), harder to detect
- **Rapid Ventricular Response**: RVR >100 bpm without rate control
- **Slow Ventricular Response**: Complete AV block, ventricular rhythm control

### Clinical Features
#### Presentation
- **Palpitations**: Most common symptom in paroxysmal AF
- **Fatigue**: Exercise intolerance, reduced cardiac output
- **Dyspnea**: Secondary to tachycardia or heart failure
- **Asymptomatic**: Discovery during routine screening

#### Classification
- **Paroxysmal**: Episodes terminate spontaneously (<7 days)
- **Persistent**: Sustained >7 days but can be cardioverted
- **Long-standing Persistent**: >1 year, usually refractory
- **Permanent**: Failed cardioversion or anticoagulation contraindication

### Diagnostic Criteria
- **Irregularly Irregular Rhythm**: No two consecutive R-R intervals equal
- **No Discrete P Waves**: Replaces by coarse or fine fibrillation
- **QRS Morphology**: Usually narrow unless aberrancy or bundle branch block
- **Clinical Context**: Associated with hypertension, diabetes, sleep apnea

### Management Principles
#### Anticoagulation
- **CHADS₂ Score**: Predicts stroke risk (≥2 indicates anticoagulation)
- **Congestive Heart Failure**: 1 point
- **Hypertension**: 1 point (if history)
- **Age ≥75**: 1 point
- **Diabetes Mellitus**: 1 point
- **Prior Stroke**: 2 points

#### Rate vs Rhythm Control
- **Rate Control**: Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin
- **Rhythm Control**: Chemical cardioversion (amiodarone, flecainide)
- **Catheter Ablation**: Pulmonary vein isolation for paroxysmal AF
- **Lifestyle**: Weight loss, sleep apnea treatment, alcohol moderation

## Atrial Flutter

### Pathophysiology
- **Macro Re-entry Circuit**: Around right atrium roof or tricuspid annulus
- **Typical Location**: Isthmus-dependent flutter around right atrial valve
- **Conduction Ratio**: 2:1, 3:1, or variable block at AV node
- **Rate**: Atrial rate 250-350 bpm, ventricular rate half that

### ECG Characteristics

#### Classic Sawtooth Pattern
- **Negative Flutter Waves**: Downward in inferior leads
- **Regular Atrial Activation**: 300-350 bpm typical
- **Isoelectric Baseline**: Between flutter waves
- **F Wave Duration**: >0.04 sec but <0.12 sec

#### Additional Findings
- **Fixed Block**: 2:1 or 4:1 conduction ratio
- **Variable Block**: Irregular ventricular response
- **Counterclockwise Flutter**: More common (negative in II, III, aVF)
- **Clockwise Flutter**: Positive in inferior leads, may be wider complex

### Variants
- **Atypical Flutter**: Higher rate (>350 bpm), unusual morphology
- **Left Atrial Flutter**: Post-ablation, varies by re-entry circuit
- **Incisional Flutter**: Post-cardiac surgery with atrial scar formation
- **Tachycardia-Brady**: Shows both atrial flutter and sinus bradycardia

### Differentiation from Atrial Fibrillation
- **Regularity**: Flutter has regular ventricular response
- **Baseline**: Regular "F" waves vs chaotic fibrillation
- **Rate Relationship**: 1:3 or 1:4 ratio vs irregular in AF
- **Termination**: Sudden vs gradual in AF

### Management
- **Rate Control**: Beta-blockers for symptoms, not rhythm conversion
- **Anticoagulation**: Same as AF (CHADS₂ score ≥2)
- **Catheter Ablation**: Cavotricuspid isthmus ablation highly successful
- **Electrical Cardioversion**: Better success than AF

## Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT)

### Classification
#### Atrioventricular Nodal Reentry (AVNRT)
- **Mechanism**: Dual pathways within AV node
- **Rate**: 140-250 bpm
- **Duration**: Sudden onset/termination (paroxysmal)
- **Precipitant**: Stress, caffeine, drugs

#### Atrioventricular Reciprocating (AVRT)
- **Mechanism**: Re-entry through AV node and accessory pathway
- **Rate**: Similar to AVNRT
- **Wolff-Parkinson-White**: Manifests pre-excitation during sinus rhythm
- **Concealed WPW**: Only during tachycardia

#### Atrial Tachycardia (AT)
- **Mechanism**: Automatic atrial focus or micro re-entry
- **Rate**: 120-220 bpm
- **P Wave Morphology**: Abnormal, precedes QRS
- **Response to Adenosine**: Poor response

### ECG Pattern Recognition

#### Narrow Complex SVT (≤0.12 sec)
- **Regular Rhythm**: R-R interval constant
- **Normal QRS**: Unless aberrancy or bundle branch block
- **Heart Rate**: 140-250 bpm
- **No P Waves Visible**: Within QRS or following

#### Differentiation Approaches
- **Rhythm Regularity**: Perfectly regular suggests AVNRT/AVRT
- **P Wave Location**: RP interval assessment
- **Rate Response**: Adenosine test for diagnosis
- **Pre-excitation Signs**: WPW with delta waves, short PR

### Diagnostic Maneuvers
#### Vagal Maneuvers
- **Carotid Sinus Massage**: Safe test if no carotid bruit
- **Valsalva Maneuver**: Bearing down against closed glottis
- **Ice Water**: Diving reflex stimulation
- **Response Time**: Conversion within 5-10 seconds diagnostic

#### Adenosine Test
- **Region Selectivity**: Blocks AV node, reveals atrial activity
- **Transient AV Block**: 6-10 second ventricular asystole
- **Atrial Activity**: Reveals flutter waves or atrial tachycardia P waves
- **WPW Diagnosis**: Degenerates to wide complex in antidromic SVT

## Wolff-Parkinson-White Syndrome

### Pathophysiology
- **Accessory Pathway**: Anomalous bundle connecting atria to ventricles
- **Anterograde Conduction**: Pre-excitation during sinus rhythm (delta wave)
- **Retrograde Conduction**: Used in AVRT tachycardia
- **Risk Stratification**: High-risk pathways require ablation

### ECG Characteristics

#### Pre-excitation During Sinus Rhythm
- **Short PR Interval**: <0.12 seconds (bypasses delay)
- **Delta Wave**: Initial slurring of QRS (0.01-0.04 sec)
- **Wide QRS Complex**: 0.11-0.12 seconds
- **Secondary ST/T Changes**: Repolarization abnormalities

#### Pathway Location Assessment
- **Left-sided Pathway**: Positive delta wave in V1, negative in V6
- **Right-sided Pathway**: Negative delta in V1, positive in V6
- **Posterior Pathways**: Delta negative in II, III, aVF
- **Septal Pathways**: Both positive and negative patterns

### Arrhythmias Associated with WPW
#### Orthodromic AVRT
- **Most Common SVT**: 75% of WPW tachycardias
- **Narrow Complex**: Ventricular depolarization via normal pathway
- **Retrograde P Waves**: Negative in inferior leads (pseudo R' in V1)
- **Rate**: 150-250 bpm

#### Antidromic AVRT
- **Wide Complex SVT**: 25%, resembles ventricular tachycardia
- **Aberrant Conduction**: Anterograde via accessory pathway
- **Treatment Priority**: Consider VT, treat like unstable SVT
- **Signs of VT**: AV dissociation, fusion beats

### Risk Assessment
#### High-Risk Features
- **Multiple Pathways**: Increased arrhythmia burden
- **Symptomatic Presentation**: Syncope, palpitations
- **Short Refractory Period**: Rapid conduction risk
- **Syncope History**: Indicates potential for VF

#### Low-Risk Features
- **Intermittent Pre-excitation**: Well-controlled pathway
- **No Symptoms**: Regular ECG, occasional palpitations
- **Long R-R DVR**: Protected, slow pathway refractory

### Management Approach
#### Conservative Management
- **Observation**: Low-risk pathways
- **Medications**: Beta-blockers for symptom control
- **Ablation Referral**: High-risk, symptomatic patients

#### Interventional Treatment
- **Radiofrequency Ablation**: 90-95% success rate
- **Trans-septal Access**: Left-sided pathways
- **Cryoablation**: His bundle protection if septal pathway
- **Fluoroscopy Guidance**: Precise localization

## Atrioventricular Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia (AVNRT)

### Mechanism
- **Dual Pathways**: Fast and slow internodal tracts
- **Re-entry Circuit**: Impulse circles within AV node
- **Trigger**: Premature atrial beat finding critical interval
- **Maintenance**: Balance between conduction velocities

### ECG Features
#### Typical AVNRT
- **Sudden Onset/Termination**: No warm-up period
- **Regular Rhythm**: Constant R-R interval
- **Narrow QRS**: Unless aberrancy
- **Pseudo r' in V1**: Retrograde P wave (short RP interval)

#### Atypical AVNRT
- **Fast/Slow**: RP interval longer than PR interval
- **Slow/Fast**: Less common, longer RP interval
- **Incessant**: Persisting between symptomatic episodes

### Differentiation from AVRT
- **RP Interval**: Very short (<90 ms) in typical AVNRT
- **Termination**: Vagal maneuvers or adenosine successful
- **No Pre-excitation**: During sinus rhythm
- **Second-degree AV Block**: VFH pattern may be seen

## Treatment Strategies for SVT

### Acute Management
#### Hemodynamically Stable
- **Vagal Maneuvers**: First-line, medication-free
- **Adenosine**: 6 mg → 12 mg → 12 mg if needed
- **Second-line**: Verapamil or metoprolol
- **Synchronized Cardioversion**: For unstable patients

#### Hemodynamically Unstable
- **Electrical Cardioversion**: Immediate with sedation
- **Energy Level**: Starting at 100J, increase as needed
- **Rhythm Confirmation**: Post-conversion ECG
- **Potential for Recurrence**: Monitor for reinitiation

### Chronic Management
- **Beta-blockers**: Propranolol, metoprolol for daily prevention
- **Calcium Channel Blockers**: Verapamil, diltiazem
- **Antiarrhythmics**: Flecainide, propafenone (Class IC)
- **Catheter Ablation**: Definitive treatment (90-95% success)

### Special Considerations
#### Pregnancy
- **Medications**: Adenosine safe, consider fetal heart rate
- **Ablation**: Third trimester if refractory
- **Delivery Planning**: Coordination with obstetric care

#### Elderly Patients
- **Drug Choice**: Consider conduction system disease
- **Monitoring**: Digoxin level monitoring if used
- **Pacing**: Consider if asymptomatic pauses develop

#### Athletic Population
- **Sports Participation**: Evaluate for underlying heart disease
- **Conduction System**: Assess for Wolff-Parkinson-White
- **Training Optimization**: Minimize arrhythmia triggers

## Key Takeaways

1. Supraventricular arrhythmias include atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, SVT, and pre-excitation syndromes
2. ECG patterns provide diagnostic clues to mechanism and appropriate treatment
3. Atrial fibrillation shows "irregularly irregular" rhythm without P waves
4. Atrial flutter demonstrates regular "F" waves with variable ventricular response
5. SVT differentiation requires understanding re-entry circuits and anatomic pathways
6. WPW syndrome involves accessory pathways with potential for dangerous arrhythmias
7. Acute management prioritizes hemodynamic stability before rhythm intervention
8. Catheter ablation offers definitive treatment for recurrent SVT

## Clinical Cases

### Case 1: Emergency Room SVT
A 45-year-old male presents with palpitations and lightheadedness. ECG shows regular narrow complex tachycardia at 180 bpm. No P waves visible.

**Interpretation:** SVT, needs urgent diagnosis and treatment
**Initial Action:** Vagal maneuvers or adenosine while preparing for cardioversion
**Key Decision:** Confirm no WPW before adenosine (risk of VF)

### Case 2: Incidental AF
Routine ECG in a 70-year-old male shows atrial fibrillation with controlled ventricular response.

**Interpretation:** Anticoagulation assessment needed
**Action:** Calculate CHADS₂ score (7 points: maximum risk)
**Management:** Warfarin or DOAC, rate control if symptomatic

### Case 3: Flutter with Variable Block
A 55-year-old female has irregular pulse. ECG shows atrial flutter with variable conduction.

**Interpretation:** 3rd-degree AV block or variable drug effect
**Action:** Evaluate conduction system, possible pacemaker consideration
**Treatment:** Beta-blockers, consider anticoagulation if AF coexists
