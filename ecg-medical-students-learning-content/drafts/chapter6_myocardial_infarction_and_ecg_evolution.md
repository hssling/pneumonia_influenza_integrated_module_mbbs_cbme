# Chapter 6: Myocardial Infarction and ECG Evolution

## Learning Objectives
After completing this chapter, the reader will be able to:
1. Describe the four phases of ECG evolution in myocardial infarction
2. Recognize ECG patterns of STEMI and their temporal changes
3. Apply diagnostic criteria for myocardial infarction using ECG
4. Differentiate between acute, evolving, and old myocardial infarctions
5. Understand serial ECG monitoring in the management of MI

## Introduction

Myocardial infarction (MI) represents the culmination of coronary artery disease, where coronary artery occlusion leads to myocardial cell death. The ECG serves as both a diagnostic cornerstone and a monitoring tool throughout the evolution of MI. Understanding the temporal ECG changes is crucial for timely diagnosis, risk stratification, and treatment decisions.

## Pathophysiology of Myocardial Infarction

### Cellular Events in MI
- **Ischemic Cascade**: Oxygen deprivation → anaerobic metabolism → cell membrane changes
- **Irreversible Injury**: Begins at 20-30 minutes, complete by 3-6 hours
- **Wave of Necrosis**: Spreads from subendocardial to epicardial regions
- **Border Zone**: Viable but ischemic myocardium at infarct periphery

### Time Course of Myocardial Damage
- **Ischemia (<20 min)**: Reversible cell injury
- **Infarction (20-180 min)**: Irreversible cell death
- **Fibrosis (weeks)**: Scar formation and left ventricular remodeling
- **Healing (months)**: Complete scar maturation

## ECG Criteria for Myocardial Infarction Diagnosis

### Universal Definition of Myocardial Infarction (2018)
- **Detection of cardiac troponin above 99th percentile of upper reference limit**
- **With at least one of:**
  - Symptoms of myocardial ischemia
  - ECG changes indicative of ischemia
  - Imaging evidence of myocardial dysfunction
  - Coronary thrombus identification

### ECG Criteria for STEMI
- **ST Elevation**: New or presumed new ST elevation ≥1 mm in ≥2 contiguous limb leads
- **Precordial Leads**: ≥2 mm in V1-V3, ≥1 mm in other precordial leads
- **Duration**: Persistent ≥20 minutes
- **Context**: Compatible clinical symptoms or serial ECG progression

### ECG Indicators of Myocardial Injury
- **ST Elevation**: Indicates transmural ischemia
- **ST Depression**: Indicates subendocardial ischemia
- **T-wave Inversion**: Represents ischemia or strain
- **Pathologic Q Waves**: Indicate myocardial necrosis

## The Four Phases of ECG Evolution

### Phase 1: Hyperacute Phase (0-30 Minutes)
- **Earliest ECG Sign**: Tall, peaked T waves (hyperacute T waves)
- **ST Segment**: Begins to elevate (often concave upward)
- **Time Frame**: First 10-20 minutes post-occlusion
- **Sensitivity**: T-wave changes most sensitive, but nonspecific

#### Hyperacute T Waves
- **Morphology**: Tall, broad, asymmetric
- **Amplitude**: 75-100% taller than baseline T waves
- **Comparison**: Symmetrical between leads
- **Rate of Change**: Appear within minutes of occlusion
- **Distinction**: From chronic ischemia or hypertrophy

#### Early ST Elevation
- **Onset**: 5-15 minutes post-occlusion
- **Morphology**: Convex upward, merges with T wave
- **Amplitude**: Progressively increases
- **Evolution Speed**: 0.5-1 mm every 5-10 minutes in evolving infarcts

### Phase 2: Acute Phase (30 Minutes - 2 Hours)
- **ST Elevation**: Becomes more obvious and convex
- **T Wave**: May remain tall or begin to invert
- **QRS Changes**: Initial R-wave loss in extensive infarctions
- **Reciprocal Changes**: ST depression in opposite leads (reciprocal injury)

#### Maximum ST Elevation
- **Peak Amplitude**: Usually 3-8 mm depending on infarct size
- **Morphology**: Straight upstroke, rounded peak
- **Distribution**: Corresponds to coronary artery territory
- **Evolution**: Gradual increase over minutes to hours

#### Reciprocal ST Depression
- **Mechanism**: Electrical injury current affecting opposite myocardium
- **Location**: Leads electrically opposite to infarct zone
- **Magnitude**: Usually 1-2 mm depression
- **Significance**: Supports diagnosis, indicates large infarct

### Phase 3: Evolving Phase (2 Hours - 24 Hours)
- **Resolution of ST Elevation**: Begins gradual return to baseline
- **T Wave Inversion**: Deeply negative, symmetric
- **Q Wave Development**: Pathologic Q waves appear
- **Maximum QRS Changes**: Most pronounced changes by 24 hours

#### Progressive T-wave Changes
- **From Tall to Inverted**: Evolution over hours
- **Depth**: Usually 3-8 mm negative
- **Duration**: Weeks to months before resolution
- **Location**: Regional, corresponding to infarct zone

#### Q Wave Formation
- **Timing**: Usually develop within 6-8 hours
- **Criteria**: ≥0.04 seconds width and ≥1/3 R-wave height in at least 2 leads
- **Significance**: Indicates myocardial necrosis, not always transmural
- **Persistence**: Some Q waves may be permanent scar markers

### Phase 4: Chronic Phase (1 Week - Life)
- **ST Segment**: Returns to baseline (except in aneurysm)
- **T Waves**: Persist inverted, then gradually normalize
- **Q Waves**: May remain as permanent ECG finding
- **Function Recovery**: Depends on infarct size and collaterals

#### Scar Formation
- **Fibrotic Tissue**: Non-conducting myocardial scar
- **ECG Manifestation**: Pathologic Q waves persist
- **Functional Consequence**: Potential arrhythmias, remodeling
- **Echocardiographic Correlation**: Wall motion abnormalities

## Infarct Localization by ECG

### Anterior Wall Myocardial Infarction
#### LAD Distribution Pattern
- **Antero-Septal**: V1-V4 with septal leads
- **Anterior Wall**: V3-V5
- **Antero-Lateral**: V4-V6, I, aVL
- **Extent Correlation**: Higher leads involved = larger territory

#### ECG Recognition
- **ST Elevation**: V2-V4 (≥2 mm criteria)
- **Reciprocal Changes**: II, III, aVF depression
- **Prognosis**: High mortality due to LCx dominance
- **Right Ventricular Extension**: V4R elevation check

### Inferior Wall Myocardial Infarction
#### RCA vs LCx Distribution
- **RCA Territory**: II, III, aVF + V4R (RV involvement)
- **LCx Territory**: II, III, aVF + I, aVL (lateral extension)
- **Posterior Involvement**: Tall R waves V1-V2

#### ECG Differentiation
- **ST Elevation**: ≥1 mm in II, III, or aVF
- **Right Ventricular Involvement**: ST elevation V4R ≥ V1
- **Atrial Involvement**: Loss of P waves (right atrial ischemia)
- **Prognosis**: Variable, depends on AV node involvement

### Lateral Wall Myocardial Infarction
#### Circumflex Artery Distribution
- **Primary Leads**: I, aVL
- **Adjunct Leads**: V5-V6
- **Hidden Lateral MI**: Clinical correlation essential
- **Size Estimation**: Lead involvement magnitude

#### ECG Recognition Challenges
- **Poor Sensitivity**: Single-lead elevation easily missed
- **Lead Positioning**: Proper V6 placement crucial
- **Reciprocal Changes**: V2-V3 depression may be seen
- **Prognosis**: Variable, often smaller infarcts

### Posterior Wall Myocardial Infarction
#### Indirect ECG Recognition
- **Primary Finding**: Tall R waves in V1-V2
- **ST Depression**: V1-V2 horizontal or upsloping
- **Dominant R/S Ratio**: R > S in V1-V2
- **Direct Confirmation**: Posterior leads (V7-V9)

#### Evolution
- **Hidden Phase**: ST depression mistaken for anterior ischemia
- **Evolution**: Progressive R-wave magnification
- **Confirmation**: Echocardiography, posterior leads
- **Size Recognition**: May be large but ECG-underdiagnosed

## Serial ECG Monitoring

### Immediate Post-Revascularization Changes
#### Successful Reperfusion
- **ST Resolution**: 50-70% within 60-90 minutes
- **T-wave Normalization**: Progressively negative → positive
- **Paradoxical Q Waves**: May appear initially then resolve
- **Urgent Cardiology Consultation**: ST resolution assessment

#### Failed Reperfusion
- **Persistent ST Elevation**: No significant resolution
- **Worsening ST Elevation**: Increasing amplitude
- **New Arrhythmias**: Ventricular irritability
- **Re-intervention Required**: Rethrombolysis or rescue PCI

### In-Hospital Monitoring Protocol
#### First 24 Hours
- **Baseline ECG**: Immediately upon admission
- **Cath Lab Activation**: STEMI identified
- **Serial ECGs**: Every 6-8 hours if stable
- **ST Monitoring**: Continuous in intensive care

#### Subacute Phase
- **Evolution Tracking**: Q wave development
- **Medical Management**: Serial ECGs for complications
- **Discharge Planning**: Final ECG before leaving hospital
- **Follow-up**: ECG at 1-2 weeks and 3 months

### Complications Detection by ECG

#### Mechanical Complications
- **Ventricular Septal Rupture**: New systolic murmur, worsening shock
- **Papillary Muscle Dysfunction**: Acute pulmonary edema, new murmur
- **Free Wall Rupture**: Inferior wall, sudden electromechanical dissociation
- **Left Ventricular Aneurysm**: Persistent ST elevation after 2-3 weeks

#### Electrical Complications
- **Complete Heart Block**: Mobitz II → third-degree block
- **Ventricular Arrhythmias**: R-on-T phenomenon
- **Atrial Fibrillation**: New-onset, hemodynamic instability
- **Bundle Branch Blocks**: New BBB, worse prognosis

## Differential Diagnosis of ST Elevation

### ST Elevation Common Causes
- **STEMI**: Coronary artery thrombosis
- **Pericarditis**: Diffuse elevation, PR depression
- **Benign Early Repolarization**: Concave ST, tall T waves
- **Left Bundle Branch Block**: New LBBB in chest pain
- **Left Ventricular Hypertrophy**: Prominent septal forces
- **Brugada Syndrome**: Right precordial ST elevation
- **Takotsubo Cardiomyopathy**: Stress-induced apical ballooning

### Clinical Differentiation
#### Coronary Spasm (Prinzmetal Angina)
- **Presentation**: Rest pain, nocturnal
- **ECG**: Transient ST elevation with pain
- **Response**: Improves with nitrates
- **Risk**: May progress to STEMI

#### Acute Pericarditis
- **ST Elevation**: Diffuse, concave upward
- **PR Depression**: Characteristic finding
- **Evolution**: ST normalization in days
- **Symptoms**: Pleuritic chest pain worse with inspiration

#### Stress Cardiomyopathy
- **Predisposition**: Emotional stress, women post-menopause
- **ECG**: Anterior ST elevation
- **Echo**: Apical akinesis, basal hyperkinesis
- **Recovery**: Complete within weeks

## ECG in Non-ST Elevation Myocardial Infarction

### NSTE-ACS Classification
#### Unstable Angina
- **Troponin**: Negative
- **Risk**: Need urgent coronary angiography
- **ECG**: ST depression, T-wave inversion, or normal
- **Management**: Anti-ischemic therapy first

#### NSTEMI
- **Troponin**: Positive (rise and fall pattern)
- **ECG**: Variable, 50% have ST depression
- **Global Risk**: TIMI or GRACE scores determine management
- **Management**: Invasive vs medical based on risk

### ECG Changes in NSTEMI
#### ST Depression
- **Horizontal or Downsloping**: Most specific for ischemia
- **Magnitude**: ≥1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads
- **Location**: Precordial more ominous than inferior
- **Duration**: Usually persists until pain resolves

#### T-Wave Changes
- **Symmetric Inversion**: New finding, evolving ischemia
- **Pseudonormalization**: Previous changes resolve briefly
- **Deep Inversion**: Indicates severe ischemia
- **Lead Groups**: Any region, but anterior worse prognosis

## Prognostic Implications

### ECG Risk Stratification
#### Poor Prognostic Findings
- **Extensive ST Elevation**: >5 leads involved
- **Anterolateral ST Elevation**: Very large infarct
- **Right Ventricular Involvement**: Inferior MI + V4R elevation
- **Post-Infarct Bundle Branch Block**: Significantly worse outcome

#### Positive Prognostic Signs
- **Quick ST Resolution**: Rapid restoration of perfusion
- **Limited Lead Involvement**: Smaller infarct size
- **Preserved R Waves**: Less myocardial necrosis
- **Swift Reperfusion**: Door-to-balloon <90 minutes

### Serial ECG Utility
#### Evolution Assessment
- **Early Recognition**: Hyperacute T waves, early ST elevation
- **Progress Monitoring**: ST resolution, Q wave formation
- **Complication Detection**: New bundle branch blocks, arrhythmia precursors
- **Recovery Evaluation**: T-wave normalization, return to baseline

#### Treatment Response Evaluation
- **Reperfusion Success**: ≥70% ST resolution within 60 minutes
- **Medical Therapy**: ST depression reduction with ischemia control
- **Invasive Procedures**: Immediate post-intervention ECGs
- **Long-term Management**: Final resting ECG assessment

## Key Takeaways

1. ECG evolution in MI follows predictable phases: hyperacute, acute, evolving, and chronic
2. Early diagnosis requires recognition of initially subtle changes like hyperacute T waves
3. Infarct localization guides coronary artery involvement and prognosis
4. Serial ECG monitoring is essential for complication detection and management
5. ST elevation differential diagnosis includes non-coronary causes
6. ECG changes provide prognostic information beyond troponin levels
7. Understanding temporal evolution enables better patient care decisions

## Clinical Cases

### Case 1: Hyperacute Recognition
A 55-year-old presents with 5 minutes of chest pain. ECG shows tall, peaked T waves in V2-V4, no ST elevation yet.

**Interpretation:** Very early anterior STEMI, hyperacute phase
**Action:** Immediate medical management, cath lab preparation
**Rationale:** T-wave changes precede ST elevation by minutes

### Case 2: Evolution Monitoring
A patient pre-PCI shows anterior ST elevation in V1-V5. Post-PCI ECG shows 80% ST resolution.

**Interpretation:** Successful reperfusion
**Action:** Continue optimal medical therapy
**Rationale:** ST resolution indicates restored coronary perfusion

### Case 3: NSTEMI Recognition
Elderly patient with chest pain shows 3 mm horizontal ST depression V4-V6, troponin positive.

**Interpretation:** High-risk NSTEMI, anterolateral ischemia
**Action:** Urgent cardiac catheterization
**Rationale:** ST depression magnitude and extent indicate large ischemic territory
