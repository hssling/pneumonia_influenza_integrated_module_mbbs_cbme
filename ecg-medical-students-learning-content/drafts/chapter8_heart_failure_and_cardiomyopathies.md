# Chapter 8: Heart Failure and Cardiomyopathies

## Learning Objectives
After completing this chapter, the reader will be able to:
1. Understand the pathophysiology of heart failure syndromes
2. Recognize ECG patterns in systolic vs diastolic heart failure
3. Differentiate between hypertrophic and dilated cardiomyopathies
4. Identify ECG changes in restrictive and infiltrative cardiomyopathies
5. Apply appropriate clinical management strategies

## Introduction

Heart failure (HF) and cardiomyopathy represent significant clinical challenges, with ECG findings providing crucial diagnostic and prognostic information. While echocardiography remains the gold standard for assessing cardiac structure and function, ECG often provides the first clues to underlying cardiac pathology. Understanding ECG manifestations enables timely intervention and risk stratification in these complex conditions.

## Pathophysiology of Heart Failure

### Heart Failure Syndromes

#### Systolic vs Diastolic Heart Failure
- **Systolic HF**: Impaired contractility, reduced ejection fraction (<40%)
- **Diastolic HF**: Impaired relaxation, preserved ejection fraction (>50%)
- **Combined HF**: Elements of both systolic and diastolic dysfunction

#### Acute vs Chronic Heart Failure
- **Acute HF**: Rapid onset, often requiring urgent intervention
- **Chronic HF**: Progressive deterioration, compensatory mechanisms activated
- **Acute on chronic**: Exacerbation of underlying chronic disease

### ECG Manifestations in Heart Failure

#### Left Ventricular Hypertrophy Patterns
- **Voltage Criteria**: Sokolow-Lyon index >35 mm (SV1 + RV5/V6)
- **Strain Pattern**: Asymmetrically inverted T waves in lateral leads
- **QRS Changes**: Increased amplitude of R and S waves
- **Axis Deviation**: Often leftward due to septal hypertrophy

#### Atrial Changes
- **Left Atrial Enlargement**: P-mitrale (bifid P wave, >0.12 sec)
- **Right Atrial Enlargement**: P-pulmonale (>2.5 mm in inferior leads)
- **Bi-atrial Enlargement**: Mixed patterns

#### Conduction Abnormalities
- **Left Bundle Branch Block**: New LBBB suggests underlying pathology
- **Intraventricular Conduction Delay**: Nonspecific finding, warrants evaluation
- **Second-degree AV Block**: Often ischemic in etiology

## Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)

### Pathophysiology
- **Genetic Disorder**: Autosomal dominant inheritance (beta-myosin heavy chain mutation)
- **Myocyte Disarray**: Disorganized myocardial architecture
- **Dynamic Obstruction**: Left ventricular outflow tract obstruction
- **Sudden Cardiac Death Risk**: Primary prevention challenge

### ECG Characteristics

#### Voltage Patterns
- **Left Ventricular Hypertrophy**: Sokolow-Lyon criteria met
- **Marked Voltage**: Extreme R-wave amplitudes (RV5 + SV1 >55 mm)
- **Q Waves**: Prominent septal Q waves in lateral leads
- **Giant Negative T Waves**: Deeply inverted in precordial leads

#### Arrhythmia Patterns
- **Premature Ventricular Complexes**: Frequent, often polymorphic
- **Ventricular Tachycardia**: Nonsustained episodes
- **Atrial Fibrillation**: Increased prevalence
- **Ventricular Fibrillation**: Sudden death mechanism

#### Conduction Abnormalities
- **Bundle Branch Blocks**: Left bundle dominant
- **AV Node Disease**: Often requires pacing
- **Accessory Pathways**: WPW-like patterns

### Risk Stratification and Management
#### High-Risk Features
- **Family History**: Sudden cardiac death in first-degree relative
- **Syncope**: Exercise-related syncope concerning
- **Massive Hypertrophy**: Septal thickness >30 mm
- **Abnormal Blood Pressure**: Blunted exercise response

#### Therapeutic Approaches
- **Beta-blockers**: Rate control, symptom relief
- **Calcium Channel Blockers**: Verapamil preferred
- **Antiarrhythmics**: Amiodarone for ventricular arrhythmias
- **ICD Implantation**: Primary prevention for high-risk patients

## Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM)

### Pathophysiology
- **Chamber Dilatation**: Left ventricle end-diastolic dimension >58 mm
- **Global Hypokinesis**: Diffuse wall motion abnormality
- **Systolic Dysfunction**: Ejection fraction <40-45%
- **Etiologies**: Idiopathic, ischemic, viral, toxic, familial

### ECG Characteristics

#### Chamber Enlargement Patterns
- **Left Ventricular Hypertrophy**: May be present or paradoxical low voltage
- **Intraventricular Conduction Delay**: Wide QRS (>0.12 sec)
- **QRS Fragmentation**: Late potentials indicative of scarring
- **Pseudoinfarct Pattern**: Q waves without coronary disease

#### Arrhythmia Manifestations
- **Atrial Fibrillation**: Common, worsens hemodynamics
- **Ventricular Tachycardia**: Monomorphic, sustained episodes
- **Bundle Branch Blocks**: Usually left bundle branch pattern
- **PVCs with Aberrancy**: Suggestive of cardiomyopathy

#### Repolarization Abnormalities
- **Nonspecific T-wave Changes**: Flattened or inverted T waves
- **ST Segment Depression**: Diffuse, nonspecific pattern
- **QT Interval Prolongation**: Metabolic or drug-related
- **U Waves**: Prominent with electrolyte abnormalities

### Clinical Management
#### Pharmacological Therapy
- **ACE Inhibitors/ARBs**: Neurohormonal blockade
- **Beta-blockers**: Rate control and reverse remodeling
- **Mineralocorticoid Antagonists**: Spironolactone/eplerenone
- **Entresto**: Sacubitril/valsartan combination

#### Device Therapy
- **ICD**: Prevention of sudden cardiac death
- **CRT**: Cardiac resynchronization for wide QRS
- **LVAD**: Bridge to transplant or permanent support
- **Heart Transplant**: Definitive therapy for end-stage disease

## Restrictive Cardiomyopathy

### Pathophysiology
- **Stiff Ventricles**: Impaired diastolic relaxation and filling
- **Preserved systolic function**: Ejection fraction often normal
- **Etiologies**: Amyloidosis, sarcoidosis, hemachromatosis
- **Prognosis**: Variable, depends on underlying cause

### ECG Characteristics

#### Conduction Abnormalities
- **AV Block**: Often second-degree type II or third-degree
- **Bundle Branch Blocks**: Fascicular or bundle branch patterns
- **Sinus Node Disease**: Symptomatic bradycardia
- **Atrial Arrhythmias**: Often atrial fibrillation

#### Low Voltage Patterns
- **Generalized Low Voltage**: <5 mm limb leads, <10 mm precordial
- **Pseudo-infarct Patterns**: Q waves without occlusion
- **Abnormal Repolarization**: Nonspecific ST-T changes
- **Arrhythmias**: Conduction system involvement

### Diagnostic Workup
#### Echocardiographic Findings
- **Normal Wall Thickness**: No hypertrophy
- **Small Ventricular Cavities**: "Teardrop" shape
- **Preserved Systolic Function**: Normal ejection fraction
- **Doppler Pattern**: Restrictive filling (E/A >2, DT <150 ms)

#### Endomyocardial Biopsy
- **Amyloid Deposition**: Congo red staining
- **Sarcoidosis**: Noncaseating granulomas
- **Hemachromatosis**: Iron overload
- **Idiopathic**: Fibrosis without specific etiology

## Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy (ARVC)

### Pathophysiology
- **Genetic Disorder**: Autosomal dominant, plakoglobin/plakophilin mutations
- **Fatty Replacement**: Right ventricular myocardium replaced by fat
- **Ventricular Arrhythmias**: Life-threatening, familial
- **Sudden Cardiac Death**: Primary prevention critical

### ECG Characteristics

#### Repolarization Abnormalities
- **T-wave Inversions**: V1-V3, beyond V1 rare in normal individuals
- **Epsilon Waves**: Late potentials visible at end of QRS
- **Prolonged S-wave Upstroke**: >55 ms in V1-V2
- **Arrhythmias During Exercise**: Premature ventricular complexes

#### Morphological Changes
- **Right Bundle Branch Block**: Complete in advanced cases
- **QRS Fragmentation**: In right precordial leads
- **R-wave Reduction**: Progressive loss of right ventricular forces
- **Axis Deviation**: Rightward deviation with disease progression

### Diagnostic Criteria (Task Force 2010)
#### Major Criteria (2 points each)
- **Imaging**: RV akinesia, dyskinesia, or aneurysm
- **Histology**: Fibrofatty replacement
- **ECG**: Epsilon wave, T-wave inversions V1-V3
- **Family History**: First-degree relative with confirmed diagnosis

#### Minor Criteria (1 point each)
- **Imaging**: RV dilation or ejection fraction reduction
- **ECG**: Ventricular tachycardia, RBBB, prolonged S-wave
- **Histology**: Mild fatty infiltration
- **Family History**: Premature sudden cardiac death

## Amyloid Cardiomyopathy

### Pathophysiology
- **Amyloid Deposition**: Light chains infiltrate myocardium
- **Restrictive Physiology**: Diastolic dysfunction predominant
- **Conduction System Involvement**: AV node and bundle branches affected
- **Poor Prognosis**: High mortality rate

### ECG Characteristics

#### Voltage Patterns
- **Low Voltage**: Classic finding despite hypertrophy
- **Pseudoinfarct**: Q waves without coronary disease
- **AV Block**: High-grade conduction abnormalities
- **Atrial Arrhythmias**: Frequent atrial fibrillation

#### Morphological Changes
- **Bundle Branch Blocks**: Usually left bundle pattern
- **QRS Fragmentation**: Multiple notching or slurring
- **QT Prolongation**: Repolarization abnormalities
- **U Waves**: Prominent in some cases

## Stress Cardiomyopathy (Takotsubo)

### Pathophysiology
- **Catecholamine Surge**: Massive sympathetic activation
- **Apical Ballooning**: Mid and apical segments affected
- **Transient Disorder**: Usually complete recovery
- **Precipitants**: Emotional stress, physical illness, surgery

### ECG Characteristics

#### Acute Phase
- **ST Elevation**: Usually anterior leads
- **Q Waves**: May develop transiently
- **T-wave Inversion**: Follows ST elevation resolution
- **QT Prolongation**: Atypical U waves

#### Recovery Phase
- **T-wave Persistence**: May take weeks to normalize
- **Giant Negative T Waves**: Marked inversion in precordial leads
- **ST Resolution**: Usually within 48-72 hours
- **Normal Ejection Fraction**: Recovery by 1-3 weeks

### Differentiation from Acute MI
- **Distribution**: Mid-ventricular or apical ballooning pattern
- **Coronary Anatomy**: Normal coronary arteries
- **Recovery Pattern**: Usually complete resolution
- **Biomarkers**: Troponin elevation but smaller magnitude

## Heart Failure Management and ECG Monitoring

### Medical Therapy

#### Triple Therapy Standard
- **ACEI/ARB/ARNI**: Neurohormonal blockade
- **Beta-blockers**: Bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate
- **Aldosterone Antagonists**: Spironolactone or eplerenone
- **Discontinuation Risk**: Abrupt withdrawal dangerous

#### Additional Agents
- **Ivabradine**: For HFrEF with resting heart rate >70 bpm
- **Hydralazine + Nitrates**: Alternative to ACEI in African Americans
- **Digoxin**: Rate control in atrial fibrillation
- **Sacubitril/Valsartan**: Preferred over ACEI in appropriate patients

### Device Therapy

#### Implantable Cardioverter Defibrillator
- **Primary Prevention**: LVEF ≤35%, NYHA II-III
- **Secondary Prevention**: Survivors of VF/VT
- **MADIT Criteria**: LVEF ≤30%, NYHA I, QRS ≥130 ms
- **SCD-HeFT Population**: LVEF ≤35%, NYHA II-III

#### Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy
- **Indications**: LVEF ≤35%, NYHA III-IV, QRS ≥120 ms
- **Benefits**: Mortality reduction, functional improvement
- **Response Predictors**: Female gender, non-ischemic etiology
- **Optimization**: AV and VV delay adjustment

### ECG Monitoring in Heart Failure

#### Serial ECG Assessment
- **QRS Duration**: Baseline and follow CRT response
- **Conduction Pattern**: New bundle branch blocks concerning
- **Ventricular Rates**: Target 60-100 bpm with beta-blockers
- **Acute Changes**: New ST elevation or arrhythmias

#### Ambulatory Monitoring
- **Holter Monitoring**: Symptom-rhythm correlation
- **Event Recording**: For infrequent palpitations
- **Cardiac Loop Recorder**: Longest recording duration
- **Mobile ECG Apps**: Increasing role in long-term monitoring

## Key Takeaways

1. Heart failure ECG patterns reflect underlying pathophysiology (systolic vs diastolic)
2. Cardiomyopathy ECG changes vary by subtype (hypertrophic, dilated, restrictive)
3. Voltage criteria and strain patterns help identify LVH
4. Low voltage with conduction abnormalities suggests infiltrative disease
5. Arrhythmias are common in all cardiomyopathy types
6. Serial ECG monitoring guides therapy and predicts prognosis
7. Device therapy (ICD, CRT) has specific ECG indications

## Clinical Cases

### Case 1: Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy
A 28-year-old male with exertional syncope shows massive voltage criteria (SV1 + RV6 = 65 mm), deep T-wave inversion V2-V6. Diagnosis confirmed by echocardiogram showing septal thickness of 32 mm.

### Case 2: Dilated Cardiomyopathy
A 55-year-old female with progressive fatigue has low voltage complexes (<5 mm in limb leads), LBBB with QRS 160 ms. Echo confirms LVEF 25%.

### Case 3: Cardiac Amyloidosis
A 65-year-old patient with protein AL amyloidosis presents with low voltage ECG despite clinical heart failure. Echo shows concentric hypertrophy with restrictive physiology.
