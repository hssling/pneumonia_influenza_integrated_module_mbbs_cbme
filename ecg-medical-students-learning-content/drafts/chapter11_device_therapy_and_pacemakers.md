## Introduction

Cardiac implantable electronic devices (CIEDs) have revolutionized the management of cardiac rhythm disorders. Pacemakers and implantable cardioverter defibrillators (ICDs) restore normal heart rhythm and protect against sudden cardiac death. Understanding device function, indications, programming, and troubleshooting is essential for modern cardiac care. This chapter covers the fundamentals of cardiac device therapy and their ECG manifestations.

## Historical Development of Cardiac Pacing

### Early Attempts at Cardiac Stimulation
- **1899 Animal Experiments**: The first attempts at electrical cardiac stimulation
- **1926 Closed-Chest Method**: Studies on transcutaneous cardiac pacing
- **1932 First Transvenous Pacing**: Catheter-based right ventricular pacing
- **1958 First Permanent Pacemaker**: Internal battery-powered implantable device

### Modern Pacemaker Evolution
- **1960s Lithium Battery**: Longer device longevity and reliability
- **1970s Dual-Chamber Pacing**: Physiologic pacing with AV synchrony
- **1980s Rate-Adaptive Pacing**: Sensors for metabolic demand detection
- **1990s Biventricular Pacing**: Cardiac resynchronization therapy development

## Pacemaker Classification Systems

### Generic Pacemaker Code (NASPE/BPEG 2002)

#### Position I: Chambers Paced
- **V**: Ventricle only
- **A**: Atrium only
- **D**: Dual (atrium and ventricle)

#### Position II: Chambers Sensed
- **V**: Ventricle only
- **A**: Atrium only
- **D**: Dual (atrium and ventricle)
- **O**: None sensed

#### Position III: Response to Sensing
- **T**: Triggered (paces if intrinsic rhythm sensed)
- **I**: Inhibited (inhibits pacing if intrinsic rhythm sensed)
- **D**: Dual response (triggered + inhibited)
- **O**: None

#### Position IV: Rate Modulation
- **R**: Rate adaptive (adjusts to metabolic demand)
- **O**: Non-rate adaptive

#### Position V: Multisite Pacing
- **V**: Ventricle (left ventricle added)
- **A**: Atrium (left atrium added)
- **O**: No multisite pacing

## Common Pacemaker Configurations

### Single-Chamber Ventricular Pacemaker (VVIR)
**Indications:**
- Atrial fibrillation with bradycardia
- Chronic atrial standstill
- Non-progressive AV conduction disease

**ECG Features:**
- **Spike Morphology**: Vertical pacing spike followed by wide QRS
- **AV Dissociation**: P waves independent of paced ventricles
- **Rate Stability**: Usually 60-80 bpm, adjusts with activity

**Advantages:**
- Simple implantation
- Lower cost
- Battery longevity

### Dual-Chamber Pacemaker (DDD/DDDR)
**Indications:**
- Sick sinus syndrome with intact AV conduction
- AV conduction abnormalities requiring pacing
- Prevention of pacemaker syndrome

**ECG Features:**
- **Physiologic Rhythm**: Maintenance of AV sequence (120-160 ms AV delay)
- **Spike Timing**: Appears before both atrial and ventricular activation
- **Normal AV Relationship**: Preserved cardiac output parameters

**Advantages:**
- Physiologic pacing
- Superior hemodynamics
- Decreased thromboembolism risk

### Atrial-Based Pacing (AAI/DVI)
**Indications:**
- Isolated sinus node dysfunction
- Complete heart block above His bundle
- Preservation of cardiac output

**ECG Features:**
- **Normal Conduction**: Electrical impulses conducted through native AV node
- **Antegrade Activation**: Preserves His-Purkinje system function
- **Rate Responsiveness**: Adapts to physiological demands

**Advantages:**
- Maintains normal ventricular activation
- Improved diastolic function
- Lower incidence of AF

### Biventricular Pacemaker (CRT-P/CRT-D)
**Indications:**
- Heart failure with LV dyssynchrony
- QRS >120 ms with LVEF <35%
- NYHA class II-IV symptoms

**ECG Features:**
- **Right and Left Bundle**: Electrical activation of both ventricles occurs
- **Shortening PR Interval**: Left ventricular lead contributes to conduction
- **Fusion QRS Morphology**: Combination of intrinsic and paced pathways

**Advantages:**
- Improves cardiac synchrony
- Reduces heart failure hospitalizations
- Enhances quality of life

## Pacemaker ECG Recognition

### Pacing Spike Recognition
**Spike Characteristics:**
- **Height Variation**: Depends on lead placement and sensitivity
- **Width Variation**: Range from 0.4-2.0 ms depending on generator
- **Repolarization Artifact**: May create atrial flutter confusion
- **Generated Signal**: Small deflection representing pacing stimulus

### Ventricular Paced Complexes
**QRS Morphology:**
- **LBBB Pattern**: Typical for right ventricular apical pacing
- **Deep S Wave**: V6 lead demonstrates late conduction spread
- **R Wave Progression**: Different from native ventricular activation
- **P Wave Independence**: Atria continue firing at normal rates

### Dual-Chamber Paced Rhythm
**AV Sequence:**
- **AV Delay Programming**: Interval between atrial and ventricular spikes
- **Optimal Synchronization**: Balanced diastolic filling time allowance
- **Rate Responsiveness**: Both atrial and ventricular rate adjustments
- **Wenckebach Response**: Upper rate limit handling mechanism

### Refractory Period Concepts
**Absolute Refractory Period (ARP):**
- **Immediate Post-Pacing**: No sensing or capture possible (usually 100-150 ms)
- **Prevents Oversensing**: Duplicate stimuli and sensitivity periods
- **Far Field R Wave**: Ventricular signal sensed in atrial channel
- **Crosstalk Prevention**: Mathematically calculated refractory

**Post-Ventricular Atrial Refractory Period (PVARP):**
- **Far Field Mitigation**: Longitudinal dissociation periods
- **Endocardial Potentials**: Prevented by extended blanking intervals
- **Refractory Break-Through**: Overridden by ventricular depolarization
- **Rate Adaptive Duration**: Shortens with increased heart rates

## Troubleshooting Pacemaker Function

### Failure to Output
**Circuit Integrity:**
- **Lead Fracture**: Mechanical discontinuity prevents current delivery
- **Restrictive Insulation**: High resistance prevents threshold current
- **Header Pin Contact**: Loose connections at generator-lead interface
- **Battery Depletion**: Energy source insufficient for normal requirements

**Threshold Testing:**
- **Voltage Measurement**: Required current at generator connection
- **Strength-Duration Curve**: Energy correlation analysis
- **Polarity Switching**: Anode/cathode configurations testing
- **Rheobase Determination**: For chronicity pacing margin prescriptions

### Failure to Capture
**Patient-Related:**
- **Increased Threshold**: Myocardial fibrosis causing resistance elevations
- **Antiarrhythmic Medications**: Amiodarone causing stability fluctuations
- **Electrolyte Imbalance**: Potent Kool metabolic parameter abnormalities
- **Lead Dislodgement**: Electrode position shift during cardiac cycling

**Device-Related:**
- **Battery Depletion**: Significant voltage drops over time
- **Circuit Failure**: Open or short circuit preventing current passage
- **Lead Integrity**: Insulation breakdown causing threshold elevations
- **Generator Output**: Insufficient programming values for patient needs

### Inappropriate Sensing
**Oversensing:**
- **Diaphragm Myopotentials**: Skeletal muscle electrical interference
- **Electromagnetic Interference**: External electric fields disruption
- **Lead Fracture Potentials**: High-frequency signals from wire damage
- **T Wave Sensing**: Ventricular depolar>_ization৷ misinterpreted as new activity

**Undersensing:**
- **Lead Dislodgement**: Electrode migration reducing local signal strength
- **Long Refractory Period**: Pacing stimulus affecting post-refðacción detection
- **Low Sensitivity Settings**: Programmed below intrinsic electrical amplitudes

### Failure to Pace (Runaway Pacemaker)
**Rare Complications:**
- **Battery Failure Scenario**: Internal component malfunction causing communication loss
- **Reed Switch Damage**: Battery discharging from magnetic field exposure
- **Circuit Breakage**: Wire continuity loss during insertion manipulation

### Device Syndrome Complications
**Pacemaker Syndrome Manifestations:**
- **Retrograde Conduction**: Atrial contraction during ventricular systole
- **Blood Pressure Reduction**: Cardiac output decrease from dysynchrony
- **Fatigue Symptoms**: Exercise intolerance secondary to SV reduction
- **Palpitation Episodes**: Awareness of pacemaker rhythm variations

**Cannon A-Waves:**
- **Venous Pressure Variation**: Giant A waves during jugular palpation
- **AV Mechanical Dissociation**: Delayed ventricular rhythms causing confusion
- **Symptom Development**: Orthostatic hypotension and presyncopal episodes

## Implantable Cardioverter Defibrillators

### Primary Prevention Indications

#### High-Risk Patients
- **Ischemic Cardiomyopathy**: Ejection fraction ≤35%, prior myocardial infarction
- **Nonischemic Cardiomyopathy**: Dilated idiopathic with EF ≤30%
- **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy**: Extreme hypertrophy and risk factors
- **Arrhythmogenic RV Cardiomyopathy**: Family history and structural disease

#### Secondary Prevention
- **Cardiac Arrest Survivors**: VF arrest not due to transient causes
- **Unstable VT Episodes**: Synchronized cardioversion required
- **Syncope with VT Induction**: EPS-proven hemodynamic instability
- **Aborted Sudden Death**: Resuscitated from VF outside hospital

### ICD Programming and Therapy

#### Shock Therapies
**Defibrillation Vector:**
- **RV to Can Coil**: Superior vena cava coil configuration
- **Biphasic Waveform**: Dual-phase current delivery protocol
- **Success Rate Measurement**: First shock efficacy monitoring
- **Titre Shock Prevention**: Voltage escalation protocols

**Anti-Tachycardia Pacing (ATP):**
- **Burst Pacing**: High-frequency pulses to ventricular tachycardia termination
- **Adaptive Algorithms**: Rate and morphology resource utilization
- **Success Measurement**: VT cycle length increases after intervention
- **Energy Savings**: Battery conservation approach reducing replacements

#### Device Feature Integration

**Smart Shock Delivery:**
- **Rhythm Discrimination**: SVT from VT differentiation algorithms
- **Morphological Analysis**: QRS complex pattern recognition systems
- **Stability Assessment**: Rate variability and regularity evaluation
- **Activity Monitoring**: Patient movement correlation analysis

### Complications of ICD Therapy

#### Inappropriate Shocks
**Oversensing Situations:**
- **Lead Integrity Problems**: Insulation break leading to EMG pickup
- **Physiological Artifacts**: Diaphragm contraction signal detection errors
- **Far-field Potentials**: Adjacent chamber signal misinterpretation
- **Double Counting Issues**: Refractory period cerebellar wave recognition challenges

#### Device Issues
**Lead-Related Complications:**
- **Perforation Events**: Myocardial penetration causing tamponade concerns
- **Dislodgement Problems**: Electrode position shift affecting capture reliability
- **Fracture Development**: Wire breakages limiting system effectiveness
- **Twiddler's Syndrome**: Generator rotation causing lead displacement

#### Arrhythmia Augmentation
**Proarrythmia Potential:**
- **Increased Symptom Complexity**: Previously stable patients experiencing gradients
- **ICM Induction Risks**: Morphology changed arrhythmias from device intervention
- **Psychological Impact**: Anxiety amplified by shock anticipation
- **Monitoring Challenges**: Holter recordings complicated by spikes

## Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy

### Patient Selection Criteria
- **NYHA Functional Class**: Classes II-IV with optimal medical therapy
- **LVEF Measurements**: ≤35% confirmed by echocardiography
- **QRS Duration Parameters**: >120 ms in typical narrow complex cases
- **Sinus Rhythm Requirement**: Atrial fibrillation deterioration risk assessment

### Echocardiographic Assessment
- **Mechanical Dyssynchrony**: Radial and longitudinal function discrepancies
- **Septal Bounce Observation**: Interventricular septum paradoxical movement
- **Pulmonary Pressure Elevation**: Secondary to LV dysfunction patterns
- **Reverse Remodeling Potential**: End-diastolic volume reduction predictions

### Lead Positioning Optimization
- **Target Vein Selection**: Coronary sinus tributary identification
- **Lateral Wall Preference**: Maximum resynchronization benefit locations
- **Stabilit Prinsiple**: Threshold stability minimization strategies
- **Imaging Guidance**: Fluoroscopic and electrophysiological probe usage

### Response Prediction
- **Clinical Indicators**: NYHA class improvement expectations
- **Structural Remodeling**: End-diastolic volume reduction assessment
- **Functional Gains**: Peak VO2 increase measurement capabilities
- **Survival Benefits**: All-cause mortality improvement documentation

## Device Follow-up and Management

### Routine Device Checks
- **Visit Frequency Planning**: Quarterly, semi-annual, or annual schedules
- **Battery Status Monitoring**: Current draw and remaining longevity estimates
- **Lead Integrity Assessment**: Impedance measurements and system alerts
- **Threshold Testing Requirements**: Safety margin verification processes

### Remote Monitoring Systems
- **Automated Data Transmission**: Daily status transmission capabilities
- **Arrhythmia Detection Networks**: Unsustained VT episode tracking
- **Device Performance Analytics**: Clustering and trending information
- **Clinician Alert Systems**: Abnormal parameter escalation protocols

### End-of-Life Considerations
- **Battery Depletion Signs**: Voltage reduction and pacing parameter declines
- **HF Response Strategies**: Generator replacement timing optimization
- **Lead Management Decisions**: Extract versus abandon strategy options
- **Contingency Planning**: Bridging approaches for implant vulnerabilities

## Advanced Device Features

### Subcutaneous ICD Systems
- **Vessel Access Avoidance**: Intravenous lead system elimination benefits
- **Hematological Protection**: Infection and thrombosis risk minimization
- **Implantation Facility Reduction**: Procedure complexity and perioperative period
- **Magnet Appraisal Mechanisms**: Temporary arrhythmia prevention solutions

### Leadless Pacemakers
- **Transcatheter Delivery**: Femoral vein approach method implementation
- **Single-Chamber Limitations**: Asynchronous atrial activation acceptance
- **Retrieval Capabilities**: Extraction procedure availability options
- **Capsule Position Requirements**: Right ventricle septal placement priorities

### Multisite Pacing Systems
- **His Bundle Applications**: Native conduction preservation methodologies
- **Left Bundle Branch Areas**: Direct stimulation intervention approaches
- **Quadripolar Lead Technology**: Displacement and threshold optimization
- **Programmable Vector Selection**: Output configuration adaptability features

## Key Takeaways

1. Cardiac implantable electronic devices restore normal heart rhythm through pacing and defibrillation.
2. Pacemaker classification codes provide essential information about device capabilities.
3. ECG recognition of paced rhythms is critical for proper diagnosis and management.
4. Device malfunction requires systematic troubleshooting approach.
5. ICD therapy prevents sudden cardiac death through rhythm termination.
6. Cardiac resynchronization therapy improves heart failure outcomes.
7. Regular device follow-up ensures optimal performance and longevity.

## Clinical Cases

### Case 1: Pacemaker Malfunction
A 78-year-old patient presented with multiple idiopathic pacemaker shocks. ECG revealed complete AV block progression despite dual-chamber device programming.

**Device Analysis:** Lead dislodgement confirmed by chest radiography investigations, requiring extraction and revision procedures.

### Case 2: Inappropriate ICD Shocks
A 65-year-old veteran with ischemic cardiomyopathy experienced harsh chest bumps hourly. Device interrogation revealed T-wave oversensing patterns causing therapy delivery.

**Management Approach:** Sensitivity reprogramming and antiarrhythmic medication addition addressed the presenting rhythm disorder problems.

### Case 3: CRT Non-Response
A refractory heart failure patient failed to achieve medical therapy improvements. CRT implantation was pursued with biventricular stimulation implementation.

**Clinical Response:** NYHA class improvement and echocardiography-documented remodeling reduction occurred after consistent pacing optimization efforts.
