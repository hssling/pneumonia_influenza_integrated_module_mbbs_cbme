# Chapter 10: Conduction Blocks and Bradyarrhythmias

## Learning Objectives
After completing this chapter, the reader will be able to:
1. Identify different types of atrioventricular conduction blocks
2. Distinguish between fascicular blocks and bundle branch blocks
3. Recognize sinus node dysfunction and AV nodal disease
4. Understand pacemaker indications and ECG patterns of paced rhythms
5. Apply clinical management strategies for conduction abnormalities

## Introduction

Conduction blocks interrupt the normal sequential depolarization of the heart, potentially leading to bradycardia, hemodynamic compromise, and increased arrhythmia risk. These blocks can occur at the sinus node, atrioventricular node, or His-Purkinje system. Understanding their ECG manifestations is crucial for appropriate intervention and pacemaker consideration.

## Sinus Node Dysfunction (Sick Sinus Syndrome)

### Pathophysiology
- **Sinus Bradycardia**: Inappropriate slowing of normal sinus rhythm
- **Sinus Arrest**: Transient cessation of sinus node activity
- **Tachy-Brady Syndrome**: Alternating tachycardias and bradycardia episodes
- **Exit Block**: Failure of sinus impulses to exit the node

### ECG Characteristics

#### Sinus Bradycardia
- **Rate**: <60 bpm in resting condition
- **Morphology**: Normal P-QRS complex
- **Rhythm**: Regular atrial and ventricular activity
- **Atrioventricular Conduction**: 1:1 AV relationship maintained

#### Sinus Arrest
- **Absent P Waves**: Brief period without atrial depolarization
- **Escape Rhythm**: Junctional or ventricular escape activation
- **Duration**: Variable from milliseconds to several seconds
- **Compensatory Pause**: Gap length varies with arousal level

#### Tachy-Brady Syndrome
- **Paroxysmal Tachycardia**: Atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter
- **Sudden Termination**: Leads to significant pauses
- **Junctional Escape**: Starts after AV nodal recovery
- **Ventricular Ectopy**: May occur during termination phase

### Clinical Significance
- **Syncope**: Related to pauses >6-8 seconds
- **Fatigue**: Due to reduced cardiac output
- **Pacemaker Indication**: Permanent pacing for symptomatic cases
- **Drug Interactions**: Many medications exacerbate conduction

## Atrioventricular Block (AV Block)

### First-Degree AV Block

#### ECG Features
- **PR Interval**: Prolonged >0.20 seconds (normal 0.12-0.20 sec)
- **P-QRS Relationship**: 1:1 conduction maintained
- **QRS Morphology**: Normal unless pre-existing bundle branch block
- **Rhythm**: Regular P-P and R-R intervals

#### Physiological Variations
- **Athletes**: Common due to vagal tone
- **Medications**: Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers
- **Age-Related Change**: Progressive with advancing years
- **Disease Associations**: Hypothyroidism, infiltrative processes

#### Clinical Implications
- **Benign Patterns**: Well-tolerated, requires no treatment
- **Progression Risk**: May advance to higher degree blocks
- **Symptom Correlation**: Usually asymptomatic
- **Monitoring**: Periodic ECG follow-up advised

### Second-Degree AV Block

#### Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach)
- **Mechanism**: Progressive PR prolongation until dropped beat
- **Location**: Usually within AV node
- **RR Interval**: Shortening between consecutive beats
- **Grouped Beating**: Long RR interval followed by progressive shortening

#### ECG Recognition
- **PR Progressive Increase**: Seen across 3-5 cycles
- **Dropped QRS**: One P wave not conducted
- **Post-Drop Reset**: Following cycles begin with shorter PR interval
- **RR Interval Pattern**: Typical 7:6:5:4:3:2 pattern before drop

#### Mobitz Type II
- **Mechanism**: Complete block below AV node in His-Purkinje system
- **Sudden Block**: Without PR prolongation
- **QRS Relationship**: Consecutive blocks show no graduated changes
- **High Risk**: Often requires urgent pacemaker placement

#### ECG Characteristics
- **PR Interval**: Usually constant until sudden block
- **Dropped Beats**: Regular pattern (2:1, 3:1 block)
- **QRS Duration**: May be prolonged if bundle branch block present
- **Ventricular Rhythm**: Most frequent P to R correlation

### Third-Degree AV Block (Complete Block)

#### Pathophysiology
- **Complete Disconnection**: Atria and ventricles activate independently
- **Escape Mechanism**: Junctional or ventricular pacemaker takeover
- **Impuls Activity**: Dependant on escape focus location
- **Circulatory Status**: Depends upon ventricular rate and perfusion

#### ECG Features
- **AV Dissociation**: P waves independent of QRS complexes
- **Regular QRS Rhythm**: Originates from escape pacemaker
- **P Wave Count**: More P waves than QRS complexes
- **Fusion/Fusion Beats**: May be seen at similar rates

#### Escape Rhythm Location
- **Nodal Escape**: Rate 40-60 bpm, narrow QRS complex
- **His Bundle Escape**: Rate 40-60 bpm, wide or narrow QRS
- **Ventricular Escape**: Rate 20-40 bpm, wide QRS complex
- **Rate Stability**: Lower foci slower but more stable rhythm

## Fascicular Blocks (Hemiblocks)

### Left Anterior Fascicular Block (LAFB)
**ECG Features:**
- **Axis Deviation**: Leftward shift to -45° to -90°
- **QRS Duration**: Minimal prolongation (<0.12 sec)
- **Morphology**: Small Q waves in I and aVL
- **Inverted Forces**: Anterior displacement of inferior vectors

**Clinical Correlation:**
- **Age-Related**: Progressive thoracic changes
- **Hypertension**: Accelerated conduction system disease
- **Coronary Artery Disease**: Subtle manifestations
- **Prognostic Value**: Modest impact on outcomes

### Left Posterior Fascicular Block (LPFB)
**ECG Features:**
- **Axis Deviation**: Rightward shift to >120°
- **Small R Wave**: Reduced R-wave amplitude in inferior leads
- **QRS Morphology**: Slight prolongation possible
- **Lead II/LIII Changes**: Right bundle branch block may coexist

**Clinical Significance:**
- **Rare Presentation**: Represents cerebrovascular compromise
- **Acute Pathologic States**: Hypertension crisis or ischemia
- **Progressive Block**: May lead to complete heart block
- **Treatment Approach**: Address underlying cardiovascular disease

### Bilateral Bundle Branch Block
**Conduction Pattern:**
- **Alternating Morphologies**: Right bundle block followed by left bundle block
- **Intermittent Manifestation**: Episodic conduction system failures
- **Progressive Slowing**: Precursor to complete block
- **High-Risk Condition**: Prompt pacemaker consideration

## Bundle Branch Blocks

### Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB)

#### Complete RBBB Criteria
- **QRS Duration**: >0.12 seconds
- **V1 Morphology**: rSR' pattern
- **V6 Morphology**: Deep S wave, absent R wave
- **MECG Adjusted Morphology**: R' amplitude equals or exceeds initial R wave

#### Incomplete RBBB Features
- **QRS Duration**: 0.11-0.12 seconds
- **Terminal Delay**: Delayed conduction evidenced in precordial leads
- **Pulmonary Changes**: Frequently associated with pulmonary hypertension
- **Hemodynamic Stress**: Right ventricular pressure overload

#### Etiology and Implications
- **Idiopathic Occurrence**: Normal variation in some individuals
- **Cardiac Pathology**: Suggestion of pulmonary hypertension
- **Pulmonary Disease**: Right heart strain indication
- **Pre-Catheterization**: Assess surgical valvular disease risk

### Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)

#### Diagnostic Criteria
- **QRS Duration**: >0.12 seconds constant
- **V1 Morphology**: Deep QS or rS waves
- **V6/I Morphology**: Broad, notched R waves
- **Mid-Precordial Complex**: Absence of Q waves in V5-V6

#### Physiological Mechanism
- **Conduction Failure**: Delayed activation through left ventricle
- **Septal Depolarization**: Right bundle branch predominates initially
- **Lateral Wall Activation**: Prolonged total ventricular activation
- **Hemodynamic Impact**: Synchronous ventricular contraction impaired

#### Clinical Associations
- **Hypertension Complications**: Progressed cardiomyopathy manifestation
- **Ischemic Heart Disease**: Left ventricular systolic dysfunction
- **Cardiac Amyloidosis**: Conduction system infiltration
- **Prognostic Value**: Increased mortality when present

### Intraventricular Conduction Delay (IVCD)
- **QRS Prolongation**: >0.12 seconds without specific bundle block pattern
- **Multiple Etiologies**: Non-specific cardiac conduction slowing
- **Prognostic Weight**: Associated with adverse cardiac outcomes
- **Further Evaluation**: Encouraged echocardiography recommended

## Timing and Pacemaker Therapy

### Permanent Pacemaker Indications

#### Class I Indications
- **Third-Degree AV Block**: Associated with neurocardiogenic symptoms
- **Second-Degree AV Block**: Type II or complete block
- **Bifascicular Block**: Intermittent complete heart block episodes
- **Irreversible Bradyarrhythmias**: Post-cardiac surgery complications

#### Class IIa Indications
- **Sick Sinus Syndrome**: Hemodynamic instability episodes
- **Hypersensitive Carotid Sinus**: Recurrent syncope manifestations
- **Chronic Bifascicular Block**: Sustained wide QRS complexes
- **Post-Cardiac Surgery**: Conduction abnormalities occurrence

#### Class IIb Indications
- **First-Degree AV Block**: Symptomatic PR interval prolongation
- **Vasovagal Syncope**: Recurrent syncope with demonstrable pauses
- **Outflow Tract Obstruction**: Pacing response beneficial intervention
- **Long QT Syndrome**: Prevention of torsades episodes

### Pacemaker ECG Patterns

#### Single-Chamber Pacing

##### AAI (Atrial Pacing)
- **P Wave Morphology**: Sensitive depending on lead placement
- **Intrinsic Rhythm**: Reduced during increased activity
- **AV Synchrony**: Maintained physiological conduction
- **Anti-Arrhythmic Effect**: Prevents atrial arrhythmia episodes

##### VVI (Ventricular Pacing)
- **QRS Morphology**: LBBB configuration patterns
- **Spike Preceding QRS**: Multisite ventricular activation
- **AV Dissociation**: P waves independent from paced complexes
- **Rate Adaptive Pacing**: Increases with body movement

#### Dual-Chamber Pacing (DDD)
- **AV Synchrony**: Optimized cardiac hemodynamics
- **Rate Responsive Features**: Physiological rate adaptation
- **Adaptive AV Delay**: Variable基づdelay with hardware adjustments
- **Progressive Disease**: Maintains ventricular synchrony

## Clinical Management

### Symptom Assessment
- **Syncope Evaluation**: Presyncope vs complete loss of consciousness
- **Exercise Tolerance**: Assess physical limitations due to bradycardia
- **Medication Review**: Identify agents causing conduction abnormalities
- **Sleep Quality**: Nocturnal bradycardia detection possibilities

### Diagnostic Approach
- **Holter Monitoring**: 24-48 hour ECG rhythm capture
- **Event Recorder**: Symptom correlation with rhythm changes
- **Electrophysiology Study**: Detailed conduction system mapping
- **Tilt Table Testing**: Neurocardiogenic syncope diagnosis

### Therapeutic Considerations
- **Drug Therapy**: Temporizing while permanent solution evaluated
- **Temporary Pacing**: Bridge to permanent device implantation
- **Catheter Ablation**: Arrhythmia-mediated conduction blocks treatment
- **Revascularization**: Ischemia-related conduction abnormalities management

## Key Takeaways

1. Conduction blocks occur at different levels with specific ECG characteristics and clinical significance.
2. AV blocks progress from first-degree to complete block with varying symptomatic manifestations.
3. Fascicular blocks indicate localized conduction system disease often requiring further evaluation.
4. Bundle branch blocks reflect interventricular conduction abnormalities with associated cardiac pathology.
5. Pacemaker therapy is indicated for symptomatic conduction blocks or high-risk asymptomatic patients.
6. ECG patterns of paced rhythms differ based on chamber and timing of stimulation.
7. Symptomatic bradycardia underlies most pacemaker decisions with irreversible conduction abnormalities.

**ECG Interpretation:** Second-degree AV block, type I with measured intervals consistent with nodal disease.

**Treatment Rationale:** Evidence of documented complete heart block with Adams-Stokes syndrome presentation leading to permanent pacing therapy.

**Risk Assessment:** High likelihood of progression to complete heart block with observed ECG conduction abnormalities.
