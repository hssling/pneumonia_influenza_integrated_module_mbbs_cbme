# Chapter 9: Ventricular Arrhythmias

## Learning Objectives
After completing this chapter, the reader will be able to:
1. Differentiate ventricular arrhythmias from supraventricular rhythms
2. Recognize ECG patterns of ventricular tachycardia, premature ventricular complexes, and fibrillation
3. Understand the clinical implications of ventricular arrhythmias
4. Apply appropriate management strategies for life-threatening ventricular rhythms
5. Distinguish between monomorphic and polymorphic ventricular tachycardias

## Introduction

Ventricular arrhythmias originate from the ventricles and represent some of the most serious rhythm disturbances encountered in clinical practice. Unlike supraventricular arrhythmias, ventricular rhythm disturbances are more likely to compromise cardiac output and require immediate intervention. Understanding their ECG characteristics is crucial for emergency diagnosis and treatment, directly impacting patient survival and outcomes.

## Mechanisms of Ventricular Arrhythmias

### Electrical Basis
- **Abnormal Automaticity**: Enhanced pacemaker activity in ventricular myocardium
- **Re-entry**: Spiral wave propagation through scar tissue or ischemic regions
- **Triggered Activity**: Delayed afterdepolarizations leading to triggered beats
- **Anatomic Substrate**: Scar tissue from prior infarction creates re-entry circuits

### Structural Considerations
- **Myocardial Scar**: Post-infarct fibrosis creates conduction barriers
- **Ischemic Tissue**: Acute ischemia induces automaticity and re-entry
- **Hypertrophied Myocardium**: Accentuated conduction heterogeneity
- **Electrolyte Imbalance**: Potassium, magnesium, pH disturbances

## Premature Ventricular Complexes (PVCs)

### ECG Characteristics

#### Morphology Features
- **Broad QRS Complex**: ≥0.12 seconds (unless aberrancy in SVT)
- **Unaltered P Waves**: Continues normal atrial rhythm when present
- **Full Compensatory Pause**: Due to AV block and junctional escape inhibition
- **Morphological Variations**: Left bundle branch block (LBBB) or right bundle branch block (RBBB) patterns

#### Lead-Specific Analysis
- **V1 Analysis**: Crucial for assessing inferior cardiac chambers
- **Bundle Branch Pattern**: Similar to BBB during SVT
- **Amplitude Criteria**: More prominent than native complexes
- **T Wave Orientation**: Discordant or upright in inferior leads

### Frequency Classification
- **Rare PVCs**: <30/hour, usually benign
- **Frequent PVCs**: >1000/day, may indicate underlying pathology
- **PVC Couplets**: Two consecutive PVCs, more concerning
- **Salvos**: Three or more PVCs in sequence

### Clinical Significance

#### Benign Variants
- **Post-PVC Compensatory Pause**: AV node designed pause
- **PVC with Aberrancy**: Reentrant SVT may mimic PVC
- **Athlete's Heart**: 1-2 PVCs per hour common
- **Psychological Stress**: Anxiety-related PVCs

#### Pathological Indicators
- **Multifocal PVCs**: Various morphologies suggest medical pathology
- **R-on-T Phenomenon**: PVC landing on T-wave predisposes to VT/VF
- **Post-MI PVCs**: Marker for increased arrhythmic risk
- **Concomitant Heart Disease**: Symptomatic PVCs need investigation

## Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

### Classification Systems

#### Duration-Based Classification
- **Non-sustained VT**: 3-30 consecutive PVCs, breaks before 30 seconds
- **Sustained VT**: ≥30 consecutive PVCs without cessation
- **Hematodynamically Stable**: Patient maintains consciousness and circulation
- **Unstable VT (Pulseless)**: Immediate cardiopulmonary resuscitation required

#### Morphological Classification
- **Monomorphic VT**: Consistent QRS morphology throughout tachycardia
- **Polymorphic VT**: Continuous change in QRS morphology
- **Bidirectional VT**: Alternating right and left axis deviation
- **Bundle Branch Reentry VT**: Utilizes left bundle branch in cardiomyopathy

### ECG Recognition Principles

#### Monomorphic VT Criteria
- **Vent Wide Complex Tachycardia**: Rate 100-250 bpm, regular
- **AV Dissociation**: Ventricular rhythm independent of atrial rhythm
- **Capture and Fusion Beats**: Fusion of conducted sinus beat with VT complex
- **RS Complex** >100 ms in V1-V2 (except VR morphology)
- **QRS Concordance**: All precordial leads point same direction

#### Additional Diagnostic Features
- **RS Interval >100 ms**: Measured from R to S wave peak in precordial leads
- **QR Complex in Multiple Leads**: Arabesques or bizarre complexes
- **RBBB Pattern Superiority**: R>S in all precordial leads (V7-V9 if available)
- **Absence of RS Complexes**: Suggests VT over aberrancy

### Hemodynamic Implications

#### Tachycardiomyopathy
- **Rate-Related Cardiomyopathy**: Sustained tachycardia')). induces LV dysfunction
- **Heart Failure**: Overall decrease in cardiac output with symptoms
- **Recovery of Function**: Contractility improvement after rate control
- **Beta-blocker Therapy**: Primary treatment for tachycardia-induced dysfunction

#### Acute Circulatory Compromise
- **Pulse Rate Discrepancy**: Palpable rate slower than ECG-documented rate
- **Hypotension Manifestation**: Mean arterial pressure <65 mmHg
- **Cardiogenic Shock**: Cardiac index <2.2 L/min/m²
- **Syncope Episodes**: Pre-syncopal symptoms precede loss of consciousness

## Idiopathic Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)

### Pathophysiology
- **Multiple Wavelet Hypothesisa konfer: Circuitous conduction through myocardium
- **Re-entry Circuits**: Fragments into multiple small wavelets
- **Electrical Silence**: No coordinated contraction occurs
- **Immediate CPR Indication**: Resuscitation must begin immediately

### ECG Characteristics

#### Classic Pattern
- **Irregular Baseline**: Completely disorganized electrical activity
- **Rate Estimation**: 350-500 ventricular depolarizations per minute
- **Amplitude Variability**: Coarse VF vs fine VF morphology
- **Termination Criteria**: No coherent rhythm helpful for rhythm monitoring

#### Phases of VF Evolution
- **Coarse VF**: Initial phase with visible coarse defibrillation waves
- **Fine VF**: Degradation to smaller amplitude waves over time
- **Pulseless Electrical Activity**: Organized rhythm without pulsatility
- **Asystole**: Complete spectrum from VF progression to cardiac arrest

### Clinical Management

#### Immediate Interventions
- **Chest Compressions**: Preferential to defibrillation in unwitnessed arrests
- **Defibrillation Delivery**: Biphasic shocks starting at 120-200 joules
- **Vascular Access**: Epinephrine 1mg IV every 3-5 minutes
- **Antiarrythmia Drugs**: Amiodarone or lidocaine after failed shocks

#### Advanced Life Support
- **Cardiac Arrest Algorithm**: Following ACLS guidelines
- **Reversible Causes**: H's and T's (Hypoxia, Hypovolemia, Hypokalemia, etc.)
- **Targeted Temperature Management**: Post-resuscitative care protocol
- **Neurological Prognostication**: Neurologic recovery assessment protocols

## Torsades de Pointes

### Pathophysiological Mechanism
- **Fran Movement Typical**: QT syndrome abnormalities create re-entry
- **Early Afterdepolarization**: Triggered by potassium channel abnormalities
- **QT Interval Prolongation**: Risk factor for polymorphic VT development
- **QT Prolongation Causes**: Pharmacological, congenital, or metabolic

### ECG Characteristics

#### Polymorphic VT Pattern
- **Changing QRS Morphology**: Continuous alteration every 5-10 beats
- **Torsional Appearance**: Spindling deviation around baseline
- **Rate Spectrum**: Typically 200-250 bpm with characteristic variations
- **Self-Terminating Episodes**: Episodes resolve spontaneously if not sustained

#### Torsades de Pointes Criteria
- **QT Prolongation**: QTc >500 ms preceding the arrhythmia
- **Absent RS Complexes**: Characteristic during polymorphic VT
- **Central retorial Axis**: Rotating cardiac rhythm around isoelectric line
- **____________Tachycardia Initiation**: Often following a short-long-short cycle

### Treatment Strategies

#### Acute Management
- **Magnesium Sulfate**: 2 grams IV bolus over 1-2 minutes
- **Isoproterenol Infusions**: When both bradycardia and torsades present
- **Temporary Pacing**: Overdrive pacing to suppress early afterdepolarizations
- **DC Shock**: Immediate defibrillation if hemodynamically unstable

#### Preventive Measures
- **QT-Prolonging Drugs**: Avoidance in high risk patients
- **Potassium Correction**: Maintaining serum potassium levels
- **Calcium Correction**: Managing serum calcium concentrations
- **Genetic Testing**: If inherited channelopathy suspected

## Bundle Branch Reentry Tachycardia

### Mechanism of Arrhythmia
- **Circus Movement Animation**: Depolarization through left bundle, return via right bundle
- **Nonischemic Cardiomyopathy**: Occurs in dilated cardiomyopathy patients
- **Narrow Excursion**: Appears narrower than typical ventricular tachycardia
- **Roth Progression**: Incremental analysis for accurate diagnosis

### Diagnostic Criteria
- **Hemodynamic Stability**: Usually stable at rates 180-200 bpm
- **Absence of Structural Heart Disease**: Completely unchanged cardiac anatomy
- **Catheter Ablation Cured**: Definitive treatment targets tract removal
- **No Fusion Beats**: Captured complexes absent during tachycardia

### Clinical Management
- **Catheter Ablation**: Target left-sided His bundle for cure
- **Antiarrhythmic Drugs**: For symptomatic or contraindications
- **Defibrillator Consideration**: ICD placement in high-risk patients
- **Beta-blocker Therapy**: Prophylactic approach for prevention

## Clinical Decision Making

### Hemodynamic Stability Assessment
- **BP and Mental Status**: Preservation indicates stable tachycardia
- **Serum Lactate Measurement**: Rising lactate suggests hypoperfusion
- **Urine Output Monitoring**: Oliguria indicates renal dysfunction
- **Lactate Clearance**: Normal clearance maintains perfusion status

### Arrhythmia Differentiation Algorithm
1. **Wide Complex Tachycardia**: Regular or irregular appearance
2. **Monomorphic Pattern**: VT highly likely if regular
3. **Polymorphic Features**: Torsades or VF consideration
4. **AV Dissociation**: Strong predictor of ventricular origin
5. **Morphological Criteria**: RS complex analysis and concordance

### Management Prioritization
- **ABC Stabilization**: Airway, breathing, and circulation first
- **Pharmacologic Strategies**: Amiodaron`,
- **Electrical Cardioversion**: Synchronized for unstable VT
- **Defibrillation**: Unsynchronized for VF or pulseless VT

### Risk Stratification
- **Primary Prevention**: LVEF assessment for ICD implantation
- **Secondary Prevention**: History of successful cardiac arrest intervention
- **Longevity Determination**: Age and comorbidities influence decisions
- **Quality of Life Assessment**: Symptom burden instrumental in management planning

## Key Takeaways

1. Ventricular arrhythmias arise from ventricles and represent life-threatening dysrhythmias requiring immediate attention.
2. PVC morphology, frequency, and associated symptoms help determine benign vs pathologic classification.
3. VT presents with wide complex tachycardia and may be monomorphic or polymorphic in nature.
4. VF requires immediate CPR and defibrillation with rapid sequence interventions.
5. Torsades de pointes requires magnesium sulfate and addressing underlying QT prolongation.
6. Hemodynamic stability assessment guides treatment urgency and modality selection.
7. Proper detection requires ECG analysis of morphology, rate, and AV conduction relationship.

## Clinical Cases

### Case 1: PVC with Variable Coupling
A 65-year-old male presented after presyncope during exercise. ECG demonstrated multiple PVCs with varying coupling intervals and morphologies.

**Interpretation:** Multifocal PVCs requiring investigation for cardiologist evaluation.
**Action:** Echocardiogram and possible rhythm monitoring with event recorder.

### Case 2: Monomorphic VT
A 72-year-old female underwent cardiac catheterization with VT detected early in recovery phase. Patient remained hemodynamically stable.

**Interpretation:** Sustained monomorphic VT with bundle branch morphology.
**Action:** Electrophysiologic study followed by catheter ablation procedure.

### Case 3: VF in Acute MI
A 58-year-old male arrested during admission chest pain episode. ECG displayed VF immediately following ST-elevation MI diagnosis.

**Interpretation:** Sudden VF resulting from provoked acute myocardial ischemia.
**Action:** Immediate defibrillation, cooling protocols, and consideration for automatic implantable cardioverter defibrillator.
