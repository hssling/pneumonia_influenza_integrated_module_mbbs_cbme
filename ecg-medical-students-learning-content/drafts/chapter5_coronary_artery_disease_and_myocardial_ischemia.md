# Chapter 5: Coronary Artery Disease and Myocardial Ischemia

## Learning Objectives
After completing this chapter, the reader will be able to:
1. Understand the pathophysiology of myocardial ischemia
2. Recognize ECG patterns of stable and unstable angina
3. Differentiate between subendocardial and transmural ischemia
4. Identify regional wall ischemia based on ECG lead locations
5. Apply appropriate clinical decision-making for ischemic patterns

## Introduction

Coronary artery disease (CAD) is the leading cause of cardiovascular morbidity and mortality worldwide. The ECG remains the cornerstone of initial diagnosis in acute coronary syndromes (ACS), providing critical information about coronary artery involvement and myocardial viability. Understanding ECG ischemic patterns enables timely intervention and improves patient outcomes.

## Pathophysiology of Myocardial Ischemia

### Cellular Basis of Ischemia
- **Oxygen Supply**: Coronary arteries provide oxygen and nutrients to myocardium
- **Oxygen Demand**: Determined by heart rate, contractility, preload, and afterload
- **Ischemic Cascade**: Progresses from myocardial supply-demand mismatch to infarction

### Types of Ischemic Injury

#### Subendocardial Ischemia
- **Location**: Inner one-third of left ventricular wall
- **Cause**: Reduced coronary perfusion pressure
- **Risk factors**: Tachycardia, hypotension, aortic stenosis
- **ECG pattern**: ST depression in multiple leads

#### Subepicardial Ischemia
- **Location**: Outer one-third of myocardial wall
- **Cause**: Partial coronary artery occlusion
- **Risk factors**: Atherosclerotic plaques, vasospasm
- **ECG pattern**: ST elevation or reciprocal changes

#### Transmural Ischemia
- **Location**: Entire myocardial wall thickness
- **Cause**: Complete coronary artery occlusion
- **Risk factors**: Thrombus formation, plaque rupture
- **ECG pattern**: ST elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI)

### Ischemic Electrocardiographic Manifestations

#### Primary Changes
- **ST Segment Elevation**: Suggests transmural injury
- **ST Segment Depression**: Indicates subendocardial ischemia
- **T Wave Inversion**: Represents ischemia or strain
- **Q Wave Formation**: Indicates myocardial necrosis/fibrosis

#### Secondary Repolarization Changes
- **T Wave Asymmetrically Inverted**: Deep, symmetric in anterior leads
- **U Wave Changes**: Inverted U waves in ischemia
- **QT Interval Prolongation**: Due to altered repolarization

#### Regional ECG Patterns

##### Anterior Wall Ischemia (V1-V4)
- **Leads affected**: V1-V4 primarily
- **Coronary artery**: Left anterior descending (LAD)
- **ST elevation criteria**: ≥2 mm in V2-V3, ≥1 mm in other anterior leads
- **Prognostic significance**: Anteroseptal MI has high mortality risk

##### Inferior Wall Ischemia (II, III, aVF)
- **Leads affected**: II, III, aVF
- **Coronary artery**: Right coronary artery (90%), left circumflex (10%)
- **ST elevation criteria**: ≥1 mm in inferior leads
- **Associated findings**: Right ventricular involvement (V4R elevation)

##### Lateral Wall Ischemia (I, aVL, V5-V6)
- **Leads affected**: I, aVL, V5, V6
- **Coronary artery**: Left circumflex artery (LCx) or diagonal branches
- **ST elevation criteria**: ≥1 mm in lateral leads
- **Clinical correlation**: Often presents as non-dominant vessel occlusion

##### Posterior Wall Ischemia (V7-V9, indirect)
- **Indirect evidence**: Prominent R waves in V1, ST depression V1-V3
- **Direct leads**: V7 (posterior axillary), V8 (scapula), V9 (paraspinal)
- **Coronary territory**: Posterior descending artery from RCA or LCx
- **Serial ECGs essential**: Posterior leads often required for confirmation

### ECG Classification of Acute Coronary Syndromes

#### ST Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI)
- **Criteria**: New ST elevation ≥1 mm in limb leads, ≥2 mm in precordial leads
- **Duration**: Persistent >20 minutes
- **Need for urgent intervention**: Primary PCI or thrombolysis
- **Prognosis**: Highest immediate mortality among ACS types

#### Non-ST Elevation ACS (NSTE-ACS)
- **Unstable Angina**: Transient ischemic symptoms, normal cardiac enzymes
- **NSTEMI**: Ischemia with myocardial necrosis (elevated troponin)
- **ECG findings**: ST depression, T-wave inversion, or normal ECG
- **Management**: Medical therapy first, early invasive strategy if high risk

#### Stable Angina Pectoris
- **Symptoms**: Predictable chest pain with exertion
- **ECG at rest**: Usually normal
- **Stress ECG**: Essential for diagnosis (ST depression ≥1 mm)
- **Management**: Medical therapy, risk factor modification

### Clinical Recognition Patterns

#### Prinzmetal's Variant Angina
- **Mechanism**: Coronary artery vasospasm (episodic)
- **ECG findings**: Transient ST elevation, often during rest
- **Associated symptoms**: Nocturnal chest pain, not exertion-related
- **Diagnosis**: Hyperventilation or Ergonovine provocation testing

#### Cocaine-Induced Ischemia
- **Mechanism**: Coronary vasospasm and accelerated atherosclerosis
- **ECG findings**: ST elevation or depression, arrhythmias
- **Presentation**: Acute chest pain in young patients
- **Management**: Benzodiazepines, calcium channel blockers (avoid beta-blockers)

#### Stress Cardiomyopathy (Takotsubo)
- **Presentation**: Chest pain mimicking infarction
- **ECG findings**: ST elevation, especially anterior leads
- **Diagnostic clue**: Echocardiogram shows apical ballooning
- **Prognosis**: Usually excellent recovery within weeks

### ECG Evolution in Myocardial Infarction

#### Hyperacute Phase (First Hours)
- **Tall, peaked T waves**: Most sensitive early sign
- **ST elevation**: Developing over 15-30 minutes
- **Tall R waves**: With loss of S waves in anterior leads
- **Reciprocal changes**: ST depression in opposite leads

#### Fully Evolved Phase (Hours to Days)
- **Marked ST elevation**: Convex upward in affected leads
- **T wave inversion**: Beginning of normalization
- **Q wave formation**: Developing within 6-8 hours
- **Maximum changes**: Usually 24-48 hours post-onset

#### Resolution Phase (Days to Weeks)
- **ST segment**: Begins gradual return to baseline
- **T waves**: Persistently inverted, later normalize
- **Q waves**: May persist as permanent scar
- **Complete resolution**: Usually within 2-3 weeks

### Clinical Decision-Making

#### Immediate Management Priorities
1. **ABC stabilization**: Airway, breathing, circulation
2. **Monitor ECG continuously**: Detect arrhythmias, evolution
3. **Pain management**: Morphine, nitroglycerin, oxygen
4. **Anticoagulation**: Aspirin 325 mg, heparin 4000-5000 units IV
5. **Timing is crucial**: "Door-to-balloon" time <90 minutes

#### Risk Stratification
- **TIMI Risk Score**: Predicting 14-day mortality in ACS
- **GRACE Score**: 6-month mortality prediction
- **High-risk features**: Heart failure, multiple leads involved, anterior MI
- **Guided by ECG**: Extent of ST elevation predicts mortality

#### Reperfusion Therapy Selection
- **Primary PCI**: Preferred when available (<90 minutes transfer time)
- **Thrombolysis**: When PCI not available within guidelines timeframe
- **Fibrinolysis contraindications**: Active bleeding, recent surgery, stroke history
- **Door-to-needle time**: Aim for <30 minutes

### Complications of Myocardial Infarction

#### Electrical Complications
- **Arrhythmias**: Life-threatening requiring ACLS protocols
  - Ventricular fibrillation (VF): Most common cause of sudden death
  - Ventricular tachycardia (VT): Sustained VT needs cardioversion
  - Atrial fibrillation (AF): New-onset may indicate heart failure
  - Heart block: Advanced AV block due to septal involvement

#### Mechanical Complications
- **Ventricular septal defect**: New pansystolic murmur, severe dyspnea
- **Papillary muscle rupture**: Acute pulmonary edema, cardiogenic shock
- **Myocardial free wall rupture**: Pericardial tamponade, sudden death
- **Left ventricular aneurysm**: Sustained ST elevation, paradoxical pulsation

#### Failing Heart
- **Heart failure syndromes**: Cardiogenic pulmonary edema
- **Cardiogenic shock**: Hypotension despite fluid challenge
- **Right ventricular infarction**: Jugular venous distension, clear lungs
- **Aggressive diuresis**: While maintaining perfusion pressure

### Monitoring and Follow-up

#### Initial 24-48 Hours
- **Continuous ECG monitoring**: Detect reperfusion arrhythmias
- **Troponin monitoring**: Serial cardiac enzymes
- **Blood pressure**: Hypotension signals shock or heart failure
- **Renal function**: Contrast-induced nephropathy risk

#### Discharge Planning
- **LV function assessment**: Echocardiogram identifies reduced ejection fraction
- **Stress testing**: Before hospital discharge or post-recovery (2-4 weeks)
- **Medication reconciliation**: Antiplatelet, beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, statins
- **Cardiac rehabilitation**: Lifestyle modification, exercise prescription

### Key Takeaways

1. ECG patterns reflect underlying coronary artery anatomy and territory
2. ST elevation indicates immediate reperfusion therapy candidacy
3. Regional ECG changes help localize coronary artery involvement
4. Clinical symptoms combined with ECG guide management decisions
5. Serial ECGs demonstrate evolution of ischemic injury over time
6. Complications are ECG-visible and require prompt intervention

## Clinical Cases

### Case 1: Anterior STEMI
A 58-year-old male presents with crushing chest pain, sweating, nausea.

**ECG Findings:**
- ST elevation in V2-V4 (3 mm), I, aVL (2 mm)
- Reciprocal ST depression II, III, aVF
- T wave inversion forming (early evolution)

**Interpretation:** Anterior STEMI involving LAD, high risk
**Action:** Immediate cardiac cath lab activation, morphine, oxygen, aspirin, heparin

### Case 2: Inferior STEMI with RV Involvement
A 62-year-old female with chest pain, hypotension (90/60 mmHg).

**ECG Findings:**
- ST elevation II, III, aVF (2-3 mm)
- Right-sided leads show V4R-V6R elevation (1-2 mm)
- T wave inversion in inferior leads

**Interpretation:** RCA occlusion with RV extension
**Action:** Intravenous fluids, careful with nitroglycerin (may worsen hypotension), urgent PCI
