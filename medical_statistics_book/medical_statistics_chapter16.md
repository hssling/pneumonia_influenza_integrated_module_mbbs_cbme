# Chapter 16: Critical Appraisal of Medical Literature

## 16.1 Introduction to Critical Appraisal

**Critical appraisal** is the systematic evaluation of research evidence to assess its validity, reliability, and applicability to clinical practice.

### Why Critical Appraisal Matters:
- **Evidence-based medicine**: Make decisions based on best available evidence
- **Avoid misinformation**: Identify flawed studies and biased results
- **Clinical decision making**: Apply research findings appropriately
- **Research improvement**: Learn from well-designed studies
- **Resource allocation**: Prioritize effective interventions

### Key Questions for Critical Appraisal:
1. **Validity**: Is the study design appropriate and well-executed?
2. **Reliability**: Are the results reproducible?
3. **Applicability**: Can results be applied to your patients?
4. **Clinical importance**: Are the results meaningful for patient care?

**Example 16.1**: Reading a clinical trial
- Study claims new drug reduces heart attacks by 25%
- Critical appraisal reveals: Small sample, short follow-up, selective outcome reporting
- Conclusion: Evidence not strong enough to change practice

## 16.2 Appraising Different Study Designs

### 16.2.1 Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)

**Key appraisal questions:**
- **Randomization**: Was allocation truly random?
- **Blinding**: Were patients, clinicians, and assessors blinded?
- **Intention-to-treat**: Were all randomized patients analyzed?
- **Baseline balance**: Were groups comparable at baseline?
- **Follow-up**: Was follow-up complete?

**Example 16.2**: RCT appraisal checklist
- Yes/No/Can't tell for each quality criterion
- Overall quality: Good if most criteria met
- Poor quality RCTs may be worse than good observational studies

### 16.2.2 Observational Studies

**Cohort studies:**
- **Selection bias**: How were exposed/unexposed groups selected?
- **Confounding**: Were important confounders measured and controlled?
- **Follow-up**: What proportion lost to follow-up?
- **Outcome assessment**: Was outcome assessment blinded?

**Case-control studies:**
- **Case selection**: How were cases identified?
- **Control selection**: Were controls representative?
- **Recall bias**: Differential recall between cases and controls?
- **Matching**: Appropriate matching criteria?

### 16.2.3 Diagnostic Test Studies

**STARD criteria:**
- **Patient spectrum**: Representative of clinical practice?
- **Reference standard**: Appropriate gold standard?
- **Blinding**: Was test interpretation blinded to reference standard?
- **Reproducibility**: Inter-observer and intra-observer variability?

## 16.3 Statistical Appraisal

### 16.3.1 Sample Size and Power

**Questions to ask:**
- Was sample size calculated before study started?
- Is study adequately powered to detect clinically important effects?
- Are negative results due to lack of power?

**Example 16.3**: Underpowered study
- Study finds no treatment benefit (p=0.08)
- Post-hoc power = 45%
- Conclusion: Study inconclusive, not evidence of no effect

### 16.3.2 p-Values and Confidence Intervals

**p-value interpretation:**
- p < 0.05 does not mean "clinically important"
- p > 0.05 does not mean "no effect"
- p-value depends on sample size and effect size

**Confidence intervals:**
- More informative than p-values
- Show precision of estimate
- Allow assessment of clinical significance

**Example 16.4**: Treatment effect
- Relative risk = 0.85, 95% CI: 0.70-1.03
- Includes 1.0, not statistically significant
- But upper bound 1.03 suggests minimal 3% risk increase
- Lower bound 0.70 suggests 30% risk reduction possible

### 16.3.3 Multiple Testing

**Problem**: Multiple statistical tests increase false positive rate

**Solutions:**
- Pre-specify primary outcome
- Adjust p-values (Bonferroni, FDR)
- Focus on confidence intervals

## 16.4 Assessing Bias and Confounding

### 16.4.1 Types of Bias

**Selection bias**: Non-random selection of participants
**Information bias**: Systematic errors in measurement
**Confounding**: Mixing of effects between exposure, outcome, and third factor

**Example 16.5**: Confounding in observational study
- Coffee consumption associated with heart disease
- Confounding by smoking: Coffee drinkers more likely to smoke
- After adjustment: No association between coffee and heart disease

### 16.4.2 Assessing Confounding

**Questions:**
- Are important confounders identified?
- Were confounders measured accurately?
- Was confounding controlled in analysis?
- Are results sensitive to confounding assumptions?

## 16.5 Interpreting Results

### 16.5.1 Clinical Significance vs Statistical Significance

**Statistical significance**: Unlikely due to chance
**Clinical significance**: Meaningful impact on patient outcomes

**Example 16.6**: Blood pressure medication
- Reduces BP by 2 mmHg (p<0.001)
- Statistically significant but clinically questionable
- May not justify medication side effects

### 16.5.2 Absolute vs Relative Effects

**Relative risk**: Ratio of risks
**Absolute risk**: Difference in risks
**Number needed to treat (NNT)**: 1/absolute risk reduction

**Example 16.7**: Treatment benefit
- Control group: 10% risk of complication
- Treatment group: 8% risk of complication
- Relative risk = 0.8 (20% reduction)
- Absolute risk reduction = 2%
- NNT = 1/0.02 = 50 (treat 50 to prevent 1 complication)

### 16.5.3 Generalizability

**External validity**: Can results be applied to other populations?

**Questions:**
- Similar patients to your practice?
- Similar setting (hospital vs community)?
- Similar intervention (dose, duration)?
- Similar outcomes measured?

## 16.7 Evidence Grading Systems

### 16.7.1 GRADE System

**Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation**

**Quality levels:**
- **High**: Further research unlikely to change confidence
- **Moderate**: Further research likely to impact confidence
- **Low**: Further research very likely to impact confidence
- **Very Low**: Very uncertain about estimate

**Downgrading factors:**
- Risk of bias
- Inconsistency
- Indirectness
- Imprecision
- Publication bias

**Upgrading factors:**
- Large effect size
- Dose-response gradient
- Confounding would reduce effect

### 16.7.2 Oxford Centre for Evidence-Based Medicine Levels

**Levels of evidence:**
- Level 1: Systematic reviews of RCTs
- Level 2: Individual RCTs
- Level 3: Cohort studies
- Level 4: Case-control studies
- Level 5: Expert opinion

## 16.8 Common Statistical Pitfalls

### 16.8.1 Misinterpretation of p-Values

**Common mistake**: p < 0.05 means "proven" or "important"

**Reality**: p-value is probability of data given null hypothesis true

**Better approach**: Focus on effect size and confidence intervals

### 16.8.2 Over-reliance on Statistical Significance

**Problem**: Dichotomous thinking (significant vs non-significant)

**Solution**: Consider clinical importance, precision, and study quality

### 16.8.3 Subgroup Analysis Over-Interpretation

**Problem**: Emphasizing subgroup findings without interaction tests

**Solution**: Require significant interaction test before interpreting subgroups

## 16.9 Applying Evidence to Patient Care

### 16.9.1 Patient Preferences and Values

**Evidence-based medicine equation:**
Clinical decision = Best evidence + Clinical expertise + Patient values

**Example 16.8**: Treatment choice
- Strong evidence for Drug A (better efficacy)
- Patient prefers Drug B (fewer side effects)
- Shared decision making incorporates both

### 16.9.2 Local Context

**Consider:**
- Available resources
- Local expertise
- Healthcare system constraints
- Cultural factors

## 16.11 Medical Applications

### 16.11.1 Therapy Articles

**Key questions:**
- Were patients randomized?
- Were groups similar at baseline?
- Was follow-up complete?
- Were all patients analyzed in assigned groups?
- Were outcomes blinded?

**Example 16.9**: Antidepressant trial appraisal
- Randomized: Yes
- Blinding: Double-blind
- ITT analysis: Yes
- Follow-up: 85% complete
- Conclusion: High quality, results reliable

### 16.11.2 Diagnostic Test Articles

**Key questions:**
- Did test clinicians use the test in practice?
- Was diagnostic uncertainty present?
- Was gold standard applied to all patients?
- Were test results interpreted blindly?

### 16.11.3 Systematic Reviews

**Key questions:**
- Did review address focused question?
- Were inclusion criteria appropriate?
- Was search comprehensive?
- Were studies quality assessed?
- Were results combined appropriately?

## 16.12 Exercises

### Exercise 16.1: RCT Appraisal
Read the following study abstract and appraise it:

"A randomized trial of 200 patients compared Drug A vs placebo for hypertension. Drug A reduced systolic BP by 5 mmHg more than placebo (p=0.03). 15% of Drug A patients experienced side effects vs 5% with placebo (p=0.01)."

1. What are the strengths of this study?
2. What are the limitations?
3. Would you recommend Drug A for your patients?

### Exercise 16.2: Statistical Interpretation
A study reports: "Treatment reduced mortality by 50% (RR=0.5, 95% CI: 0.3-0.8, p=0.004)"

1. What does the confidence interval tell you?
2. Is this result clinically important?
3. What other information would you want?

### Exercise 16.3: Publication Bias Assessment
A meta-analysis of 10 studies shows treatment benefit. The funnel plot is asymmetric with smaller studies showing larger effects.

1. What does this suggest?
2. How would you investigate further?
3. How might this affect interpretation?

### Exercise 16.4: Applying Evidence
A high-quality RCT shows new treatment reduces heart attacks by 25% compared to standard treatment.

Your patient characteristics:
- 65-year-old male
- Multiple risk factors
- Cannot tolerate standard treatment side effects

1. How applicable are the results to your patient?
2. What other factors should you consider?
3. How would you discuss this with the patient?

## 16.13 Exercise Answers

### Answer 16.1: RCT Appraisal
1. **Strengths:**
   - Randomized design
   - Statistically significant result
   - Compared side effects

2. **Limitations:**
   - Small sample size (underpowered?)
   - Short-term outcome only
   - No information about blinding
   - No information about ITT analysis

3. **Recommendation:**
   - Need more information about study quality
   - Consider magnitude of benefit vs side effects
   - Wait for larger confirmatory trials

### Answer 16.2: Statistical Interpretation
1. **Confidence interval**: 70% to 20% risk reduction possible (wide interval suggests uncertainty)

2. **Clinical importance**: Depends on baseline risk and patient preferences

3. **Additional information needed**:
   - Absolute risk reduction
   - Number needed to treat
   - Study duration and follow-up
   - Patient characteristics

### Answer 16.3: Publication Bias Assessment
1. **Suggests publication bias**: Small studies with negative results may not be published

2. **Further investigation**:
   - Search clinical trial registries
   - Contact authors for unpublished data
   - Perform trim and fill analysis

3. **Effect on interpretation**:
   - May overestimate treatment effect
   - Reduces confidence in results
   - May change clinical recommendations

### Answer 16.4: Applying Evidence
1. **Applicability**:
   - Age similar to study population
   - Multiple risk factors match high-risk group
   - But cannot tolerate standard treatment (exclusion criterion?)

2. **Other factors**:
   - Patient's values and preferences
   - Cost of new treatment
   - Availability of new treatment
   - Alternative treatments

3. **Patient discussion**:
   - Present benefits and risks
   - Discuss uncertainty
   - Explore patient preferences
   - Shared decision making
